# Melodic Construction

A melody is a sequence of pitches in time perceived as a single coherent line. "Coherent" is doing a lot of work in that sentence — it's the engineering problem of melodic writing. This file covers the parameters you adjust to make a line cohere, breathe, and stay memorable.

## When to consult this file

- The user asks how to write a melody, fix a melody, or evaluate one
- Range, contour, climax, melodic peaks
- Why a melody "feels right" or "feels wrong"
- The interaction between melody and harmony at the line-by-line level

## The parameters of a melody

A melody is the joint trajectory of these dimensions:

1. **Pitch content** — which scale degrees are used, how chromatic, how often the line touches consonance vs. tension.
2. **Contour** — the shape over time: up, down, undulating, arched, terraced.
3. **Range** — the distance from lowest to highest note. Affects perceived size and which voice/instrument can sing it.
4. **Tessitura** — which part of the range is used most. A melody can have wide range but live mostly in one zone.
5. **Interval profile** — proportion of steps vs. leaps; the size and direction of the leaps; whether leaps are "filled in" by following stepwise motion in the opposite direction.
6. **Rhythm** — durations, density, syncopation, repetition. Rhythm is half of melody. Probably more.
7. **Climax** — where the highest pitch (or longest note, or loudest moment) lands relative to the line's length.
8. **Relationship to harmony** — which notes are chord tones, which are non-chord tones (passing, neighbor, suspension, anticipation, appoggiatura, escape tone).

When a melody isn't working, it's almost always one or two of these dimensions misbehaving.

## Contour shapes (the macro shape)

Most strong melodies fall into one of a few archetypal contour shapes. None is "best" — they suit different feelings.

- **Arch**: rises to a peak somewhere past the middle, then falls. The most common shape in folk and classical. Feels balanced and sentence-like.
- **Inverted arch**: dips to a low point, returns. Less common. Feels reflective or recovering.
- **Ascending**: climbs throughout, ending high. Feels open, aspirational, unresolved (especially if it ends on ^2, ^4, or ^7).
- **Descending**: falls throughout. Feels conclusive, melancholy, or resigned.
- **Undulating / wave**: oscillates across a roughly horizontal axis. Common in chant, rap, and conversational melodies.
- **Terraced / step-up**: each phrase is a register higher than the last, like a staircase. Builds energy across multiple phrases. Common in pop pre-choruses and gospel build-ups.
- **Pendulum**: alternates between two notes or two zones. Hypnotic, often used for tension or trance.

A common stronger structure is to nest one shape inside another — a wave shape on the small scale, riding an arch on the large scale.

## Range and tessitura

- **Speech range** is roughly an octave. Anything within the speech range feels conversational — natural for verses, raps, narrative ballads.
- **Singable range** for an untrained voice: about 1.5 octaves. Skilled singers comfortable with 2 octaves; pros can push to 2.5 or 3.
- **Tessitura matters more than range.** A melody with a 2-octave range that lives in the middle 5th feels modest. A melody with a 5th of range that sits at the top of the singer's range feels intense.
- **Chorus tessitura is usually higher than verse tessitura.** This is a primary mechanism by which choruses "lift" — see `form/narrative-and-transitions.md`.
- **For instrumental melodies, the same idea applies.** Using the comfortable middle of an instrument feels easy; venturing into the extremes makes the line feel strained or virtuosic. See `orchestration/instruments-ranges-character.md`.

### Vocal range zones

Most vocal melodies sit in one of three zones:

| Zone | Position in voice | Effect |
|------|-------------------|--------|
| Low chest | Bottom 4th of singer's range | Intimate, conversational, sometimes "spoken" feel |
| Mid chest / mix | Middle of range | Comfortable, sustainable, default |
| Belt / head voice | Upper 4th to top | Intense, emotional, can't sustain forever |

A song that lives entirely in mid chest feels even-keeled. A song that builds from low chest in the verse to belt in the chorus has the most natural "lift" — and is the structural default of most pop ballads.

## Steps vs. leaps

The basic guideline: stepwise motion is the connective tissue, leaps are the punctuation.

- **Mostly stepwise melodies** (folk, chant, lullabies) feel smooth, vocal, predictable.
- **Leap-heavy melodies** (some jazz heads, fanfares, "Somewhere Over the Rainbow") feel athletic and dramatic. They demand more from the singer/player.
- **A leap larger than a 5th creates expectation.** The classical convention is to fill it in: leap up, then walk back down stepwise (or leap down, walk back up). This isn't a rule but a strong tendency that the ear notices when broken.
- **Multiple consecutive leaps in the same direction** (e.g., outlining a triad: C–E–G) feel arpeggiated and melodically thin. Useful when you want the line to feel like a chord, not a melody.
- **A leap to a non-chord tone is usually disruptive.** Leaps tend to land on chord tones; the surprise of leaping to an extension (a 9th, 11th, 13th) is striking and useful in jazz, neo-soul, and some film writing.

## The climax

Most strong melodies have a single highest moment — the climax. It's usually:

- **Past the midpoint.** Around the 60–75% mark of the line is a common location. (The "golden ratio" gets cited; it's a reasonable rule of thumb.)
- **The highest pitch in the line.** Or the longest. Or the most rhythmically charged.
- **Approached by stepwise ascent or a single dramatic leap.** The approach is what makes the climax feel earned.
- **Followed by descent.** The release. Without descent the climax doesn't feel like a peak — it feels like a plateau.

If a melody feels flat, check whether there's a clear climax. If it feels exhausting, check whether the climax comes too early or repeats too often.

A song's overall climax (the highest note in the entire piece) is usually saved for the final chorus or the bridge's payoff. Spending the highest note early leaves nowhere to go.

## Relationship to harmony — chord tones and non-chord tones

Every note in a melody is either a chord tone (at the moment it sounds, given the underlying harmony) or a non-chord tone. Chord tones are stable; non-chord tones are colored or tense. The richness of a melody comes from the dance between them.

The standard non-chord tones (NCTs):

- **Passing tone (PT)** — connects two chord tones by step. Approached and left by step in the same direction.
- **Neighbor tone (NT)** — moves up (or down) a step from a chord tone and returns to it. Approached and left by step in opposite directions.
- **Suspension** — a chord tone is held over from the previous chord, becomes a dissonance against the new harmony, and resolves down by step. The classic 4-3, 7-6, 9-8 suspensions.
- **Anticipation** — the next chord's note arrives early. Common in pop on the "and" of beat 4 anticipating beat 1.
- **Appoggiatura** — a leap to a non-chord tone, which then resolves by step (usually down). Lyrical and emotive.
- **Escape tone (échappée)** — moves stepwise from a chord tone, then leaps in the opposite direction.

These categories aren't pedantic — they correspond to perceptually different kinds of "tension". A passing tone is barely felt; an appoggiatura is a heart-tug.

For melodies to sound rich without sounding "wrong", a useful pattern: aim notes on strong beats at chord tones, decorate with NCTs on weak beats. This is the default. Inverting it — putting NCTs on strong beats — produces yearning, suspended-feeling melodies common in ballads.

### Strong-beat note choice and emotional weight

The pitch on a strong beat over a given chord carries that beat's emotional weight. Quick reference for what each chord tone sounds like in the melody:

| Chord tone | Stability | Affect when on a strong beat |
|------------|-----------|------------------------------|
| Root | Most stable | Grounded, declarative |
| 3rd | Stable; defines major/minor | Brightness or darkness depending on major/minor |
| 5th | Stable, relatively neutral | Open, "out there" |
| 7th (in 7th chord) | Tense | Yearning, unresolved |
| 9th | Tense, sweet | Lush, modern |
| ♯11 | Tense, ambiguous | Floating, Lydian |
| 13th | Tense, warm | Gospel, soul |
| Altered tensions (♭9, ♯9, ♭13) | Highly tense | Edgy, bluesy, jazzy |

Songwriters often choose melody notes for their *vertical* feeling against the chord, not just their horizontal flow. A chorus melody that lands on the 9th of the I chord (D over Cmaj9) feels modern and lifted; one that lands on the root feels grounded.

## Rhythm in melody

Pitch is half of melody. Rhythm is the other half — and the half that often does more work.

### Rhythmic identity

A motif's rhythm is usually more recognizable than its pitch contour. The opening four notes of Beethoven's 5th — three short notes and a long one — would be recognized as "the theme" even with completely different pitches. Rhythmic identity is how the listener locks onto a motif.

When writing a memorable melody, give it a distinctive rhythm — not necessarily complex, but characterful. Repetitive rhythms with subtle variations are often more memorable than rhythms that change every bar.

### Syllabic density

In vocal melodies, the number of syllables per beat affects the song's emotional pacing. Three modes:

- **Sparse (1–2 syllables per bar)**: open, ballad-like, gives space for sustained notes.
- **Moderate (3–6 syllables per bar)**: the default for pop verses.
- **Dense (7+ syllables per bar)**: rap-adjacent, conversational, stuffed with information.

Mixing densities across sections is a primary contrast tool. Verses often dense; choruses often sparse (or vice versa).

### Anacrusis and phrase rhythm

Many memorable melodies start before the downbeat — the anacrusis (pickup). It creates forward motion into the strong beat. "Happy Birthday" starts on the upbeat. Most pop choruses start on or just before beat 1 of the bar after the chord change.

Where phrases land also matters. A phrase that ends on a downbeat feels conclusive; one that ends on the off-beat feels suspended, encouraging continuation.

## Repetition, variation, and surprise

Memorable melodies repeat. Pure variation is forgettable; pure repetition is monotonous. The art is the ratio.

- **Exact repetition** — fine for short motifs (1–2 bars) but tedious over long stretches.
- **Sequenced repetition** — same shape, different starting pitch. Up a step, down a step, up a third. The default development tool.
- **Rhythmic repetition with melodic variation** — same rhythm, different pitches. Holds together rhythmically while exploring melodically.
- **Melodic repetition with rhythmic variation** — same pitches, different rhythm. Less common; works for hooks where the pitches are the identity.
- **Fragment-and-extend** — repeat a short motif; the third or fourth time, extend it into something new. The "rule of three" then break.

See `motivic-development.md` for systematic development of motifs across a piece.

## Genre-specific notes (high level — see genre files for depth)

- **Classical period melodies** are often clear-arched, balanced phrases with cadential closure. Mostly stepwise; leaps usually filled in.
- **Romantic melodies** are wider in range, more chromatic, more emotionally extreme. Wagner, Tchaikovsky.
- **Bebop melody** is leap-driven, chord-tone-heavy, fast, and rhythmically irregular. Outlines harmony aggressively. Charlie Parker.
- **Pop / rock melodies** are usually narrow-range, repetition-heavy, and rhythmic. Verse melodies often live within a 5th; choruses open up.
- **Hip-hop / R&B vocal melodies** have heavy rhythmic syncopation and often use blue notes (♭3, ♭5, ♭7 in major). Melisma is common in R&B.
- **EDM "melodies"** are often short, repetitive synth motifs whose interest is timbral and rhythmic, not melodic in the classical sense.
- **K-pop melodies** often combine multiple distinct hooks within a single song — each section has its own memorable line. The "post-chorus" or "drop chorus" introduces a third melodic identity.
- **Film/TV melody** ranges from full-blown themes (Williams, Goldsmith, Zimmer) to fragmentary motivic gestures. Theme writing is a specific discipline — see `genres/film-tv-scoring.md`.
- **Folk melodies** prioritize singability — narrow range, mostly stepwise, predictable phrase lengths.

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't write a melody by stacking chord tones.** Strict chord-tone-only melodies sound mechanical. The non-chord tones are where the soul lives.
- **Don't write melodies note-by-note without thinking about contour.** A line that goes ↑↓↑↓↑↓ with no shape is forgettable, even if every note is "right".
- **Don't aim for too high a peak too early.** If bar 2 is the highest note, the rest of the melody is descent; the listener doesn't have a destination.
- **Don't ignore rhythm.** A melody that's pitch-only is half a melody. Always consider syllabic density, syncopation, and the rhythm of the contour.
- **Don't forget the tessitura.** Especially for vocal melodies, where a line lives matters more than where it occasionally goes. Match it to the singer.
- **Don't fight the underlying harmony.** A melody that consistently lands on the 7th over every chord can sound deliberately strange (good if intended) or accidentally sour (bad if not). Know which you're doing.

## Diagnostic checklist for "this melody isn't working"

When the user has a melody that feels off, walk through:

1. **Is there a clear contour shape?** Or is it noodly?
2. **Where's the climax?** Is there one? Is it placed well?
3. **Is the range appropriate** for the voice/instrument and the section's role (verse vs. chorus)?
4. **Is the step/leap balance right** for the genre?
5. **Are the strong-beat notes mostly chord tones?** If they're all NCTs, the melody might feel suspended.
6. **Is there enough repetition** for memorability? Too much for interest?
7. **Does the rhythm have a clear identity** or is it shapeless?
8. **Does the line answer itself** at phrase boundaries (antecedent–consequent), or is every phrase just trailing off?
9. **Does the climax or peak moment fall on a memorable lyric or harmonic event?** Or is the highest note arbitrary?
10. **Is the melody distinguishable from the bass line and the chord voicings?** Sometimes a melody fails because it's overlapping too closely with another part.

Diagnose, then prescribe specific fixes.
