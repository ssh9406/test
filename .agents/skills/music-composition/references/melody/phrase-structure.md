# Phrase Structure

A phrase is the basic unit of musical "thought" — a self-contained melodic-harmonic gesture that ends with some kind of cadential closure. It's the musical equivalent of a sentence in language. Phrase structure is how phrases combine into larger units (periods, sentences, hybrid forms) and how their grammar shapes the listener's expectation.

## What is a phrase

A phrase is a complete musical thought — a unit that begins, develops, and ends with a cadence (or cadential gesture). It corresponds roughly to a singer's breath or an instrumentalist's bow stroke, though it can be longer than a single breath.

Three diagnostic features:

1. **Internal coherence** — the notes belong together; the listener perceives them as a single gesture.
2. **Cadential closure** — the phrase ends on a cadence (or cadence-like resolution), giving the listener a clear "ending point" before the next phrase begins.
3. **Pacing** — phrases have a sense of breath; a phrase that doesn't pause is one extended gesture, not two phrases.

A phrase can be as short as 2 bars or as long as 16+ bars, depending on tempo and style. The "default" classical phrase is 4 bars; the default pop phrase is also typically 4 or 8 bars, depending on tempo.

## Phrase length conventions

| Genre / context | Default phrase length |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| Classical period (Mozart, Haydn) | 4 bars (with frequent 4+4 = 8-bar periods) |
| Classical late-Romantic (Wagner, Mahler) | Variable, often 6, 8, or asymmetric |
| Pop / rock verse | 4 bars (with 4+4 forming an 8-bar half-section) |
| Pop / rock chorus | 4 or 8 bars |
| Blues | 4 bars (12-bar form is 3 × 4-bar phrases) |
| Jazz standards | 8-bar phrases (32-bar AABA = four 8-bar phrases; 16-bar A) |
| Folk songs (anglo-american) | 4 or 8 bars |
| Hip-hop verse | 16 bars (a "16"); often subdivided into 4 × 4 |
| EDM | Long phrases, often 16 or 32 bars, defined by structural changes |
| K-pop | Mixed: pre-chorus often 4+4, chorus often 8 bars, post-chorus often 4 bars |

These are defaults. Phrase length is a creative choice; deviation from the default creates expectation and surprise.

## The 4-bar default

Why 4 bars? Several converging reasons:

- **Speech rhythm**: a typical sung lyric phrase fits in roughly 4 bars at moderate tempo.
- **Breath length**: 4 bars at moderate tempo (~120 BPM) is roughly 8 seconds — comfortable for a singer.
- **Memory**: a 4-bar phrase is short enough for the listener to hold in working memory and feel as a single unit.
- **Cultural inheritance**: Western European folk and dance music established 4-bar phrasing as default.

Phrases that are 3 bars or 5 bars are perceived as asymmetric — striking in classical contexts, more accepted in jazz and contemporary pop. Phrases of 2 bars are often felt as half-phrases (or as "antecedents" of a 4-bar period). Phrases of 6+ bars feel expanded.

## Periodic structure (antecedent + consequent)

The classical period is two phrases that complement each other:

- **Antecedent**: the first phrase, ending with weak cadence (typically half cadence on V, or imperfect authentic cadence). Sets up an expectation.
- **Consequent**: the second phrase, ending with stronger cadence (typically PAC). Resolves the expectation.

Together they form a balanced, sentence-like statement. The grammar:

```
Antecedent (4 bars, ends HC or IAC)
Consequent (4 bars, ends PAC)
———
Period (8 bars total)
```

### Parallel period

Antecedent and consequent share the same opening (often identical for the first 1–2 bars), with the consequent diverging to provide stronger closure.

`a a' b a` is a typical small-scale parallel period: motif `a` opens both halves, `a'` is its variation in the antecedent, `b` is contrasting material, and a return to `a` (slightly altered for cadence) closes the consequent.

### Contrasting period

Antecedent and consequent have different opening material. The connection between them is the question-answer relationship of their cadences (HC → PAC), not melodic similarity.

Less common than parallel period in classical music, but still standard.

### Modulating period

The consequent ends in a different key (often the dominant) instead of the original tonic. Common in sonata-form expositions for transitions.

## Sentence structure

The sentence (a term from Schoenberg, formalized by William Caplin) is a different grammatical structure: a phrase that grows from itself rather than answering itself.

Three parts:

1. **Presentation** (typically 4 bars): a basic idea (~2 bars) followed by its repetition (~2 bars). The repetition can be exact, sequenced, or varied.
2. **Continuation** (typically 4 bars): increasing fragmentation, accelerated harmonic rhythm, "liquidation" of the basic idea.
3. **Cadence**: the conclusion, embedded in the continuation or as its own brief gesture.

```
Presentation (basic idea + repetition, 4 bars)
Continuation (fragmentation, accelerated motion, 4 bars including cadence)
———
Sentence (8 bars total)
```

A famous example: the opening of Beethoven's Piano Sonata No. 1 in F minor. The first 4 bars present a motif and its repetition; the next 4 fragment and accelerate, ending in a cadence.

### Sentence vs. period — the difference

- **Period**: question + answer. Antecedent ends weakly, consequent ends strongly. Two equal phrases in dialogue.
- **Sentence**: statement + development + conclusion. One growing phrase, internally articulated.

Both are 8 bars in classical practice. The grammar is different. A sentence has a more directional, growing feel; a period has a more balanced, dialogic feel.

## Hybrid structures

Real music often blends sentence and period grammar.

- **Compound period**: a period where each phrase is itself a sentence. Total: 16 bars.
- **Compound sentence**: a sentence where the presentation is itself a small period. Total: 16 bars.
- **Sentence with consequent**: a sentence completes, then a parallel sentence (or period) follows as a "consequent" at the larger scale.

These hybrids are classical conventions but the principles apply broadly. A pop verse-chorus pair can be analyzed as a hybrid period (verse = antecedent, chorus = consequent) or as a sentence (verse-chorus-bridge-chorus structure).

## Asymmetric and expanded phrases

Most phrases are 4 or 8 bars. Asymmetry is striking precisely because it deviates from the default.

### 5-bar phrases

A 4-bar phrase plus a one-bar extension. The extension often elaborates the cadence ("delaying" the resolution). Common in folk traditions (Irish reels, Scottish strathspeys) and in 20th-century classical.

### 6-bar phrases

Two 3-bar groups, or 4+2 (a 4-bar phrase with a 2-bar tag), or 2+2+2 (three short groups). Mahler uses these often.

### 7-bar phrases

Often 4+3 or 3+4 — a regular 4 with one added or one taken away. Brahms is the master.

### Expanded phrases (extension techniques)

A phrase of "default" length expanded by:

- **Repetition of an internal motif** before completing the phrase
- **Extension of the cadence** through deceptive cadence, evaded cadence, or pedal point
- **Internal sequence** that lengthens the development before the cadence
- **Insertion of a contrasting interpolation**

The result: a 4-bar phrase can become 6, 7, or 9 bars without losing its essential grammar.

### Truncated phrases

Phrases shorter than expected. A 3-bar phrase where 4 was expected. Used for breathlessness, urgency, or specific effects (e.g., "cutting off" a phrase to suggest interruption).

## Phrase elision

When the cadence of one phrase coincides with the start of the next, eliding them into a single beat. Effect: forward propulsion; the listener doesn't get the breath they expected.

Common in Bach, Brahms, and modern jazz. In pop, less frequently — pop phrases tend to have clear gaps between them for vocal breath.

Notation: an elision often shows up as a strong-beat downbeat that's both the resolution of the previous phrase and the opening of the next. Sometimes marked with a "phrase elision" indication in the score.

## Phrases in popular music

Pop song phrasing follows similar grammar but with different defaults.

### Verse phrases

Pop verses are typically 4-bar phrases, with two or four phrases per verse (8 or 16 bars total). Within a verse, phrases often follow:

- **Parallel period** structure (each pair of phrases is parallel)
- **Repetitive** structure (each phrase is a slight variation of the same idea)
- **Building** structure (phrases get more rhythmically dense as the verse progresses, leading into the pre-chorus)

### Chorus phrases

Pop choruses often use:

- **Same-melody phrasing**: each 4-bar phrase has the same melody (the chorus hook)
- **Bookending**: the chorus opens and closes on the title hook, with development in the middle 4 bars

The chorus melody is *less* grammatically complex than verses because memorability is the priority. A chorus where every phrase ends differently is harder to remember than one where the phrases are nearly identical.

### Pre-chorus phrasing

Pre-choruses often use phrases shorter than the verse (2-bar phrases instead of 4) to accelerate the harmonic and melodic rhythm into the chorus.

### Bridge phrasing

Bridges are the most "developed" section in pop. They often introduce a new phrase length (e.g., a 4-bar phrase among 8-bar verses), modulate, or use sentence-like grammar with continuous development.

## Phrasing in jazz

Jazz phrases are often longer (8 bars) and looser (driven by swing feel and chord changes rather than strict 4-bar grammar).

A 32-bar AABA standard has eight phrases of 4 bars each (or four 8-bar units of two paired phrases). The bridge (B) often departs from the A's phrase structure.

Jazz solos typically build phrases that span 4, 8, or sometimes 16 bars, with breath points at chord-change boundaries. A great improviser thinks in long phrases; a beginner often plays in 1–2 bar fragments.

## Phrasing in hip-hop

Hip-hop verses are usually 16 bars, often divided into 4 × 4-bar phrases or sometimes 8 × 2-bar phrases. The phrase structure is heavily influenced by rhyme scheme — the end of a couplet (or quatrain) often coincides with phrase end.

Rappers vary phrase length deliberately: shorter phrases (2 bars) for impact, longer phrases (4+) for storytelling, 8-bar runs for breathless intensity.

## Phrasing in EDM

EDM works at the section level rather than the phrase level. A "drop" is typically 16 or 32 bars, structured by build-ups, drops, breakdowns, and returns rather than phrase grammar in the classical sense. But within a section, basslines and lead synths still follow phrasing principles — short repeated motifs that build over many bars.

## Phrasing in film/TV

Film scoring varies wildly. A short cue may be a single phrase. A longer cue can use through-composed phrasing (each phrase contrasts) or motivically tight phrasing (every phrase derived from the leitmotif).

Trailer music often uses extended phrases that build over 30+ seconds, with the "drop" or climax functioning as a structural cadence.

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't impose 4-bar default on all genres.** Hip-hop is built on 16-bar phrases; some folk traditions have asymmetric phrasing as default. The 4-bar phrase is a Western European convention.
- **Don't ignore phrase boundaries when composing.** Melodies that don't breathe (no clear cadential moments) feel exhausting. The listener needs a place to land before the next idea begins.
- **Don't make every phrase the same length.** A song with eight 4-bar phrases lined up identically is rhythmically rigid. Mix in variation — a 6-bar phrase among the 4-bars, or an extended cadence — to add interest.
- **Don't confuse phrase length with section length.** A "16-bar verse" is the section; the phrases inside it might be 4 bars each. Always specify which scale you're discussing.
- **Don't forget to land cadences.** A phrase that doesn't end somewhere harmonically is not really a phrase — it's an incomplete thought. Even pop phrases need at least an implied cadential resolution.
- **Don't impose classical period structure on music that wants something else.** Sentence structure, hybrid forms, and asymmetric phrases all have their place.

## Quick decision matrix

| Want | Use |
|------|-----|
| Balanced, conversational feel | Parallel period (antecedent + consequent) |
| Building, growing feel | Sentence (presentation + continuation) |
| Striking, surprising | Asymmetric phrase (3, 5, 7 bars) |
| Hypnotic, meditative | Repetitive phrasing with little variation |
| Driving, propulsive | Phrase elision (no breath between phrases) |
| Conclusive ending | Strong PAC at the end of a clear period |
| Open-ended | End on HC or deceptive cadence; leave the period unfinished |
| Pop chorus memorability | Identical phrases bookending the chorus |
| Pop verse build | Phrases that get rhythmically denser as the verse progresses |
| Jazz solo coherence | Longer phrases (8 bars) that develop a single motif |
