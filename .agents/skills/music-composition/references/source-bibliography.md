# Source Bibliography and Verification Guide

> **Curated snapshot:** 2026-04-27. This file is a maintainer-facing bibliography map. It is not a replacement for active web research where current scenes, copyright, AI tools, charts, law, platform behavior, or living-artist references are involved.

Use this file to verify claims, deepen future documents, and choose trustworthy sources. The skill itself should usually synthesize guidance rather than cite bibliography entries directly to the user unless citations are requested.

## Source hierarchy

When checking a claim, prefer sources in this order:

1. **Primary sources** — official documentation, legal texts, platform chart methodology pages, museum/archival pages, composer/performer statements, scores, recordings.
2. **Authoritative reference sources** — Grove/Oxford Music Online, Encyclopaedia Britannica, national libraries, national academies, university presses.
3. **Specialist pedagogy** — conservatory/Berklee materials, orchestration texts, reputable practitioner books.
4. **Peer-reviewed research** — especially for ethnomusicology, cognition, copyright, AI, and regional scenes.
5. **Industry reporting** — Billboard, IFPI, RIAA, Recording Academy, Music Business Worldwide, Reuters, AP, reputable local press.
6. **Community sources** — useful for scene vocabulary and practice, but verify before turning into general rules.

## Western theory and composition

Recommended stable references:

- Aldwell, Schachter, and Cadwallader — *Harmony and Voice Leading*
- Kostka, Payne, and Almén — *Tonal Harmony*
- Piston — *Harmony* and *Counterpoint*
- Caplin — *Classical Form*
- Hepokoski and Darcy — *Elements of Sonata Theory*
- Schoenberg — *Fundamentals of Musical Composition*
- Persichetti — *Twentieth-Century Harmony*
- Forte / Straus — post-tonal and set-theory references

Use these for:

- Roman numeral analysis
- cadences and phrase structure
- modulation and chromatic harmony
- classical forms
- counterpoint and voice-leading

## Jazz harmony and arranging

Recommended references:

- Mark Levine — *The Jazz Theory Book* and *The Jazz Piano Book*
- Ted Pease — *Jazz Composition*
- Berklee harmony/arranging materials
- Sher Music / Real Book conventions, with caution about edition differences
- Scores and transcriptions from authoritative publishers when exact changes matter

Use these for:

- ii-V-I, tritone substitution, modal interchange
- chord-scale choices
- rootless voicings
- rhythm-section arranging
- lead-sheet notation conventions

Do not treat simplified online chord charts as authoritative for famous-song analysis.

## Orchestration and instrument idiom

Recommended references:

- Adler — *The Study of Orchestration*
- Rimsky-Korsakov — *Principles of Orchestration*
- Piston — *Orchestration*
- Blatter — *Instrumentation and Orchestration*
- Philharmonia Orchestra instrument guides
- Vienna Symphonic Library Academy / Instrumentology
- Actual scores and session-player feedback

Use these for:

- ranges, registers, transposition
- articulation and idiomatic writing
- ensemble balance
- orchestral texture and doublings

Always distinguish playable range from good tessitura.

## Songwriting, lyric writing, and topline

Recommended references:

- Pat Pattison — lyric writing and prosody materials
- Sheila Davis — lyric craft books
- Jimmy Webb — *Tunesmith*
- Berklee songwriting/topline materials
- Published interviews and stems where available
- Native-speaker review for non-English lyrics

Use these for:

- prosody
- rhyme and scansion
- title placement
- verse/chorus contrast
- hook design

## Popular music, production-aware composition, and arranging

Recommended references:

- Everett — rock harmony and form research
- Tagg — popular music semiotics and analysis
- Moore — analysis of recorded popular song
- Middleton — popular music studies
- Berklee arranging and rhythm-section materials
- Reputable producer/arranger interviews, when clearly attributable

Use these for:

- pop/rock form
- rhythm-section writing
- arrangement density
- production-facing composition decisions

## Country, Americana, and roots music

Useful sources:

- Country Music Hall of Fame and Museum
- Encyclopaedia Britannica entries on country music, bluegrass, Nashville Sound, outlaw country
- Library of Congress archives and recordings
- Published Nashville Number System references
- Artist/songwriter interviews and session-player materials

Verify separately:

- current country radio/streaming trends
- regional country scenes
- Nashville Number System notation details when writing full charts

## Gospel and CCM

Useful sources:

- Encyclopaedia Britannica on gospel music
- Recording Academy category definitions and field guides
- Gospel Music Association / industry sources, with context
- Hymnals, church songbooks, and denominational resources for liturgical contexts
- Scholarly work on Black gospel, Pentecostal worship, Southern gospel, and CCM

Verify separately:

- denominational fit
- current worship set norms
- theological language and intended community

## Metal, punk, and hardcore

Useful sources:

- Encyclopaedia Britannica entries on heavy metal, punk, hardcore punk, straight edge
- Scene histories, label archives, and oral histories
- Guitar/bass/drum practitioner sources for substyle-specific technique
- Current local-scene reporting and festival/label rosters

Verify separately:

- current subgenre labels
- regional scenes
- tuning/gear conventions for specific substyles

## Brazilian music

Useful sources:

- Library of Congress music blog and Brazilian collections
- Encyclopaedia Britannica on bossa nova, samba, Brazil performing arts
- Scholarly work on MPB, samba, bossa nova, choro, and funk carioca
- Brazilian local press and platform charts for current funk/pop scenes
- Native Portuguese lyric review

Verify separately:

- current funk substyles and regional terms
- Portuguese prosody
- whether a requested track references Brazil broadly or a specific scene

## Korean traditional music and Korean pop

Useful sources:

- 국립국악원 국악사전
- 한국민족문화대백과
- 국립국악원 recordings and educational materials
- Korean-language academic writing and practitioner interviews
- Circle Chart and official platform/agency sources for current K-pop trends

Verify separately:

- romanization
- instrument technique terms
- current idol/pop production trends
- chart and fandom/platform behavior

## MENA, South Asian, Southeast Asian, and other regional files

Useful sources:

- MaqamWorld and practitioner-led maqam resources
- Academic/practitioner raga and tala resources
- National archives, regional music scholarship, and local-language sources
- Current local platform charts and press

Use caution:

- Do not reduce maqam/raga/dastgah/gamelan to scales.
- Do not present Western equal-tempered approximations as the full tradition.
- When writing serious tradition-based work, recommend practitioner collaboration.

## AI, copyright, and style-reference requests

Useful sources:

- U.S. Copyright Office AI reports and copyright circulars
- Court opinions and filings where available
- Reuters / AP / major legal reporting for ongoing litigation
- Official terms from music platforms and AI tools
- Music publishers/labels' official statements when relevant

Verify every time:

- current legal status
- platform rules
- AI model terms
- living-artist style policies
- public-domain status

## Streaming-service and user listening context

Useful sources:

- Official platform chart pages such as Spotify Charts, Apple Music charts, YouTube Charts, Circle Chart, Oricon, Billboard, and Official Charts Company
- User-provided public playlists, track lists, Replay/Wrapped summaries, and scrobbling logs
- Spotify Web API and Apple/Last.fm documentation when a developer or connected-tool workflow is explicitly relevant
- Local music platforms and charts for the target market

Use caution:

- Never ask users for passwords, account tokens, or private credentials.
- A user's personal listening history is taste evidence, not a universal market signal.
- Platform charts have different weighting rules and demographics.

## Microgenres, hybrid genres, and regional trend evolution

Useful sources:

- Current reference tracks and playlists from the scene itself
- Local press, label rosters, festival lineups, DJ sets, and producer interviews
- Platform tags and community terminology, checked against listening evidence
- Major genre files in this skill as stable craft anchors

Use caution:

- Do not over-define unstable scene labels.
- Do not treat one viral tag as a whole genre.
- For hybrid genres, identify which ingredient controls form, groove, harmony, texture, and cultural context.


## Creative workflow and brainstorming

Useful sources:

- Composition pedagogy texts that emphasize sketching, constraint, and revision
- Songwriting process interviews, when tied to concrete craft decisions
- Creativity research only when translated into musical behavior, not generic productivity advice
- User feedback from actual agent sessions, especially where brainstorming prompts succeed or fail

Use these for:

- musical brainstorming prompts
- iterative user-agent collaboration
- decision-log and project-card templates
- writer's block workflows

Verify separately:

- whether a proposed workflow creates usable musical material
- whether the workflow preserves user agency rather than over-directing the composition

## Chart and trend research

Use `research/web-music-trend-research.md` for workflow. High-signal sources include:

- official charts and chart methodology pages
- platform charts and playlists, interpreted cautiously
- local music press
- IFPI / industry reports
- Billboard / Official Charts / Circle / Oricon / YouTube / Spotify chart pages

A chart position does not automatically become a composition rule. Extract repeated variables across multiple references.

## Release validation and audit

Useful sources:

- The current project files themselves: `SKILL.md`, `references/00-navigation.md`, `RELEASE-ROADMAP.md`, and validation reports
- Stable theory references for disputed notation, harmony, and voice-leading claims
- The automated regression script for broken links and known high-risk patterns
- Prompt smoke-test transcripts from representative user sessions

Use these for:

- first-release readiness decisions
- prompt-routing validation
- high-use asset audits
- release-blocker triage

Use caution:

- A passing script does not prove the music theory is complete or correct.
- A prompt smoke test can pass behaviorally while still needing fact review.
- Release reports must distinguish checks actually run from checks merely planned.

## Verification checklist before adding or revising a file

- Are historical claims supported by at least one reliable source?
- Are current-scene claims marked with snapshot/currentness guidance?
- Are terms from living traditions spelled and romanized carefully?
- Are examples labeled as simplified when they are not exact transcriptions?
- Are cultural references specific rather than vague?
- Are instrument claims checked for range, tessitura, and playability?
- Are copyright/style-reference claims framed cautiously?
- Did `scripts/music_theory_sanity_check.py` pass?
- If preparing a release, did the first-release readiness checklist and prompt smoke tests get reviewed?
