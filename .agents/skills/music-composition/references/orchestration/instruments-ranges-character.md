# Instruments — Ranges and Character

This file is a quick reference for the major instrument families: their ranges, characteristic registers, idiomatic writing, and what to use them for. It's not exhaustive (each instrument has volumes written about it), but it covers what a composer or arranger needs to know to make sensible choices.

For voicing decisions across instruments, see `voicing-and-texture.md`. For decisions about how many instruments to use simultaneously, see `arrangement-density.md`. For genre-specific orchestration, see relevant `genres/*.md` files.

## When to consult this file

- The user is writing for a specific instrument or ensemble
- Choosing between instruments for a melodic line
- Avoiding ranges that are awkward for the instrument
- Understanding why a part sounds wrong (often a register or character mismatch)
- Idiomatic writing — what's natural for each instrument

## How to read this file

Each instrument is given:

- **Range**: written range (the notes you'd write in the score) — sometimes different from sounding range due to transposition.
- **Sweet spot / tessitura**: where the instrument sounds best and is easiest to play.
- **Character by register**: how the instrument's tone varies across its range.
- **Idiomatic uses**: what the instrument is naturally good at.
- **Genre / context**: where you'd typically encounter it.

## Strings (orchestral)

The most flexible family. Strings can play almost any line; they sustain indefinitely (with bow changes), play double-stops and chords, and produce a vast range of timbres.

### Violin

- **Written range**: G3 to E7 (extending to A7+ for soloists). Concert pitch (non-transposing).
- **Sweet spot**: G3–A6.
- **Character by register**:
  - **Low (G3–B3)**: warm, gutsy, "throaty"
  - **Mid (C4–C5)**: lyrical, default singing range
  - **High (D5–E6)**: brilliant, soaring, intense
  - **Very high (F6+)**: piercing, often virtuosic
- **Idiomatic**: melodic lines, arpeggios, double-stops, fast passages, sustained singing tone. The lead voice in orchestral writing.
- **Notes**: Written in treble clef. Section divisi (violins splitting into 2+ parts) is standard. Tremolo, pizzicato, harmonics, sul ponticello, and many other effects are available.

### Viola

- **Written range**: C3 to E6 (alto clef typically; treble clef in high register).
- **Sweet spot**: C4–G5.
- **Character**: darker and warmer than violin. Often described as "the voice that gets lost in the orchestra" — the viola's mid range is in the same zone as cellos and second violins, so it can be hard to feature.
- **Idiomatic**: inner-voice writing, lyrical solos in its mid-high register, intimate accompaniment. Featured prominently in chamber music.
- **Notes**: Alto clef takes practice to read. The viola's middle and lower register is where it sounds most "viola" — high register starts to sound violinish.

### Cello

- **Written range**: C2 to A5 (bass clef typically; tenor clef in upper register; treble clef in very high).
- **Sweet spot**: G2–E5.
- **Character by register**:
  - **Low (C2–G2)**: thick, growly
  - **Mid (A2–E4)**: rich, singing — the most beloved register
  - **High (F4–A5)**: lyrical, intense, even strained
- **Idiomatic**: melodic and bass roles equally; doubles bass an octave higher; capable of soaring solo lines. The "tenor" of the string section.
- **Notes**: Tenor clef appears for moderately high passages to avoid ledger lines. Pizzicato in low register is punchy and resonant.

### Double bass

- **Written range**: E1 to D5 (bass clef, sounds an octave lower than written).
- **Sweet spot**: E1–A3.
- **Character**: foundation of the string section. Provides bass throughout the orchestra; can play melodic in its upper range (rare in classical, common in jazz).
- **Idiomatic**: bass lines, walking bass (jazz), pizzicato in jazz/pop. Bowed bass in classical for sustained low notes.
- **Notes**: Transposes down an octave. Most basses go to E1; some have a C1 extension for very low notes.

### String section conventions

Standard orchestral string section: violin I, violin II, viola, cello, double bass. Modern/Classical orchestras have varying section sizes (e.g., 16-14-12-10-8 in a full Romantic orchestra; smaller in Classical-period works).

Common scoring techniques:

- **Unison melody** (all strings on one line): warm, full
- **Octave doublings** (e.g., violin I + cello an octave below): rich, hymnic
- **Divisi**: strings split into multiple parts; common for chord-voicing in slower passages
- **Pizzicato** for sections: percussive, energetic
- **Tremolo**: intense agitation or shimmering
- **Mute (con sordino)**: softer, hazier

## Strings (popular)

### Acoustic guitar

- **Written range**: E2 to E6. Notated treble clef, sounds an octave lower than written.
- **Sweet spot**: A2–E5.
- **Character**: warm, percussive, harmonic. Steel-string is bright; nylon (classical) is warmer.
- **Idiomatic**: chord strumming, fingerpicking, arpeggios, melodic lines in mid-high register. Folk, country, singer-songwriter staple.

### Electric guitar

- Same range as acoustic. Tone varies enormously with effects, amp, pickup configuration.
- **Idiomatic**: power chords, riffs, lead lines, sustained tones with feedback, palm-muted rhythms. The cornerstone of rock.
- **Notes**: extended techniques include bends, slides, hammer-ons/pull-offs, vibrato, harmonics, whammy bar effects.

### Bass guitar (electric)

- **Written range**: E1 to G4 (4-string standard tuning); 5-string bass extends to B0.
- **Sweet spot**: E1–C3.
- **Character**: foundation of pop, rock, R&B, funk. Round, percussive, can be played fingerstyle (warm, smooth) or with a pick (punchy, articulate).
- **Idiomatic**: groove-focused bass lines; melodic bass in funk and prog rock; sustained roots in pop ballads.

### Banjo, mandolin, ukulele, and other folk strings

Each has its own range and character. Banjo: bright, plucky, bluegrass and country. Mandolin: shimmery tremolo, folk and classical. Ukulele: small, warm, Hawaiian and indie pop. Each has its own idiomatic patterns — researching a specific tradition is wise.

## Woodwinds

### Flute

- **Written range**: C4 to C7 (or higher). Concert pitch.
- **Sweet spot**: G4–B6.
- **Character by register**:
  - **Low (C4–F4)**: hollow, breathy, intimate
  - **Mid (G4–G5)**: pure, lyrical
  - **High (A5–C7)**: brilliant, piercing
  - **Very high (above C7)**: shrill
- **Idiomatic**: agile melodic lines, runs, trills, sustained singing tones. Almost always non-doubling (one player per part).
- **Variants**: piccolo (sounds an octave higher; brilliant top voice), alto flute (lower, warmer), bass flute (rare).

### Oboe

- **Written range**: B♭3 to A6.
- **Sweet spot**: D4–F5.
- **Character**: nasal, plaintive, very distinctive. The orchestra tunes to the oboe's A.
- **Idiomatic**: lyrical melodies, especially in the middle register. Solo passages in pastoral or melancholy contexts. Doubling other instruments thinly.
- **Variants**: English horn (cor anglais; sounds a fifth lower; even more melancholy), oboe d'amore (between oboe and English horn).

### Clarinet

- **Written range**: D3 to G6. Transposing instrument: most common is B♭ clarinet (sounds a M2 lower than written); A clarinet (sounds m3 lower) for sharp-key music.
- **Sweet spot**: G3–C6.
- **Character by register**:
  - **Chalumeau (low, D3–F4)**: dark, smoky, woody
  - **Throat (F♯4–B♭4)**: weak, less resonant — write through it, not in it
  - **Clarion (B4–C6)**: bright, expressive — the main melodic register
  - **Altissimo (above C6)**: piercing, used for special effect
- **Idiomatic**: agile, expressive in all registers (except throat). Smooth legato; rapid runs; lyrical solos.
- **Variants**: bass clarinet (sounds an octave lower), E♭ clarinet (smaller, brighter), contrabass clarinet (rare, very low).

### Bassoon

- **Written range**: B♭1 to E♭5. Bass clef and tenor clef.
- **Sweet spot**: F2–G4.
- **Character**: woody, double-reed buzz. "The clown of the orchestra" in some passages; deeply solemn in others. Can be lyrical or comedic.
- **Idiomatic**: bass voice in winds, melodic in mid-high register, comedic effects (staccato low notes — see "Sorcerer's Apprentice"), sustained pedal tones.
- **Variants**: contrabassoon (sounds an octave lower; among the lowest instruments).

### Saxophones

- **Written range** (depends on size): all transposing.
  - **Soprano sax (B♭)**: written B♭3 to F♯6
  - **Alto sax (E♭)**: written B♭3 to F♯6
  - **Tenor sax (B♭)**: written B♭3 to F♯6
  - **Baritone sax (E♭)**: written B♭3 to F♯6
- All saxes have the same written range; their sounding ranges differ by their key.
- **Character**: smooth, flexible, expressive. Range from sweet to growling.
- **Idiomatic**: lead lines (jazz), section work (big band), solos (jazz, pop, R&B). The most flexible single-reed instrument.
- **Notes**: The saxophone family is the cornerstone of jazz wind sections. Less common in orchestral music (though Ravel, Glazunov, and others wrote for it).

## Brass

### Trumpet

- **Written range**: F♯3 to D6 (typically; advanced players go higher). Most often B♭ trumpet (sounds M2 lower); also C trumpet (concert pitch).
- **Sweet spot**: B♭3–G5.
- **Character**: bright, brilliant, declarative. The trumpet is the most penetrating brass instrument.
- **Idiomatic**: fanfares, bright melodic lines, jazz lead, lyrical solos with a mute. Strong in mid-high register.
- **Variants**: piccolo trumpet (high, brilliant), flugelhorn (warmer, mellower), cornet (slightly mellower than trumpet).

### French Horn

- **Written range**: F2 to F5 (in concert pitch as F horn; transposes a P5 lower than written, so a written G3 sounds C3).
- **Sweet spot**: F3–G4.
- **Character**: warm, noble, melodic. The most "vocal" brass instrument. Can blend into woodwinds or stand out as solo.
- **Idiomatic**: melodic solos, sustained chordal harmony, hunting calls (historical), heroic fanfares, soft chordal pad behind woodwinds.
- **Notes**: Horn players use the right hand inside the bell to adjust pitch and color. Hand-stopping changes the timbre dramatically.

### Trombone

- **Written range**: E2 to F5 (tenor trombone; bass clef and tenor clef).
- **Sweet spot**: B♭2–C4.
- **Character**: noble, full, slightly slower-articulating than trumpet due to slide mechanism. Smooth glissandos possible (and idiomatic).
- **Idiomatic**: chordal harmony in brass section, solo lyrical lines, jazz solos with slide effects, big band shouts.
- **Variants**: alto trombone (smaller, higher), bass trombone (lower, with extra valves for very low notes), contrabass trombone (rare).

### Tuba

- **Written range**: D1 to F4 (depends on instrument; F tuba is higher, B♭ tuba lower).
- **Sweet spot**: F1–B♭2.
- **Character**: foundation of the brass; deep, rumbling. Surprisingly agile for its size when written for the instrument.
- **Idiomatic**: bass voice in brass section; doubling cellos and basses an octave below; rare melodic solos in mid-high register.

### Brass section conventions

Standard orchestral brass: 4 horns, 3 trumpets, 3 trombones, 1 tuba. Big band brass: 4 trumpets, 4 trombones (no horn or tuba). Brass band: a different family altogether (cornets, flugelhorns, baritones, euphoniums).

## Percussion

A vast family. Two main subdivisions: pitched and unpitched.

### Pitched percussion

- **Timpani** (4 drums tuned to specific pitches; range roughly D2 to A3): the orchestral pitched bass drum. Tuned for each piece. Strong rhythmic and tonal role.
- **Xylophone** (F4 to C8 or higher): bright, dry, percussive. Often doubling melodic lines for emphasis.
- **Marimba** (C2 to C7): warm, woody, sustaining. Solo and ensemble use; warmer than xylophone.
- **Vibraphone** (F3 to F6): metallic, sustained with motor-driven vibrato. Jazz and classical solo. Pads can be used for muting.
- **Glockenspiel / Bells** (G5 to C8): bright, bell-like.
- **Crotales**, **Tubular Bells**, etc. — specific colors for specific moods.

### Unpitched percussion

The full universe is too vast for this file. Major categories:

- **Drums** (snare, bass drum, toms, kick): rhythmic backbone in pop, jazz, rock.
- **Cymbals** (suspended, crash, hi-hat, ride): sustained and accent percussion.
- **Hand percussion** (tambourine, shaker, claves, congas, bongos): texture and rhythm.
- **World percussion** (djembe, cajón, talking drum, frame drum, etc.): each has its own role.

For drum kit programming and writing, see `../rhythm-groove/groove-and-feel.md` and genre-specific files.

## Voice

### Vocal range categories

Standard four-part choral ranges (extended to six for solo voices):

| Voice | Range (typical) | Range (extended) |
|-------|-----------------|------------------|
| Soprano | C4 to G5 | A3 to C6 |
| Mezzo-soprano | A3 to E5 | F3 to A5 |
| Alto | F3 to D5 | E3 to F5 |
| Tenor | C3 to G4 (sometimes notated octave higher) | B2 to A4 |
| Baritone | A2 to E4 | F2 to G4 |
| Bass | E2 to C4 | C2 to E4 |

### Vocal registers (chest, mix, head)

Singers move between vocal registers:

- **Chest voice**: lower register; full, resonant, "speaking-like".
- **Mix voice / middle**: blends chest and head; the comfortable middle register.
- **Head voice**: upper register; lighter, "head-resonant".
- **Falsetto** (for males): light, pure, unfull tone in upper register.

The transition points (passaggi) are where the voice naturally changes character. Skilled singers smooth them; novice singers may "break" between registers.

### Vocal characteristics by genre

Different genres have different vocal expectations:

- **Classical**: trained, even tone across registers, vibrato, full dynamic range, careful diction.
- **Pop**: less classical formality; mix voice and belt prominent; auto-tune common in modern production.
- **R&B / soul**: melisma, vocal runs, ad-libs, raspy or smooth depending on artist.
- **Rock**: belted, sometimes raw; head voice rare except for certain styles.
- **Jazz**: scatting, melodic improvisation, Sinatra-style phrasing, conversational tone.
- **Hip-hop**: rap (rhythmic speech) more than singing; melodic vocals (sing-rap) increasingly common.
- **Folk**: natural, conversational; minimal vibrato; often nasal.
- **Musical theatre**: belted "money notes" in chest voice mid-high; controlled mix voice for sustained passages.

## Keyboard instruments

### Piano

- **Range**: A0 to C8 (88 keys).
- **Sweet spot**: bass clef from F1 to C4; treble from C4 to C7.
- **Character**: most flexible solo instrument. Can play melody, harmony, bass simultaneously. Wide dynamic range. No sustain like winds or strings but has pedal-controlled sustain.
- **Idiomatic**: solo, accompaniment, ensemble. Foundation of art song, jazz combo, much pop production.

### Harpsichord

- **Range**: roughly C2 to G6.
- **Character**: plucked rather than struck; less dynamic variation; bright, percussive.
- **Idiomatic**: Baroque solo and continuo. Limited modern use but appears in early-music performance and certain rock contexts (Beatles, prog).

### Organ

- **Range**: depends on the specific instrument; church organs have multiple manuals (keyboards) plus pedals, with combined range A0 to A8+.
- **Character**: sustained, powerful, capable of vast dynamic range and timbral variety through stops.
- **Idiomatic**: sacred music, classical solo, jazz Hammond B-3 (electric organ with rotating Leslie speaker — very different sound).

### Synthesizer

- **Range**: depends on settings; can theoretically produce any pitch.
- **Character**: as variable as the user's design choices. Can imitate other instruments or produce purely synthetic sounds.
- **Idiomatic**: pop, electronic music, film scoring. Modular synths in experimental music.

## Choosing instruments — what each does best

A rough decision guide:

| Want | Try |
|------|-----|
| Sustained melodic warmth | Cello, viola, English horn, French horn |
| Bright, declarative melody | Trumpet, oboe, soprano voice |
| Soft, lyrical accompaniment | Strings (muted), harp, piano (gentle) |
| Driving rhythmic energy | Drum kit, distorted electric guitar, sustained brass |
| Mysterious, atmospheric | Solo flute, marimba (soft), harp, ambient pad |
| Powerful low end | Tuba, contrabassoon, bass guitar, low organ |
| Agile, virtuosic line | Violin, flute, clarinet, piano |
| Bluesy, soulful melody | Saxophone, blues guitar, voice with melisma |
| Full orchestral wash | Strings divisi, full woodwinds, brass chord support |
| Intimate, narrative-friendly | Piano + voice, acoustic guitar + voice, solo woodwind |
| Tradition-specific instrumental color | A specific instrument used idiomatically within its tradition or fusion context; research tuning, role, ornamentation, and cultural use before dropping it in |

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't write outside the instrument's range.** The viola can't play below C3; the trumpet's lowest practical note is F♯3. Check ranges before composing.
- **Don't ignore tessitura.** A line that's technically playable but lives in an awkward register sounds strained.
- **Don't pick instruments without knowing their character.** A trumpet melody is very different from an oboe melody, even with the same notes.
- **Don't mix transposing and non-transposing instruments without care.** When writing for a B♭ clarinet, write a M2 above the desired sounding pitch. Easy to forget.
- **Don't double instruments that don't blend.** Cello and trombone have similar registers but different timbres — they may not fuse. Test combinations.
- **Don't ignore practical limitations.** Brass players need to breathe; long phrases without rest are problematic. Pizzicato is short and percussive — sustained pizz lines are awkward.
- **Don't over-specify or under-specify articulation.** A score with no articulation lets the player default; one with too many marks becomes unreadable.
