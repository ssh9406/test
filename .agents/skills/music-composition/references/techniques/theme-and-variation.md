# Theme and Variation

Theme and variation is both a *form* (a piece structured as a theme followed by progressively transformed restatements) and a *technique* (the operations used to vary a theme). This file focuses on the technique: what gets varied, how, and to what effect. The form is treated in `form/classical-forms.md`.

For motivic development (related but different), see `melody/motivic-development.md`. For the sonata-form-style fragmentation that's distinct from variation, see also `melody/motivic-development.md`. For 20th-century process-based variation (like Reich's phasing), see `techniques/20th-century-techniques.md`.

## When to consult this file

- The user wants to vary a theme without changing it beyond recognition
- Writing a variations set
- Working out a piece organized around a single tune that gets repeated with changes
- Jazz solos as variations on chord changes
- Folk variation traditions (each verse the same melody, gradually embellished)
- Songwriting where verses share melody but feel different

## Theme and variation vs. motivic development

These are different techniques, often confused:

- **Theme and variation**: keeps the *whole theme* as the unit. Each variation transforms the theme as a whole — same length, same harmonic skeleton, same phrase structure. The theme is recognizable in every variation.
- **Motivic development**: extracts *fragments* (motifs) from a theme and develops those fragments freely. The theme as a whole may not appear; only its building blocks do.

In a sonata-form development section, motivic development dominates. In a variation set, theme and variation dominates. Sometimes they blend.

## What gets varied

When you "vary" a theme, what's actually changing? Several parameters can be transformed:

### 1. Melodic surface (figural variation)

The melody is decorated with passing tones, neighbor tones, ornaments, and rhythmic subdivisions. The basic skeleton (strong-beat notes) is preserved; the surface is elaborated.

This is the most common variation type. The listener still hears the original melody underneath the decoration.

Example: a theme with quarter notes becomes a variation in eighth-note triplets, with the same underlying pitches on each strong beat.

### 2. Rhythmic transformation

The theme's rhythm is changed while preserving pitch content. Common moves:

- **Augmentation**: notes get longer (a quarter-note theme becomes half notes).
- **Diminution**: notes get shorter.
- **New rhythm**: same pitches but completely new rhythmic shape.
- **Syncopation**: pitches displaced to weaker beats.

### 3. Harmonic recoloring

The chords accompanying the theme are changed. The melody might be the same, but the chords underneath produce different feelings.

Example: a theme harmonized with diatonic triads gets reharmonized with extended chords, secondary dominants, or substitutions. See `harmony/reharmonization.md` for techniques.

### 4. Modal transformation

The theme is recast in a different mode of the same tonic. Major theme → parallel minor. Minor theme → parallel major. Mode changes (Dorian instead of natural minor; Lydian instead of major).

Effect: dramatic emotional shift while keeping the theme recognizable.

Example: Schubert's *Winterreise* — songs in major mode with parallel-minor variants for "winter" effect.

### 5. Textural transformation

The accompaniment style changes:

- Block chords → arpeggios
- Solo melody → harmonized in 3rds and 6ths
- Chordal accompaniment → walking bass + chords
- Sparse accompaniment → dense polyphony

The theme remains; the texture around it is reimagined.

### 6. Contrapuntal transformation

The theme is combined with a counter-melody or a canon imitation. The theme is no longer alone but in dialogue.

Example: Bach's *Goldberg Variations* alternate decorative variations with canons (canon at the unison, 2nd, 3rd, 4th, etc.).

### 7. Character / topical transformation

The theme is recast in a new style — march, dance, hymn, scherzo, lullaby. The theme's surface changes radically; only the harmonic structure and phrase length identify the kinship.

Example: Beethoven's *Diabelli Variations* — some variations are completely transformed in character, almost unrecognizable.

### 8. Tonal transformation

The theme is in a new key. Less interesting on its own (it's just the theme transposed), but combined with other changes, can be effective.

### 9. Form-level transformation

The theme's internal structure is altered — perhaps a 16-bar theme is condensed to 8 bars, or expanded to 32 bars by inserting new material.

Less common in strict variation form (where length is usually preserved), but important in jazz and pop variation.

## Common variation types and their patterns

### Decorative variation

Surface ornament added to a clear theme. The listener recognizes the theme through decoration. Common in Mozart, Haydn, and pre-Romantic variation.

### Character variation

Each variation has a strongly different character — march, lullaby, scherzo, fugue. Beethoven and Brahms favored this approach.

### Form-altering variation

The variation includes a structural change — say, an introduction, a coda, or a fugal passage. Some variations are essentially mini-pieces in their own right.

### Fugal / canonic variation

A variation written as a fugue or canon on the theme. The theme becomes a contrapuntal subject. Common in Bach.

### Slow variation

A variation in noticeably slower tempo, often with deeper emotional expression. Adagio variations are typical at the climax of a variation set.

### Final variation

The closing variation, often most elaborate. May include extended coda, quotations of earlier variations, or transformative apotheosis of the theme.

| Work | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Bach, *Goldberg Variations* | 30 variations on a sarabande theme; every third variation is a canon at progressively wider intervals |
| Bach, *Aria with 30 Variations* (same as Goldberg) | Same |
| Handel, *Harmonious Blacksmith* (5 variations) | Compact set, mostly figural |
| Mozart, "Ah, vous dirai-je, Maman" Variations | 12 variations on the "Twinkle, Twinkle" tune |
| Beethoven, *Diabelli Variations* | 33 variations on a banal waltz; ranges from witty to profound |
| Beethoven, *Eroica Variations* | Op. 35; piano variations on his own theme |
| Schubert, *Trout Quintet* (4th movement) | Variations on his own song "Die Forelle" |
| Schumann, *Symphonic Etudes* | Piano variations as character studies |
| Brahms, *Variations on a Theme by Haydn* | Orchestral; ends with passacaglia |
| Brahms, *Variations and Fugue on a Theme by Handel* | Piano; concludes with massive fugue |
| Brahms, *Paganini Variations* | Piano; technical showpiece |
| Rachmaninoff, *Rhapsody on a Theme of Paganini* | Variations for piano and orchestra; 18th variation is famous |
| Britten, *Young Person's Guide to the Orchestra* | Variations on Purcell theme; pedagogical |
| Elgar, *Enigma Variations* | Each variation portrays a friend |

## Variation in non-classical contexts

### Jazz

Improvisation over chord changes is essentially variation. The chord progression (the "form" or "head") is the theme; each chorus of solo is a variation.

Jazz variation differs from classical:

- **Improvised**, not pre-composed
- **Spontaneous**, not deliberate
- **Variation often departs from theme melodically**, returning only periodically
- **Multiple choruses** allow accumulation: each variation builds on the previous

In jazz, the most thematic improvisers (Sonny Rollins, Wynton Marsalis, Joshua Redman) explicitly develop motifs from the head across their solos. Less thematic improvisers play patterns that fit the changes without referencing the theme.

### Blues

12-bar blues form is a classic variation framework. Each chorus uses the same 12-bar form; the variation comes in the soloist's performance. Multiple choruses build over the form.

### Folk traditions

Many folk traditions have variation built in. A ballad's verses may share a melody, but skilled singers vary subtly between verses — adding ornaments, slightly altering rhythm, introducing different inflections.

In Irish traditional music, a tune is rarely played the same way twice. Each repetition adds different ornaments (rolls, cuts, slides) so the melody is constantly transforming.

### Pop song verses

A pop song's two or three verses share a melody, but a great topliner subtly varies them — different syllabic placement, slight melodic alterations, different ad-libs. The melody is "the theme"; each verse is a "variation".

This is more subtle than classical variation but follows the same principle.

### Process music

Steve Reich's "Music for 18 Musicians" and similar works use process-based variation: a pattern slowly transforms through procedure (one note at a time getting added, or two voices slowly drifting out of phase). Each "variation" is one step in the process.

This is variation operating at a long timescale — a single idea unfolding over an entire piece.

## Designing a variation set

If the user wants to write a variations piece:

### 1. Choose the theme

The theme should be:

- **Memorable**: distinctive enough that variations remain recognizable.
- **Simple**: not over-elaborate; the variations need room to add complexity.
- **Harmonically clear**: a clear chord progression that can serve as the framework.
- **Manageable length**: 8 or 16 bars is typical; longer themes make variations longer.
- **Suitable for variation**: themes that already have lots of ornament are harder to vary.

### 2. Decide the variation strategy

- **Strict variation**: every variation has the same length and harmony as the theme.
- **Free variation**: variations can extend, contract, modulate.
- **Character-set variation**: each variation portrays a different character.
- **Process-based variation**: each variation extends a procedural idea.

### 3. Plan the variation arc

A satisfying variation set has a shape:

- **Variations 1–3**: surface decoration; the theme remains clear.
- **Variations 4–6**: more substantial transformation; mode change, character shift.
- **Middle variations**: maximum invention; counterpoint, fugue, expansion.
- **Slow variation**: emotional centerpiece, often near the end.
- **Final variation**: virtuosic display, summative.
- **Coda**: bringing it home.

### 4. Variety within unity

Each variation should be distinct in character. Avoid two variations in the same texture or rhythmic style.

### 5. Save the most surprising for late

Don't reveal all your tricks early. The audacious variations (modal, contrapuntal, character-shifted) work best after the listener has settled into the theme.

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't make variations too similar.** A set where every variation is "theme with eighth notes" gets boring fast. Variety is the point.
- **Don't lose the theme.** A variation that's so transformed it's unrecognizable isn't a variation — it's a free composition. The theme must remain perceivable, even if subtly.
- **Don't ignore harmonic skeleton.** Strict variations preserve the chord progression. Reharmonized variations are fine but should be clearly identified as such.
- **Don't over-decorate.** Surface ornament can suffocate the theme. Sometimes the most powerful variation is simpler than the theme, not more elaborate.
- **Don't ignore the variation arc.** A sequence of variations with no shape feels like a list, not a piece. Plan the journey.
- **Don't confuse variation with motivic development.** A piece that fragments the theme isn't a variation set; it's a development.

## Quick decision matrix

| Want | Try (variation type) |
|------|---------------------|
| Surface elaboration of theme | Figural / decorative variation (faster note values, ornaments) |
| Emotional darkening | Modal transformation (major to parallel minor) |
| Different character | Character variation (march, lullaby, scherzo, hymn) |
| Showcase contrapuntal skill | Fugal or canonic variation |
| Centerpiece of emotion | Slow variation (adagio, often with extended phrasing) |
| Modern textural variation | Reharmonization with extended chords or modal mixture |
| Climactic showcase | Final variation with extended coda, virtuosic display |
| Pop verse variation | Subtle melodic and ad-lib variation across verses |
| Jazz solo variation | Improvised variations over the head; thematic motivic development across choruses |
| Process-based variation | Gradual transformation over time (Reich, Glass style) |
