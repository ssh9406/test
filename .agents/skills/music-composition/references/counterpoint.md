# Counterpoint

Counterpoint is the art of writing two or more melodically independent lines that fit together harmonically. It's the discipline of *line vs. line* — how each voice has its own contour, rhythm, and identity, while the combined result still works as a coherent whole.

This is one of the deepest and most technical areas of Western composition. This file gives a working overview: what counterpoint is, the historical species method (Fux), tonal counterpoint (Bach-style), fugue, and how counterpoint shows up in modern music — including pop, film, and jazz arrangement.

## When to consult this file

- The user asks about counterpoint, polyphony, fugue, invention, or "two-part writing"
- Writing a countermelody or harmonized backing line
- Independent lines (e.g., bass + melody + countermelody) that all work together
- Bach-style chorale, fugue, or invention
- Renaissance polyphony, motets
- Modern uses: countermelodies in pop and film, contrapuntal jazz arranging
- Why "everything moves at once and clashes" in the user's piece — usually a counterpoint problem

## What is counterpoint

Counterpoint = "point against point" (note against note, voice against voice). It's the technique of combining two or more lines where:

1. **Each line is musically independent** — has its own contour, rhythm, melodic identity. Not just a chord stretched across multiple parts.
2. **The lines fit together vertically** — at every moment, the resulting harmony makes sense (consonant or strategically dissonant).
3. **The whole equals more than the sum** — the listener can either follow individual lines or listen to the combined fabric, and both work.

The opposite of counterpoint is **homophony**: one line that's the melody, accompanied by chords (no real voice independence in the chords). Most pop is homophonic. Most Bach is contrapuntal.

Counterpoint exists on a spectrum:

- **Pure homophony**: melody + block chords. (Most pop, hymns.)
- **Light counterpoint**: melody + active bass + maybe one countermelody. (Many film scores, jazz arrangements.)
- **Full counterpoint**: 3–6 independent lines, each substantial. (Bach, Renaissance motets.)
- **Strict counterpoint**: voices must follow rigid voice-leading rules. (Fugue, species exercises.)

## Why study counterpoint

Even if you'll never write a fugue, counterpoint reasoning improves:

- **Bass lines**: a melodic, independent bass line gives a track depth that block-chord bass doesn't.
- **Countermelodies**: a good second line in an arrangement is a counterpoint problem.
- **Inner-voice movement**: the alto and tenor of a chorale or string arrangement gain interest from contrapuntal motion.
- **Backing vocals**: harmonized vocals that follow the lead in parallel are weak; vocals that have their own independent line (while still harmonizing) are strong.
- **Synth pad orchestration**: layered pads with independent inner motion sound richer than blocks.
- **Solo improvisation**: a soloist who plays "around" a chord change creates implied counterpoint with the bass and harmony.

The discipline trains the ear and hand to think in lines simultaneously, which is one of the most important skills in composition.

## Species counterpoint (Fux's method)

Johann Joseph Fux's *Gradus ad Parnassum* (1725) codified counterpoint pedagogy through five graded exercises ("species"). Generations of composers — Haydn, Mozart, Beethoven, Mahler, Brahms — learned counterpoint from this book.

Species counterpoint trains a student to write a counter-line against a fixed *cantus firmus* (a slow melody, originally in whole notes). Each species adds rhythmic complexity.

### First species: note-against-note

Two voices, both in whole notes. Every note in the new voice (the counterpoint) sounds against one note of the cantus firmus.

Rules:

- Begin and end on a perfect consonance (P5, P8, or unison).
- Use mostly imperfect consonances (3rds and 6ths) in between.
- Avoid parallel P5s and P8s. (See `harmony/voice-leading.md`.)
- Move mostly by step in the counterpoint; leaps are fine but should be balanced.
- Aim for contrary motion (voices moving in opposite directions).
- Keep the counterpoint within a reasonable range; avoid awkward leaps and wide skips.

The discipline: write a counter-line that's *independent* (different contour from the cantus firmus) but *consonant* throughout. This trains line independence.

### Second species: 2:1

Two notes in the counterpoint for every one in the cantus firmus. Now half-notes against whole notes (or whatever ratio matches).

New rules:

- The first note of each measure (downbeat) must be a consonance.
- The second note (the off-beat) can be a passing tone (dissonance approached and left by step) or a consonance.
- Don't introduce parallel 5ths/8ves through the rhythm.

Effect: the counterpoint becomes more melodically active. Passing tones add melodic interest.

### Third species: 4:1

Four notes against one. Quarter notes against whole notes.

New rules:

- The first note of each beat-group must be a consonance.
- Three of the four sub-notes can be passing tones, neighbor tones, or consonances.
- Avoid awkward leaps in the counterpoint.

Effect: the counterpoint becomes a flowing line. Embellishment now happens at a denser rhythmic level.

### Fourth species: suspensions

Each note in the counterpoint is tied across the barline, becoming a suspension on the next downbeat.

The pattern: prepare (consonance) → suspend (dissonance) → resolve (down by step to consonance). The classic 4-3, 7-6, 9-8 suspensions.

Effect: dramatic dissonance with controlled resolution. This species teaches you the tension/release cycle that drives much of tonal music.

### Fifth species: florid (mixed)

All techniques combined. The counterpoint uses any rhythmic value (whole notes, half, quarter, eighth) and any device (passing tones, neighbor tones, suspensions). The line should be *musical* — not a textbook demonstration but a real, beautiful counter-line.

By the time a student reaches fifth species, they can write a counterpoint that sounds like a Bach two-part invention.

## Tonal counterpoint (Bach style)

Species counterpoint is "modal" — based on Renaissance modal voice-leading. **Tonal counterpoint** applies similar principles within functional tonality, with leading-tone resolution and dominant→tonic progressions.

Bach is the master. His Two- and Three-Part Inventions, the Well-Tempered Clavier, and the Goldberg Variations are studied as the gold standard of tonal counterpoint.

Key features:

- **Functional harmonic progressions** underly the counterpoint. Even "free" counterpoint sits within a clear key.
- **Phrases have cadences** — voice-leading converges on PAC, HC, deceptive, plagal, etc., as in homophonic music.
- **Voices have idiomatic ranges** for the instrument.
- **Dissonance is more freely treated** than in strict species — passing tones can be longer, suspensions can be ornamented.

Tonal counterpoint is the underpinning of fugue, invention, chorale, and many forms of contrapuntal writing into the 20th century.

## Imitation

A central technique in contrapuntal writing: one voice introduces a motif, then another voice repeats it (often delayed, often at a different pitch). The ear loves to track imitation — it creates dialogue between voices.

Types:

- **Strict imitation**: exact pitch and rhythm reproduction.
- **Free imitation**: similar but with adjustments (notes changed for harmonic reasons).
- **Imitation at the octave / 5th / 4th / etc.**: the imitating voice starts on a different scale degree.
- **Inverted imitation**: the imitating voice plays the inversion (intervals flipped).
- **Augmented / diminished imitation**: the imitating voice uses different rhythm (longer/shorter notes).

Imitation drives much of fugue writing, but it appears in pop too — call-and-response between voices in gospel, in Beatles backing vocals, in jazz arrangements.

## Fugue

A fugue is a contrapuntal form (and a contrapuntal *technique*) where multiple voices imitate a *subject* in a structured way. It's the most rigorous and technically demanding form in tonal music.

Components:

- **Subject**: the main theme, introduced alone by one voice.
- **Answer**: a second voice enters with the subject transposed (typically up a fifth or down a fourth — a "tonal answer" adjusted to remain in the home key, or a "real answer" with strict transposition).
- **Countersubject**: when the second voice plays the answer, the first voice plays a counter-line called the countersubject. This countersubject is reused throughout the fugue.
- **Episodes**: passages between subject entries, often built on motivic fragments and modulating sequences. Episodes provide breathing space and structural articulation.
- **Stretto**: subject entries overlap (one voice begins the subject before the previous voice has finished). Used for climax.
- **Augmentation / diminution**: subject played slower or faster, often near the end.
- **Pedal point**: a sustained tonic or dominant note, usually approaching the final cadence.

A typical fugue in 3 voices:

1. **Exposition**: voices enter one by one with the subject (or answer). Once all voices are in, the exposition is complete.
2. **First episode**: modulating, motif-based interlude.
3. **Second exposition** (in a related key): subject entries in the new key.
4. **Episode** → another set of entries, typically in further keys.
5. **Stretto and intensification** → climactic point.
6. **Final entry** in the home key, often with augmentation or pedal point.
7. **Cadence** in the home key.

Bach wrote the most famous fugues (the WTC contains 48). Beethoven wrote several (the *Grosse Fuge*, the fugue in his late sonatas). Shostakovich wrote 24 fugues for the *24 Preludes and Fugues*.

Fugue is rare in modern composition because it's labor-intensive and requires a specific aesthetic. But fugal moments — short stretches of fugal texture — appear in film scores, prog rock, and contemporary classical.

## Invention

A two-part invention is a smaller contrapuntal piece — like a baby fugue. Bach's 15 Two-Part Inventions are the classic study set. Features:

- A subject (motif), introduced alone, then imitated.
- Some development (sequencing, transposition).
- A clear key plan (typically I → V → I or I → vi → I).
- Roughly 30–60 bars long.

Inventions are an excellent practice exercise: simpler than fugue but trains the same skills.

## Contrapuntal devices and devices

A grab-bag of techniques used in contrapuntal writing:

| Device | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Invertible counterpoint** | Voices designed so they can swap (top voice becomes bottom and vice versa) without violating voice-leading rules. The interval inversion table (`harmony/voice-leading.md`) tells you which intervals stay consonant when inverted. |
| **Double counterpoint at the octave** | Most common; voices invert by exchanging an octave. |
| **Double counterpoint at the 10th / 12th** | Voices invert by exchanging a 10th or 12th interval. Less common; specific to advanced Bach. |
| **Triple counterpoint** | Three voices that can be arranged in any vertical order. |
| **Canon** | Strict imitation maintained throughout: every voice plays the same line, delayed. The simplest is a round (e.g., "Row, Row, Row Your Boat"). |
| **Crab canon (canon cancrizans)** | One voice plays the line forward, another plays it backward simultaneously. |
| **Mirror canon** | One voice plays the line; another plays the inversion. |
| **Mensuration canon** | Voices play the same line at different rhythmic values (one voice slower, one faster). |

These devices show up in Bach (especially the *Art of Fugue*, the *Musical Offering*, and the *Goldberg Variations*), in 20th-century serial music, and occasionally in modern composition (Reich's process pieces use canon-like techniques).

## Counterpoint in pop, jazz, and film

### Pop counterpoint

Pop is overwhelmingly homophonic, but counterpoint shows up in:

- **Bass lines**: a melodic, independent bass that's not just root-emphasizing is counterpoint. McCartney's bass lines are textbook contrapuntal craft. Motown bass.
- **Countermelodies**: a synth or string line that has its own contour against the lead vocal. Common in 80s pop production.
- **Backing vocals**: when backing vocals do something other than parallel harmony with the lead — they're contrapuntal. Beach Boys, Queen, Bee Gees.
- **Riffs that interact with the vocal**: the iconic guitar riff in "Smoke on the Water" or "Layla" exists in counterpoint with the vocal melody.
- **Pre-chorus pads**: a moving inner voice in a synth pad creates contrapuntal motion.

When advising on pop arrangement, suggest contrapuntal additions sparingly. A single well-chosen countermelody beats a dense fabric of competing lines.

### Jazz counterpoint

Jazz is mostly soloist-plus-rhythm-section (homophonic in texture), but contrapuntal techniques show up in:

- **Two-horn arrangements**: trumpet and saxophone playing complementary lines (Mulligan & Baker, Lee Konitz & Warne Marsh).
- **Bach-influenced jazz**: Modern Jazz Quartet's "Versailles", "Concorde". Bach plays well as jazz changes.
- **Solo piano**: pianists like Bill Evans, Brad Mehldau, Bud Powell, Keith Jarrett write contrapuntally — left and right hands as independent voices.
- **Ensemble passages in big band charts**: ensemble shouts often use parallel motion, but the bass and drums remain contrapuntal against the horns.

### Film counterpoint

Film scoring uses counterpoint heavily for sophistication. Common patterns:

- **Theme + countermelody**: the love theme stated in violins, with a counter-line in cellos or woodwinds.
- **Harmony in counterpoint**: one motif transformed and stacked against itself in different sections.
- **Multiple themes in dialogue**: when two characters meet on screen, their themes can be combined contrapuntally.

John Williams is famous for contrapuntal richness. The "Ride of the Rohirrim" in Howard Shore's *Lord of the Rings* layers multiple themes contrapuntally.

### EDM and electronic counterpoint

Less common but real. Aphex Twin, Squarepusher, Brian Eno's ambient: layered melodic lines with their own identities.

## Modern uses (post-tonal)

20th-century composers extended counterpoint into atonal and serial worlds:

- **Schoenberg, Webern, Berg**: 12-tone counterpoint where voice-leading is governed by row operations rather than tonal function.
- **Bartók**: contrapuntal pieces in a folk-modal idiom (the *Mikrokosmos*).
- **Stravinsky**: rhythmic counterpoint where independence is rhythmic as much as pitched.
- **Reich, Glass**: process-based "phasing" counterpoint where two identical lines drift out of sync.
- **Ligeti**: micropolyphony with dozens of voices producing dense textures (cluster-counterpoint).

Contemporary composers use counterpoint more freely than the strict tonal traditions — voice independence, line interest, and overall texture matter more than specific dissonance treatment.

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't write counterpoint as multiple "melody lines" without harmonic awareness.** Each line is independent, but they must fit together vertically. Write one line, then check it against the others at every beat.
- **Don't write contrapuntally for music that wants homophony.** A pop chorus rarely benefits from elaborate counterpoint — it competes with the hook. Counterpoint shines in instrumental contexts, bridges, and verse-second-half arrangements.
- **Don't ignore voice-leading basics.** Parallel 5ths and 8ves are the most common counterpoint mistake. Know the rules of the style you're working in (`harmony/voice-leading.md`).
- **Don't make the lines too rhythmically similar.** If both voices move at the same rate, they sound like one voice with chords. Independence requires rhythmic differentiation — when one voice holds, the other moves.
- **Don't make the lines clash in tessitura.** Two melodies in the same register fight for the listener's attention. Spread voices across registers (bass + alto + soprano, e.g.) for clarity.
- **Don't fugue every piece.** Fugue is a powerful but specific tool. Most music wants only contrapuntal moments, not a full fugue.

## A workflow for adding counterpoint to a piece

If the user has a melody and wants to add an independent line:

1. **Decide the counterpoint's role.** Is it a bass line, a countermelody, an inner voice? Each has different priorities.
2. **Sketch its rhythm first.** Where does it move? Where does it hold? It should *not* mirror the lead's rhythm — it should complement it. When the lead holds, the counter-line should move (and vice versa).
3. **Sketch its contour.** What's its shape? Often the inverse of the lead's shape (lead goes up, counter goes down). Always with an independent climax.
4. **Fill in pitches that fit the harmony.** Each beat should produce a consonant interval with the lead (3rd, 6th, octave, 5th preferred; 2nd or 7th for momentary tension).
5. **Check for parallel 5ths/8ves.** Fix by re-routing one voice.
6. **Test the lines separately.** If the counter-line, played alone, sounds boring — the line isn't independent enough. Strengthen its identity.
7. **Test combined.** Listen to both. The lines should be distinguishable but unified.
