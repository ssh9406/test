# Groove and Feel

This file is about what happens *between* the notational grid lines — the microtiming, dynamic variation, and ensemble interaction that makes a rhythm "groove" instead of just "play". Two players can perform identical notation and produce wildly different feels; that difference is the subject here.

For meter and notational rhythm, see `fundamentals/rhythm-meter.md`. For rhythmic devices that manipulate the grid (syncopation, hemiola, displacement), see `rhythmic-devices.md`. For odd meters and polyrhythm, see `odd-meters-polyrhythm.md`. For genre-specific groove conventions, see relevant `genres/*.md` files.

## When to consult this file

- The user asks about "groove", "feel", "pocket", "swing"
- A track that "sounds stiff" or "doesn't groove" despite correct notation
- Programming drums and wanting them to feel human
- Quantization decisions in a DAW
- Understanding why an ensemble locks together (or doesn't)
- Microtiming differences between genres

## What "groove" means

Groove is the felt quality of a rhythmic performance. It includes:

1. **Microtiming**: where each note actually falls relative to the strict grid (slightly ahead, on, or behind).
2. **Velocity / dynamics**: which notes are louder than others within a repeating pattern.
3. **Ghost notes**: very quiet notes that don't register as "the beat" but contribute to the texture.
4. **Articulation**: how short or long each note is — the duration relationships within the pattern.
5. **Ensemble interaction**: how players' microtiming relates to each other (locked together, slightly off, deliberately leaning differently).
6. **Repetition with variation**: a groove is a pattern that repeats, with small variations preventing exact mechanical repetition.

A groove can be described in notation, but the feel never can — it's a property of the performance.

## Microtiming — the millisecond layer

The grid says beat 2 happens 500ms after beat 1 (at 120 BPM). The actual snare hit might happen at 480ms (ahead of the beat), 500ms (on the beat), or 520ms (behind). This 20–40ms difference is microtiming, and it's the difference between "tight", "lazy", "rushing", and "dragging".

### On the beat

The note hits exactly with the click. Mechanical, "robotic" feel. Most quantized programmed drums sit here. Effective for genres that want machine-precision — techno, EDM, modern pop production.

### Ahead of the beat ("on top", "pushing")

The note hits slightly before the click. Creates a rushed, energetic feel. Lead instruments often push slightly to lead the band; drummers who push are often described as "driving".

### Behind the beat ("laid back", "dragging", "in the pocket")

The note hits slightly after the click. Creates a relaxed, lazy, soulful feel. Most great soul and R&B drummers play behind. Hip-hop sampled drums are often deliberately behind. The classic "pocket" feel.

### Combination feel

A drummer can hit the kick on the beat, the snare slightly behind, and the hi-hat slightly ahead. Each part has its own microtiming relationship. This creates the multi-dimensional feel that machine quantization usually destroys.

J Dilla's beat-making famously used uneven hi-hat timing — some hits ahead, some behind — to create a "drunken" feel that influenced an entire generation of producers.

## Swing

The most well-known microtiming convention. Eighth notes don't divide the beat in half — they divide it asymmetrically, with the first eighth longer than the second.

### The swing ratio

Different swing ratios for different feels:

| Ratio | Description | Genres |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| 50:50 | Straight eighths | Rock, modern pop, EDM |
| ~55:45 | Slight swing | Some shuffle, lazy pop |
| 60:40 | Medium swing | Jazz at uptempo, some R&B |
| 67:33 (2:1) | Triplet swing | Classic jazz, ballads |
| 70:30 or more | Heavy swing | Slow blues, deep shuffle |

The 67:33 ratio is mathematically equivalent to a triplet feel — the first eighth gets two-thirds of the beat, the second gets one-third. This is the "swing" notation in jazz where two eighth notes are written but played as a triplet quarter + triplet eighth.

### Swing in different genres

- **Jazz swing** — typically 60:40 to 67:33 at moderate to fast tempos; loosens to almost straight at very fast tempos.
- **Shuffle** (blues, rockabilly) — closer to 67:33; very pronounced.
- **Hip-hop swing** — usually a subtle swing on 16th notes (the "MPC swing" of 50–58% on 16ths), inherited from sampled jazz and funk drums.
- **House music swing** — sometimes a slight 16th-note swing for "groove"; more often straight.
- **Samba and bossa nova** — straight on the surface, but a subtle "Brazilian swing" of microtiming on the second sixteenth note.

### Swing on 8ths vs. 16ths

In jazz, swing is typically on eighth notes. In hip-hop, R&B, neo-soul, and modern dance music, swing is more often applied to 16th notes (since the 8ths are still felt as straight beats).

## Ghost notes

Very quiet notes that don't function as primary beats but fill in the rhythmic texture. Critical to funk, R&B, and most groove-heavy music.

A funk drum pattern might have:
- Loud snare on 2 and 4
- Loud kick on 1 and selected off-beats
- Ghost notes (very quiet snare hits) on every 16th note in between

The ghost notes aren't "heard" individually — they're felt as a continuous low-level texture that frames the loud accents. Without them, the groove sounds bare. With them, the groove "breathes".

The classic study: Bernard Purdie's drum patterns. The Purdie shuffle and the Purdie half-time shuffle are built on intricate ghost-note layering.

## Velocity and dynamic accents within a groove

Beyond microtiming, the dynamics within a repeating pattern shape the feel. A 16th-note hi-hat pattern can sound completely different depending on which notes are accented:

- All hits at equal velocity: mechanical, "hi-hat as click track".
- Accent on 1, 3 (downbeats): plain, square.
- Accent on 2, 4 (upbeats): lifted, "skipping".
- Accent on every other 16th: stuttering subdivision.
- Selective accents matching the kick or snare pattern: locks the hat into the groove.

DAW programmers should use velocity to imitate human variation. Real drummers can't hit at exactly the same velocity twice; programmed drums should vary by at least 5–10% to feel natural.

## Articulation — note length

Within a groove, the duration of each note matters. Two patterns with identical pitches and microtiming can feel different if one cuts notes off short and the other lets them ring.

Examples:

- Tight, staccato hi-hats vs. open, sustained ones.
- Bass notes cut short on the upbeat (jazz "walking bass" feel) vs. sustained (rock or R&B).
- Guitar palm-muting (chunky) vs. ringing strums (open) — gives radically different feels with identical notation.

## Ensemble interaction

In a band or production, the parts have a specific relationship to each other. Three common patterns:

### Locked

All players hit the same beats with the same microtiming. The ensemble sounds as a single unit. Rare in groove music — feels mechanical when overdone, but tight ensemble playing requires this on key downbeats.

### Layered

Each player has their own microtiming relationship. The drummer is on the beat, the bass is slightly behind, the rhythm guitar is slightly ahead. The combined feel is a multi-dimensional pocket. Most great soul and R&B grooves work this way.

### Conversational

Players "talk" to each other rhythmically, with one accenting where another rests. Common in jazz quartets, where the soloist and drummer respond to each other in real time.

A producer can imitate ensemble interaction by deliberately offsetting different programmed parts — putting the kick on the beat, the snare a few ticks behind, the hi-hat a few ticks ahead.

## "In the pocket"

A phrase used to describe a groove that locks together perfectly. The "pocket" is where all the elements — microtiming, accents, ghost notes, articulation — combine into a cohesive whole. It's a felt quality.

When a band is "in the pocket", the listener feels physically grounded. When a band is "out of the pocket", the listener feels uncertain or disoriented.

Pocket is genre-specific:

- A pop pocket is straight, tight, on-the-grid.
- A funk pocket is heavily syncopated, with ghost notes and behind-the-beat snare.
- A jazz pocket includes swing and conversational interaction.
- A reggae pocket emphasizes 2 and 4, with the kick and bass behind.
- A neo-soul pocket includes microtiming variation and a relaxed feel.

## Programming drums to feel human

If the user is programming drums in a DAW:

1. **Vary the velocity.** Each hit shouldn't be the same. Loud snares should be ~110–127, ghost notes ~30–60, regular hats ~60–90. Vary within those ranges by 5–10%.
2. **Add humanization.** Most DAWs have a "humanize" function that randomly offsets notes by a few milliseconds. Use it sparingly — too much creates a sloppy feel.
3. **Don't quantize everything to 100%.** Even 90% quantize keeps some human feel. For deliberately loose grooves, 70% quantize.
4. **Layer parts with intentional offsets.** Move the snare a few ticks behind the kick. Move the hat ahead. The combined feel is more "alive".
5. **Use ghost notes.** Add quiet hits between the loud ones. Even one or two ghost snare hits per bar transforms a stiff pattern.
6. **Vary the pattern over multiple bars.** A 1-bar repeating loop sounds robotic. A 2- or 4-bar loop with subtle variations (an extra ghost note here, a missed hat there) feels alive.
7. **Pick a feel consciously.** Decide whether the snare is on the beat, slightly ahead, or slightly behind. Don't leave this to chance.

## Quantization decisions

Quantization snaps notes to the grid. Different choices for different goals:

| Quantize to | Effect | When to use |
|-------------|--------|-------------|
| 100% to 16ths | Mechanical, perfect | EDM, modern pop, anywhere "on the grid" is the aesthetic |
| 80–90% to 16ths | Tight but humanized | Most pop, rock, R&B production |
| 100% to 16th-triplets (swung) | Mechanical swing | Classic hip-hop, MPC-style beats |
| 50% (preserve original feel) | Light correction only | Live drum tracking |
| Off (no quantize) | Pure performance | Jazz, classical, anywhere live feel matters most |

## Half-time vs. full-time feel

A common groove choice. The same tempo can feel "double-time" or "half-time" depending on where the snare hits.

- **Full-time feel**: snare on 2 and 4 of every bar.
- **Half-time feel**: snare on 3 only (every other bar's "2 and 4").

Half-time feels slower and heavier even though the BPM is the same. Switching between full-time and half-time is a primary contrast tool in trap, dubstep, post-rock, and modern pop.

A song at 140 BPM can be felt as 140 (full-time, dance-energetic) or 70 (half-time, trap or dubstep, slow and weighty).

## Genre-specific groove notes

### Pop

- Mostly on-the-grid (modern production aesthetic).
- Slight swing on 16ths in some sub-genres (R&B-influenced pop).
- Anticipations and pushes at chord changes are common.

### Rock

- Generally straight 8ths.
- Drummer leads the feel; pushing or laid-back is a band identity choice.
- Half-time choruses common in modern rock.

### Hip-hop

- 16th-note hi-hats with selective accent.
- Snare typically on 3 (half-time feel) in trap and modern hip-hop.
- "MPC swing" — slight 16th-note swing inherited from the Akai MPC drum machine workflow.
- Sampled drums maintain the original recording's microtiming.

### R&B / neo-soul

- Heavily microtimed; ensemble interaction is critical.
- Drums slightly behind the beat is the default.
- Ghost notes pervasive in drums and bass.

### Jazz

- Swing eighths (variable ratio).
- Drummer "comping" creates conversational microtiming.
- Walking bass on quarter notes, slightly behind the beat for "swing" feel.

### Latin

- Clave is the rhythmic skeleton; everything aligns to it.
- Microtiming is more "on the grid" than jazz, but has its own subtle feel.
- Different feels for son, salsa, samba, bossa, etc.

### Reggae and dub

- Kick and bass behind the beat for "rootsy" feel.
- Skank guitar precisely on 2 and 4 (or off-beats in steppers' style).
- Heavy use of dropouts (silences as part of the groove).

### Funk

- "On the One" — emphatic downbeat.
- Heavily syncopated bass and rhythm guitar with ghost notes.
- Drummer plays "in the pocket" — heavily microtimed, often behind.

### EDM (house, techno)

- 4-on-the-floor kick precisely on each beat.
- 16th hi-hats often slightly swung.
- Low microtiming variation — the aesthetic is precision.

### Drum and bass / breakbeat

- Sampled breakbeat drums maintain the original groove's microtiming.
- Snare on 2 and 4 (or syncopated breaks).
- Bass plays a separate rhythmic role from the drums.

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't tell the user to "add more groove".** Specify what dimension to adjust: more swing, more ghost notes, slightly behind the snare, more dynamic variation.
- **Don't apply jazz swing to non-jazz contexts.** Swing 8ths in a rock song sound wrong; the rock idiom uses straight 8ths. Genre matters.
- **Don't overcorrect microtiming.** A heavily microtimed groove is its own aesthetic; trying to "fix" it by quantizing destroys the feel.
- **Don't ignore the producer's intent.** Some producers deliberately use mechanical, on-the-grid feel as the aesthetic (Daft Punk, much of Kraftwerk, modern pop). Suggesting "humanization" misses the genre.
- **Don't conflate groove with complexity.** A simple straight-eighth pattern with great velocity variation can groove harder than a complex pattern that's mechanically played.

## Quick decision matrix

| Want | Try |
|------|-----|
| Mechanical, modern pop feel | 100% quantize to 16ths, equal velocities |
| Tight but human feel | 80–90% quantize, ±10% velocity variation, slight ghost notes |
| Funk pocket | Snare slightly behind beat, lots of ghost notes, accented hats |
| Jazz swing | 60:40 to 67:33 ratio on 8ths, walking bass slightly behind |
| Hip-hop boom-bap | MPC swing on 16ths, snare behind beat, sampled drums preferred |
| Trap | Half-time feel (snare on 3), 16th hat rolls, kick on tresillo |
| Reggae | Kick and bass behind, skank guitar on 2 and 4, ghost rim shots |
| Neo-soul | Heavy microtiming, drums laid back, bass with ghost notes |
| EDM | 4-on-the-floor kick precise, 16th hats with selective accent, sidechained pads |
| Latin | Tight clave alignment, ensemble locked to clave, percussion layered |
