# Guitar

Use this file when the user asks for guitar chords, riffs, voicings, strumming, fingerpicking, capo use, tuning, distorted guitar, acoustic accompaniment, or guitar playability.

## Core idea

Guitar is shape-based. A progression that looks simple on piano may be awkward on guitar, and a simple guitar shape can sound huge because of open strings, resonance, and strumming motion.

```text
Guitar part = tuning + position + string set + right-hand pattern
```

## Standard tuning reference

```text
E2 A2 D3 G3 B3 E4
```

Open strings matter. Keys like E, A, D, G, C, and their relative minors often feel natural because open strings support common voicings. Capos let singer-friendly keys keep guitar-friendly shapes.

## Chord-shape thinking

| Need | Guitar-friendly move |
|---|---|
| Bright pop openness | add9/sus shapes, open strings, capo |
| Rock weight | power chords, octaves, palm-muted roots |
| Folk movement | alternating bass, hammer-ons/pull-offs inside chord shapes |
| Country sparkle | double-stops, open strings, hybrid picking |
| R&B/gospel comp | 3-note shell voicings, 6ths, slides, syncopation |
| Metal precision | low-string pedal, palm muting, drop tuning, tight rhythmic cells |

## Acoustic guitar patterns

### Basic strum

```text
Down on strong beats, up on offbeats.
Good for pop, folk, country, worship.
```

### Muted sixteenth strum

```text
Right hand keeps continuous motion; left hand controls chord/stops.
Good for modern pop, funk-pop, country-pop.
```

### Alternating bass

```text
Bass note alternates root/5th or root/3rd while upper strings strum.
Good for folk, country, singer-songwriter.
```

### Fingerpicking

```text
Thumb: bass strings
Fingers: inner/top strings
Good for intimate ballads and arpeggiated patterns.
```

## Electric guitar patterns

### Power chord riff

```text
Root + fifth, sometimes octave.
Use distortion-friendly voicing; avoid full low triads.
```

### Octave line

```text
Root + octave with muted middle string.
Cuts through rock/pop without adding chord density.
```

### Double-stops

```text
Two-note shapes, often 3rds/6ths or bluesy bends.
Good for country, soul, rock fills.
```

### Palm-muted pulse

```text
Right hand mutes near bridge; pitch stays tight and percussive.
Good for punk, metal, pop-rock drive.
```

### Swells / ambient guitar

```text
Volume pedal or knob + delay/reverb.
Good for worship, film, post-rock, pads.
```

## Capo guidance

Use a capo when:

- the singer needs a different key but the guitar part depends on open shapes
- the song needs brighter voicings
- the arrangement wants multiple guitars in different registers
- a simple part needs shimmer without new harmony

Example:

```text
Sounding key: B♭
Capo: 3
Played shapes: G-D-Em-C
Effect: B♭ major pop progression with open-string guitar resonance.
```

## Tuning guidance

| Tuning | Use |
|---|---|
| Standard | general pop/rock/jazz/country |
| Drop D | low D power, folk/rock drones, easier power chords |
| DADGAD | modal folk, drones, suspended color |
| Open G / Open D | slide, blues, roots, resonant major shapes |
| Down-tuned standard | metal/doom weight, lower vocal keys |
| Extended-range | modern metal/djent low register |

Do not transpose guitar riffs casually without checking tuning. A riff may depend on an open string or one-finger shape.

## Voicing tips

- Low full triads under distortion get muddy; use power chords or spread voicings.
- Open-string add9/sus voicings sound modern and guitar-like.
- Jazz chords on guitar often omit roots or fifths.
- A single high-string counterline can be more effective than a new chord.
- For two guitars, separate roles: one rhythm bed, one hook/counterline, or different capo positions.

## Quick examples

### Open pop progression

```text
Capo 2, shapes: G - D/F# - Em7 - Cadd9
Sounding: A - E/G# - F#m7 - Dadd9
Use: pop, worship, country-pop.
```

### Punk / rock power loop

```text
E5 - A5 - B5 - A5
All downstrokes or tight alternate picking.
```

### Country double-stop fill

```text
Over G: play B-D → C-E → B-D on top strings, with a small slide into C-E.
Use between vocal phrases, not over every lyric.
```

## Common pitfalls

- **Do not write six-note piano chords for distorted guitar.** Distortion wants simpler voicings.
- **Do not ignore open strings.** They are part of the instrument's sound.
- **Do not assume every chord inversion is easy.** Guitar voicings are shape-dependent.
- **Do not put busy guitar fills under every vocal line.** Answer the singer.
- **Do not forget right-hand rhythm.** Strum/pick pattern can define the style more than the chord names.
