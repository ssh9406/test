# Brass

Use this file when the user asks for trumpet, horn, trombone, tuba, brass section writing, fanfares, horn lines, pop horn stabs, mutes, or brass playability.

## Core idea

Brass is powerful, directional, and endurance-limited. It can sound heroic, warm, sacred, funky, brutal, or comic depending on register, articulation, and voicing.

```text
Brass writing = register + breath + endurance + voicing + articulation
```

## Family roles

| Instrument | Strengths | Cautions |
|---|---|---|
| Trumpet | brilliance, lead lines, fanfares, pop stabs | high/loud writing fatigues quickly |
| Horn | warmth, noble midrange, blend with winds/strings | exposed attacks and high writing need care |
| Trombone | power, glissando, strong bass/mid lines, section chords | fast high agility is harder |
| Tuba | low foundation, weight, comic/agile potential | dense low writing can blur |
| Flugelhorn | soft trumpet-family warmth | less cutting than trumpet |

## Register and endurance

Brass can play high and loud, but not forever.

- Save the highest notes for climaxes.
- Give rests after loud high passages.
- Repeated staccato high notes are tiring.
- Low brass needs space for clarity.
- Horns blend well but can be buried if orchestrated too softly against dense textures.

## Voicing brass

### Four-part section

Common orchestral brass spread:

```text
Trumpet / Horn / Trombone / Tuba
Top voice clear, low voice grounded, inner voices not too close in low register.
```

### Pop horn section

Common smaller section:

```text
Trumpet + tenor sax + trombone
or trumpet + alto/tenor sax + trombone
```

Write rhythmic stabs and riffs like a section, not like block keyboard chords. Leave rests between hits.

### Horn pads

Horns are excellent at warm sustained harmony. Keep motion smooth and avoid constant high writing.

## Articulation and effects

| Technique | Use |
|---|---|
| Marcato accents | fanfare, impact, trailer/media hits |
| Legato | warm chorale, noble line |
| Staccato | rhythmic stabs, comedy, lightness |
| Fall / doit | jazz/pop horn idiom |
| Glissando | trombone slide effect, comic or expressive |
| Mutes | color change; trumpet/trombone especially |
| Rip | dramatic ascent into a note |
| Swell | cinematic build, brass pad motion |

## Brass by style

| Style | Brass writing priority |
|---|---|
| Classical/film | chorales, fanfares, counterlines, climactic support |
| Jazz/big band | section voicings, articulations, lead trumpet range, sax/brass balance |
| Funk/soul/pop | short stabs, riffs, call-response, tight articulations |
| Gospel | shout support, hits, organ/choir response |
| Metal/cinematic hybrid | low brass weight, unison hits, octave reinforcement |
| Musical theatre | clear rhythmic punctuation, character-specific color |

## Quick examples

### Simple fanfare

```text
Trumpets/horns: 1 - 5 - 1 octave shape
Trombones/tuba: root/fifth support
Rhythm: dotted or triplet pickup into downbeat.
```

### Pop horn stab

```text
Chord: C9
Voicing: trumpet E, sax B♭/D, trombone G or C
Rhythm: short syncopated hits after vocal line.
```

### Warm horn pad

```text
4 horns in close/mid spacing, soft dynamics.
Move mostly stepwise; let strings carry motion if needed.
```

## Common pitfalls

- **Do not write brass at maximum volume and height for long stretches.** Endurance matters.
- **Do not close-voice low brass too densely.** It muddies.
- **Do not write pop horns as sustained orchestral pads unless that is the intended sound.** Stabs/riffs often work better.
- **Do not forget breathing.** Brass phrases need air.
- **Do not overuse mutes/effects without a clear color reason.**
