# Vocals

Use this file when the user asks for vocal melody, singer range, tessitura, backing vocals, ad-libs, choir-adjacent pop writing, belting, rap/sing-rap, or lyric-melody fit.

## Core idea

The voice is an instrument with words attached. A vocal line must fit range, tessitura, vowel, breath, language, and emotional delivery.

```text
Vocal writing = range + tessitura + vowel + breath + text stress + persona
```

## Range vs. tessitura

- **Range**: the total notes a singer can produce.
- **Tessitura**: where the line sits most of the time.

A melody can be technically within range but still exhausting if it sits too high, too loud, or on hard vowels.

## Practical range guidance

Use these as starting points only. Individual singers vary a lot.

| Voice context | Comfortable writing idea |
|---|---|
| Pop lead | tailor to singer; choruses often sit higher than verses |
| Congregational/worship | keep range narrower and avoid sustained extremes |
| Musical theatre | range and belt/mix choices are style-specific |
| Rock/metal | high belt or harsh vocals require singer-specific knowledge |
| R&B/gospel | allow melisma/ad-lib space; do not overfill the written melody |
| Rap/sing-rap | rhythm, consonants, breath, and vowel placement are central |
| Choir/SATB | use choral ranges and balance; see `../orchestration/choral-writing.md` |

## Breath and phrase length

A singer needs breath before long phrases and after dense consonants.

Good defaults:

- 2-bar phrases are usually safe.
- 4-bar phrases need planned breathing or simpler rhythm.
- Fast lyrics need shorter phrases or rests.
- Sustained high notes need setup and recovery.
- Ad-libs should not cover the main lyric unless intentionally chaotic.

## Vowels and high notes

High notes are easier on some vowels than others, and vowel modification is common in singing.

Composition-facing guidance:

- Place big high notes on open or singable vowels when possible.
- Avoid cramming many consonants at the top of the range.
- If the title word has a difficult vowel, consider placing the emotional high point before or after it.
- For non-English lyrics, check vowel and stress with a native speaker when possible.

## Melody and lyric stress

A vocal melody should respect natural word stress unless the distortion is intentional.

Check:

- important syllables land on strong beats or longer notes
- weak syllables are not consistently over-accented
- melisma does not obscure key words
- repeated hook text still sounds natural
- consonant clusters have enough time

Pair with `../fundamentals/prosody-and-language.md`.

## Lead vocal writing by section

| Section | Vocal strategy |
|---|---|
| Verse | conversational range, lyric detail, rhythmic identity |
| Pre-chorus | rising energy, more repetition, harmonic tension |
| Chorus | title/hook clarity, wider range or stronger rhythm |
| Post-chorus | chant, vowel hook, repeated tag, simpler text |
| Bridge | new viewpoint, register shift, stripped rhythm, or climax |
| Final chorus | ad-libs, octave doubles, harmony stacks, tag repetition |

## Backing vocals

### Common backing-vocal roles

| Role | Use |
|---|---|
| Unison double | strengthen hook without changing harmony |
| Octave double | widen chorus, gender/texture contrast |
| 3rds/6ths harmony | pop/country/folk sweetness |
| Block harmony | gospel, musical theatre, pop stack |
| Call-response | R&B, gospel, country, funk, pop |
| Pad vowels | cinematic/pop support |
| Gang vocals | punk, pop-punk, stadium chorus, group identity |
| Ad-libs | final chorus lift, R&B/gospel release |

### Harmony stack strategy

- Start with the melody.
- Add one harmony below or above for clarity.
- Add more notes only where the lyric repeats or the chorus needs width.
- Keep consonants together if text clarity matters.
- Thin backing vocals during verse if the lead lyric is detailed.

## Rap and rhythmic vocal writing

Rap/topline rhythm is composition.

Check:

- breath points
- internal rhyme placement
- bar-line tension/release
- consonant density
- relationship to kick/snare
- when the flow changes across sections
- whether the hook simplifies after dense verse flow

For sing-rap, pitch may be minimal but contour still matters.

## Extreme or high-intensity vocals

For screams, growls, belting, and very high rock/metal vocals:

- write alternatives if the singer is unknown
- avoid long strings of maximum intensity
- plan rests after high or harsh phrases
- do not prescribe unsafe technique
- write the emotional contour, not just "scream here"

## Quick examples

### Pop chorus lift

```text
Verse range: A3-E4
Chorus range: C4-G4
Hook title lands on G4 once, then resolves downward.
Effect: lift without exhausting the singer.
```

### Backing vocal stack

```text
Lead: title phrase
BGV 1: third below on final two words
BGV 2: octave above only on repeat
Ad-lib: after the title, not over it.
```

### Congregational melody

```text
Range: about an octave
Rhythm: mostly quarter/eighth notes
Hook: repeated phrase with clear vowels
Avoid: long high melisma or unpredictable syncopation.
```

## Common pitfalls

- **Do not write for an abstract voice type when the song has a real singer.** Tailor to the person.
- **Do not confuse range with comfort.** Tessitura is the daily problem.
- **Do not put the hardest lyric syllable on the highest sustained note by accident.**
- **Do not let backing vocals cover the lead lyric.** Support the hook.
- **Do not prescribe extreme vocal techniques casually.** Keep it safe and singer-specific.
- **Do not ignore language.** Prosody errors make even good melodies feel wrong.
