# Film and TV Scoring

See `../research/web-music-trend-research.md` and `../research/reference-track-digging.md`.

Film and TV scoring is composition in service of visual storytelling. The composer's job is to support, comment on, or contradict what's on screen — never to overshadow it. This file covers the conventions, idioms, and craft of writing music for picture.

## When to consult this file

- The user is writing music for a film, TV show, video game, or trailer
- Scoring approach for a specific scene or mood
- Using leitmotifs, themes, or thematic development in narrative music
- Trailer music conventions
- Game music (related discipline)

## What's distinctive about film scoring

Film music is *applied* music. It serves a specific purpose:

- **Supporting emotion**: making sad scenes sadder, scary scenes scarier, joyful scenes more uplifting
- **Pacing**: pulling the viewer through slower passages, accelerating tense ones
- **Information**: telling the audience how to feel about characters and events
- **Continuity**: stitching disparate scenes together
- **Identity**: giving a film a sonic personality that makes it memorable

The composer is rarely the auteur of the experience. The director, editor, and music supervisor make many decisions; the composer's craft is matching their vision while bringing musical excellence.

## Historical eras of film scoring

### Silent film era (c. 1895–1929)

Live music accompanied silent films. Pianists, organists, or orchestras played from compilation books or improvised. The first "scores" were collected pieces matched to scene types.

### Classical Hollywood era (c. 1933–1960)

Max Steiner, Erich Korngold, Alfred Newman, Bernard Herrmann established Hollywood scoring. Drew heavily from late Romantic style (Strauss, Wagner, Mahler, Tchaikovsky).

**Idiomatic features**:
- Late Romantic harmonic language
- Symphonic orchestra
- Leitmotif use (Korngold from Wagner)
- Mickey-mousing (music synced precisely to action)
- Often wall-to-wall (continuous music throughout the film)

### New Hollywood / late 20th century (c. 1965–onward)

Jazz, rock, and pop influences enter scoring. Composers like Henry Mancini, Lalo Schifrin, John Barry mix idioms.

The 1977 Star Wars score (John Williams) revives the symphonic-leitmotif tradition for the blockbuster era.

### Modern film scoring (c. 1990–onward)

Multiple parallel traditions:

- **Symphonic / leitmotif-driven**: John Williams, James Horner, Howard Shore, Alan Silvestri, Patrick Doyle. Late Romantic + film tradition.
- **Hybrid orchestral / electronic**: Hans Zimmer, Junkie XL, Steve Jablonsky, Lorne Balfe. Big sound, layered orchestra + synths.
- **Minimalist / atmospheric**: Cliff Martinez, Jonny Greenwood, Mica Levi, Daniel Hart. Texture and color over melody.
- **Indie / chamber**: Jon Brion, Mychael Danna, Nick Cave & Warren Ellis, Hildur Guðnadóttir. Smaller ensembles, more intimate scoring.
- **Pop / rock-influenced**: Atticus Ross, Trent Reznor, Cliff Martinez. Contemporary popular idioms.
- **Folk/roots or tradition-specific influenced**: Mychael Danna, Tan Dun, Bear McCreary. Cultural specificity.

## Scoring techniques

### Leitmotif

A musical theme associated with a specific character, place, idea, or emotion. The theme returns throughout the film, transformed to reflect narrative changes.

See `melody/motivic-development.md` for the details. Summary:

- Leitmotif first appears clean and recognizable.
- Returns are transformed: minor mode for sadness, faster for action, fragmented for fragmentation, full orchestra for triumph.
- Multiple leitmotifs can combine (when characters meet on screen).
- Leitmotif's final form usually delivers narrative payoff.

Wagner pioneered leitmotif in opera. John Williams is the modern master in film. Howard Shore's *Lord of the Rings* is the most elaborate film leitmotif system, with dozens of interrelated themes.

### Mickey-mousing

Synchronizing music tightly to action — every gesture on screen has a musical equivalent. A fall has a descending glissando; a hit has a percussive accent.

Common in early Hollywood, animated films, and comedy. Less common in modern dramatic scoring (where it can feel overstated).

### Underscoring

Music that supports the scene without drawing attention. Often understated, harmonically simple, atmospheric. The music carries emotion but doesn't compete with dialogue.

The opposite of "score with melodic identity". Many great film scores are mostly underscore with brief melodic peaks.

### Source music (diegetic music)

Music that exists *in* the film world — characters can hear it. A radio playing in a scene; a band performing at a wedding. The composer may write source music to match the period or character.

Distinct from non-diegetic score (which only the audience hears).

### Hybrid scoring

Modern scores often combine orchestra with electronic elements. Hans Zimmer is the influential figure; many modern scores layer:

- Live orchestra (often recorded in London)
- Synthesized pads and bass
- Sound design (atonal, noise-based elements)
- Percussion (orchestral + tradition-specific / regional + electronic)
- Choir (especially in epic moments)

The combined sonic palette is bigger and more textured than orchestra alone.

### Spotting and sync

Spotting: deciding where music starts and stops in the film. Done with the director and music editor.

Sync: aligning music to specific moments (cuts, hits, dialogue). Modern composers work with timecode — every musical event is precisely placed against the picture.

## Scoring for specific genres

Different film genres have different scoring conventions.

### Action / blockbuster

- Big symphonic palette + electronic + percussion
- Drumming, often orchestral percussion battery
- Brass-heavy (low brass for power)
- Strings often in fast ostinatos
- Tempos 110–160 BPM for sustained action
- Rhythmic, often less melodically-focused

**Examples**: Williams's Star Wars; Zimmer's Pirates of the Caribbean; Silvestri's Avengers.

### Drama

- More melodic, subtle, emotionally nuanced
- Smaller ensembles often (string quartet, chamber)
- Slower tempos
- Piano often featured
- Long sustained chords; less rhythmic activity

**Examples**: Newman's American Beauty; Reznor/Ross's The Social Network; Guðnadóttir's Joker.

### Horror

- Atonal or modal
- Dissonant clusters and effects
- Sustained tension; sudden stings
- Often experimental sound design
- String "scrapes", piano interior playing, prepared instruments

**Examples**: Herrmann's Psycho; Krzysztof Penderecki (used in The Shining); Mica Levi's Under the Skin.

### Romance / drama-romance

- Lush Romantic harmony
- Long lyrical melodies
- Strings prominent
- Sometimes solo voice
- Often quiet, intimate moments alternating with sweeping climactic moments

**Examples**: Horner's Titanic; Newman's various; Zimmer's Interstellar (love theme).

### Comedy

- Often lighter texture, smaller ensembles
- Idiomatic instruments (banjo, ukulele, kazoo for goofiness)
- Mickey-mousing more acceptable
- Period-specific source music sometimes

**Examples**: Mancini's Pink Panther; Elfman's various; Williams's Home Alone.

### Sci-fi

- Often electronic or hybrid
- Atonal or modal harmonic language for "alien"
- Sound design may dominate over melody
- Choirs for "vast space" feel

**Examples**: Goldsmith's Star Trek; Zimmer's Interstellar, Dune; Williams's Star Wars (which is uniquely Romantic for sci-fi).

### Period drama

- Period-appropriate idioms
- Often actual period music adapted, plus original score in the style
- Authentic instruments

**Examples**: Mychael Danna's various Anglo-Indian period films; Patrick Doyle's Henry V; Howard Shore's The Aviator.

### Animation

- Often more rhythmic/syncopated
- Heavier use of mickey-mousing
- Sometimes character themes extensively developed
- Disney/Pixar tradition: Alan Menken (musical theatre influence), Randy Newman, Michael Giacchino.

**Examples**: Newman's Toy Story; Giacchino's Up; Menken's Disney Renaissance films.

## Trailer music

A specialized subgenre. Trailer music has its own conventions and its own production houses (Two Steps from Hell, Audiomachine, Brand X Music, X-Ray Dog, Hi-Finesse).

### Standard trailer structure

**For a 2:30 trailer**:

```
0:00–0:15 — Cold open: quiet pad, single instrument
0:15–0:45 — Slow build: introduce theme, percussion enters
0:45–1:15 — Mid build: layered orchestration, building drums
1:15–1:30 — First peak (small drop): full ensemble
1:30–1:45 — Cool-down or "logo moment": quiet, often with title card
1:45–2:15 — Massive final build: choir, full orchestra, percussion battery
2:15–2:30 — Climactic peak; possibly with end-tag
```

### Trailer idioms

- **Big Drum™**: cinematic taiko-style drums with heavy reverb
- **Hybrid percussion**: orchestral + tradition-specific / regional + electronic
- **Choir**: especially soaring female solo or full Latin-text choir
- **Brams**: low brass swells (the "BRAAAM" of *Inception* trailers, popularized by Zimmer)
- **Risers and downers**: synth/orchestral build-up effects
- **Drone**: sustained low pad as foundation
- **Theme statement**: a single melodic theme, recognizable, used repeatedly

### Trailer music vs. film score

Trailer music has different priorities:

- 2-3 minutes long; needs to be self-contained
- Has to create instant emotional response (no time to develop)
- Highly cliché-driven (the audience expects certain sounds)
- Often more bombastic than the final film score

## Game music

Related but distinct discipline. Differences:

- **Adaptive / interactive**: game music responds to player actions and game state. Vertical layering (more intensity = more layers added) and horizontal switching (different cues for different scenes) are standard techniques.
- **Looping**: many cues need to loop seamlessly during long gameplay.
- **Emotional sustain**: game cues often last for many minutes; need to sustain interest without becoming repetitive.
- **Theme-heavy**: leitmotifs and themes are often more central than in film, since players spend longer with each character/location.

**Notable game composers**: Nobuo Uematsu (Final Fantasy), Koji Kondo (Mario, Zelda), Jeremy Soule (Skyrim), Austin Wintory (Journey), Disasterpeace (Fez, It Follows), Olivier Deriviere, Jesper Kyd.

## Practical advice for film scoring

If the user is writing for picture:

1. **Watch the scene without music first.** Understand its emotional arc before committing to anything.
2. **Identify the scene's emotional beats.** Where does emotion shift? Where's the climax? Music should align with these.
3. **Decide on the scene's "voice".** Solo instrument? Full orchestra? Synth pad? Orchestral with rock drums?
4. **Sketch the cue's structure.** Roughly: where does it start, where does it peak, where does it end?
5. **Write to picture.** Use timecode if available. Hits, dialogue, and cuts inform musical decisions.
6. **Don't over-score.** Less is often more. Silence is a tool. Some scenes work best with no music.
7. **Match the film's overall sonic identity.** Don't suddenly introduce a new palette mid-film.
8. **Serve the director, the editor, the picture.** It's their film, not yours.

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't write music as if it stands alone.** Film music is service work. The best film cue might be musically simple but support the film perfectly.
- **Don't ignore dialogue.** Music in dialogue scenes should be sub-audible mostly — not competing with the words.
- **Don't apply concert-music logic.** Sonata form doesn't fit a 2-minute scene. Film cues have their own pacing.
- **Don't ignore temp tracks.** Directors often "temp" with existing music. Composers usually need to work with or against the temp; understanding it is half the job.
- **Don't overuse the standard idioms.** "Cinematic drums" and "Inception-style brams" are clichés. Use them when appropriate but don't over-rely.
- **Don't write 5-minute themes for a film that needs 30-second cues.** Match scope to scene.
- **Don't ignore the budget.** Many films can't afford a 90-piece orchestra; sample libraries and solo musicians are often the practical reality.

## Quick decision matrix

| Want | Try |
|------|-----|
| Epic blockbuster action | Hybrid orchestra + percussion + choir; Zimmer-style |
| Classic Hollywood feel | Late Romantic symphonic; Williams or Korngold style |
| Atmospheric drama | Sustained chords, sparse melody, piano + strings |
| Horror tension | Atonal clusters, sustained dissonance, prepared piano |
| Trailer music | Standard build-and-drop structure, big percussion, choir |
| Indie / quirky | Small ensemble, idiosyncratic instruments, light texture |
| Period film | Authentic period idiom; researched and respectful |
| Sci-fi alien | Electronic + atonal + choir; "vast" sound |
| Romance lift | Lush strings, lyrical melody, late Romantic harmony |
| Action with heart | Theme that transforms — minor in tension scenes, major in triumph |
| Animation comedy | Mickey-mouse where appropriate; lighter texture; idiomatic instruments |
| Game adaptive cue | Vertical layered structure (low intensity → high intensity layers) |
