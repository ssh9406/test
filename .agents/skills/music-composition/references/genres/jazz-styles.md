# Jazz Styles

> **Snapshot note:** Last content review snapshot: 2026-04-26. Use this file for stable compositional conventions, but verify current artists, charts, platform norms, and scene-specific references with active web research. See `../research/web-music-trend-research.md` and `../research/reference-track-digging.md`.

Jazz is not a single style but a tree of related traditions, each with its own harmonic, rhythmic, and improvisational conventions. This file covers the major styles and what makes each distinctive. The harmonic and chord-construction details are in `harmony/jazz-harmony.md` and `assets/jazz-voicings.md`; this file focuses on what differentiates the styles from each other.

## When to consult this file

- The user asks about a specific jazz subgenre (bebop, cool, modal, fusion, etc.)
- Composing or arranging in a jazz style
- Picking a jazz idiom for a project
- Understanding jazz historical/aesthetic differences

## Early jazz (c. 1900–1930)

Where jazz began. New Orleans roots, ragtime piano, blues vocal traditions, brass band heritage all converging.

**Key features**:
- 12-bar blues form pervasive
- Strophic song forms, simple harmonies (I, IV, V plus secondary dominants)
- Collective improvisation (multiple horns improvising simultaneously)
- 2-beat feel (emphasis on beats 1 and 3) in early styles, transitioning to 4-beat
- Brass-heavy ensembles (trumpet/cornet, trombone, clarinet)
- Strong polyphonic textures

**Sub-traditions**:
- **Dixieland / New Orleans jazz**: Louis Armstrong, King Oliver, Jelly Roll Morton, Sidney Bechet
- **Chicago style**: faster tempos, soloist-focused, 1920s development
- **Stride piano**: Fats Waller, James P. Johnson — left hand alternates bass note and chord; virtuosic right hand

## Swing era / Big band (c. 1930–1945)

The dominant popular music of its era. Large dance-band ensembles with section playing.

**Key features**:
- Big band: 3 trumpets, 3 trombones, 4 saxophones, rhythm section (piano, bass, drums, guitar)
- Tightly arranged section playing — harmonized brass and saxophone shouts
- 4/4 swing feel with the bass walking quarter notes
- Featured soloists (Duke Ellington's bandmembers, Benny Goodman's clarinet)
- 32-bar AABA standards as primary form
- Riff-based melodies
- Vocal features (Sinatra, Ella, Billie Holiday)

**Composers / arrangers**:
- Duke Ellington (the master arranger; integrated composer-bandleader)
- Count Basie (Kansas City swing)
- Benny Goodman, Glenn Miller, Tommy Dorsey (popular dance bands)
- Fletcher Henderson (foundational big band arranger)
- Mary Lou Williams (composer / pianist)

**Idiomatic features**:
- Block chord (homophonic) writing for sections
- "Drop 2" voicings standard
- Solos over functional harmony (II-V-I, blues changes)
- Plunger mutes and growl effects in brass
- Vocal scatting and "trading fours" (alternating 4-bar solos)

## Bebop (c. 1945–1955)

The first "art jazz" — small group, harmonically advanced, extremely fast and complex.

**Key features**:
- Small combos (typically quartet or quintet: piano, bass, drums + 1–2 horns)
- Fast tempos (often 200+ BPM)
- Intricate, fast-moving heads (themes) outlining complex harmony
- Improvisations using extended chord tones (9, 11, 13)
- Heavy use of secondary dominants, ii-V-I sequences
- Rhythmic complexity in the comping and solos
- Soloist-centric (the rhythm section "comps" — accompanies rhythmically)

**Composers / players**:
- Charlie Parker (alto sax — the central figure)
- Dizzy Gillespie (trumpet)
- Thelonious Monk (piano, composer with idiosyncratic style)
- Bud Powell (piano)
- Tadd Dameron, Kenny Clarke (drums)

**Idiomatic features**:
- "Bebop scales" (chromatic passing tones added to standard scales)
- Outlining ii-V-I changes constantly in solos
- Tritone substitutions
- Chromatic enclosure (surrounding a target note with chromatic neighbors)
- "Rhythm changes" (32-bar AABA based on Gershwin's "I Got Rhythm")
- "Bird blues" (Charlie Parker's reharmonization of standard blues)

## Cool jazz / West Coast (c. 1949–1955)

A reaction to bebop's intensity. Smoother, more arranged, more relaxed.

**Key features**:
- Slower to moderate tempos
- Thoughtful, lyrical solos rather than virtuosic explosions
- Arrangements with multiple voices (not just head + solos)
- More common chamber-jazz textures with non-traditional instruments (French horn, tuba, oboe)
- Emphasis on tone, dynamics, and space

**Players**:
- Miles Davis (*Birth of the Cool* nonet)
- Lennie Tristano, Lee Konitz, Warne Marsh (long contrapuntal lines)
- Chet Baker (lyrical trumpet, vocals)
- Stan Getz, Gerry Mulligan, Paul Desmond
- Chico Hamilton, Shorty Rogers (West Coast)

## Hard bop (c. 1953–1965)

A return to bebop's energy with added soul, blues, and gospel influences.

**Key features**:
- Bebop harmony but with stronger groove
- Blues and gospel motifs in melodies
- Heavier swing, more bluesy bass lines
- Often R&B-influenced, more "earthy" than cool jazz

**Players**:
- Art Blakey (Jazz Messengers — incubator for many great players)
- Horace Silver (composer of many hard bop standards)
- Cannonball Adderley, John Coltrane (early), Lee Morgan
- Wayne Shorter, Donald Byrd, Tina Brooks

## Modal jazz (c. 1959–onward)

A move away from chord-based improvisation toward modal frameworks. One scale per long passage instead of constant chord changes.

**Key features**:
- Static or slow-moving harmony
- Modal scales (Dorian, Mixolydian, Phrygian, Lydian) as the basis for solos
- Quartal and quintal voicings
- Long sustained passages on a single chord/mode
- Less reliance on functional ii-V-I motion

**Foundational works**:
- Miles Davis: *Kind of Blue* (1959) — the watershed; tracks like "So What" use only two modes
- John Coltrane: *My Favorite Things*, *A Love Supreme*
- Herbie Hancock, McCoy Tyner, Bill Evans (modal voicings on piano)

**Idiomatic features**:
- "So What" voicing (stacked 4ths)
- Long solos over single chords
- Modal melodies that emphasize characteristic notes (Dorian's natural 6, Phrygian's flat 2)
- Pedal points

## Free jazz / avant-garde (c. 1959–onward)

A radical break from harmonic and metrical conventions. Improvisation without preset chord changes or fixed meter.

**Key features**:
- Free of fixed harmonic structures
- Free of fixed meter (pulse may be present or absent)
- Group improvisation, often dense and chaotic
- Extended techniques (multiphonics, screams, noise)
- Spiritual or political ambitions in much of the music

**Players**:
- Ornette Coleman (the pioneer)
- John Coltrane (late period: *Ascension*, *Interstellar Space*)
- Albert Ayler, Pharoah Sanders
- Cecil Taylor (free piano, percussive style)
- Anthony Braxton, Sun Ra (composer-bandleader, Afrofuturism)
- AACM collective (Chicago)

## Fusion / Jazz-rock (c. 1969–onward)

Jazz combined with rock, funk, and electronic instruments. Electric jazz.

**Key features**:
- Electric instruments (electric piano, electric guitar, electric bass, synthesizers)
- Rock and funk rhythmic feels
- Often longer compositions with sectional structure
- Virtuosic instrumental playing
- Use of odd meters and complex rhythmic structures

**Foundational works / artists**:
- Miles Davis: *In a Silent Way* (1969), *Bitches Brew* (1970)
- Weather Report (Joe Zawinul, Wayne Shorter, Jaco Pastorius)
- Mahavishnu Orchestra (John McLaughlin)
- Return to Forever (Chick Corea)
- Herbie Hancock (*Head Hunters*)
- Frank Zappa (jazz-influenced rock)

## Smooth jazz / contemporary jazz (c. 1980–onward)

Commercial, radio-friendly jazz fusion. Clean production, emphasis on melody.

**Key features**:
- Polished production
- Lyrical, accessible melodies
- Pop and R&B influences
- Less harmonic complexity than mainstream jazz
- Saxophone often the lead voice
- Common in radio formats and adult contemporary

**Players**:
- Kenny G (the most commercially successful)
- George Benson, Earl Klugh
- Bob James, David Sanborn, Spyro Gyra
- The Crusaders

## Acid jazz / nu-jazz / electronic jazz (1990s–onward)

Jazz-influenced dance and electronic music.

**Players / projects**:
- Us3, A Tribe Called Quest (jazz samples in hip-hop)
- St Germain, Jazzanova (acid jazz / lounge)
- Brad Mehldau (modern acoustic jazz with rock influences)
- Esperanza Spalding, Robert Glasper (modern jazz with R&B and hip-hop)
- Snarky Puppy, Kamasi Washington (modern fusion-jazz revival)

## Latin jazz (c. 1947–onward)

Jazz fused with Cuban, Brazilian, and other Latin rhythms.

**Key features**:
- Afro-Cuban rhythms (clave, son montuno, mambo) for *Cubop* / Latin jazz
- Brazilian rhythms (samba, bossa nova) for *Brazilian jazz* / *bossa*
- Latin percussion (congas, bongos, claves, timbales)
- Jazz harmony over Latin grooves

**Players**:
- Dizzy Gillespie (*Cubop* pioneer with Chano Pozo)
- Stan Getz (*The Girl from Ipanema*, bossa nova)
- Antonio Carlos Jobim (composer of bossa nova standards)
- Tito Puente, Eddie Palmieri (salsa-jazz)
- Chick Corea (Latin influence in his music)
- Michel Camilo, Gonzalo Rubalcaba (modern Latin jazz)

## Modern jazz (post-2000)

Contemporary jazz draws on all the above and adds:
- Hip-hop influences (Robert Glasper, Esperanza Spalding)
- Electronic production (Mark Guiliana, James Francies)
- Metal and prog rock influences (Tigran Hamasyan)
- World music influences (Anouar Brahem, Avishai Cohen)
- Singer-songwriter sensibility (Norah Jones, Madeleine Peyroux)

## Idiomatic features across jazz styles

Some elements appear across most jazz styles:

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Swing eighth notes | Most jazz uses swung 8ths (60:40 to 67:33 ratio); fusion and Latin jazz often use straight 8ths |
| Walking bass | Quarter-note bass line through chord changes |
| Comping | Rhythmic chord accompaniment, especially by piano and guitar |
| Standard repertoire | Real Book / Great American Songbook tunes (Gershwin, Cole Porter, Rodgers, Kern, Berlin, Ellington, etc.) |
| Improvisation | Solo improvisation over the form is central |
| Trading | Soloists alternate (typically "trading 4s" — 4-bar exchanges) |
| Head-solos-head | Standard form: state melody, solos over changes, restate melody |

## Jazz harmony quick reference

(Full treatment in `harmony/jazz-harmony.md`.)

- ii-V-I is the central progression
- Tritone substitution (♭II7 for V7)
- Modal interchange (borrowing from parallel modes)
- Extended chord vocabulary (9, 11, 13, alterations)
- Chord-scale relationships (Dorian over m7, altered scale over V7alt, etc.)
- Coltrane changes (chromatic mediant cycle of major chords)

## Composing in a jazz style — practical guide

1. **Pick the substyle.** "Jazz" is too broad; bebop and smooth jazz are different worlds.
2. **Choose a form.** AABA (32-bar standard), 12-bar blues, 16-bar form, modal vamp. Match the substyle.
3. **Write a head.** Melodically interesting, harmonically connected to the changes.
4. **Voice the changes.** Use the appropriate jazz voicing style (drop 2 for guitar, rootless voicings for piano left hand, quartal for modal).
5. **Plan the arrangement.** Head – solo section (chord changes for soloists) – head out. For modal, indicate vamps and modes.
6. **Write idiomatically.** Bebop heads have specific contour profiles; modal heads have characteristic note emphasis.

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't conflate substyles.** "Jazz harmony" varies enormously by substyle. Bebop changes are different from modal vamps.
- **Don't ignore the rhythm section's role.** Jazz isn't melody-and-chords; it's a band's collective performance. Comping, walking, and groove matter.
- **Don't write fully-determined music.** Jazz is about what's left to the players. Over-arranging removes the soul.
- **Don't ignore the swing feel.** Most jazz is swung; writing it in straight 8ths produces something else (fusion, Latin, or non-jazz).
- **Don't use "smooth jazz" pejoratively.** It's a legitimate style with its own audience and craft.

## Quick decision matrix

| Want | Try |
|------|-----|
| Sophisticated, fast, virtuosic | Bebop |
| Lush, lyrical, arranged | Cool jazz |
| Bluesy, soulful, grooving | Hard bop |
| Static, atmospheric, modal | Modal jazz |
| Free, abstract, exploratory | Free jazz |
| Electric, rock-influenced, virtuosic | Fusion |
| Polished, accessible, melodic | Smooth jazz |
| Hip-hop / electronic blend | Modern jazz / acid jazz |
| Latin-inflected | Latin jazz, bossa nova, Cubop |
| Standard repertoire feel | Swing era big band or AABA standard |
| Modern crossover | Glasper, Snarky Puppy, Kamasi Washington style |
