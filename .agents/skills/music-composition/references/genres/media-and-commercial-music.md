# Media and Commercial Music

> **Snapshot note:** Last content review snapshot: 2026-04-26. Use this file for stable compositional conventions, but verify current artists, charts, platform norms, and scene-specific references with active web research. See `../research/web-music-trend-research.md` and `../research/reference-track-digging.md`.

This file covers composition for media outside film, TV, and games — primarily advertising, podcasts, branded content, corporate / explainer video, social media, trailers, and similar short-form commercial work. These are large industries with their own conventions, distinct from concert music or even film/TV scoring. The constraint is usually short duration, clear functional purpose, and tight client requirements.

This file complements `film-tv-scoring.md` (linear narrative scoring; closely related), `game-music.md` (interactive media), `../production-aware/pre-production-decisions.md` (decisions before composing). For specific musical genres a media piece might draw on (orchestral, electronic, folk, etc.), see the relevant `genres/*.md` file.

## When to consult this file

- Composing for advertising, commercials, brand identity
- Podcast theme and stinger composition
- Corporate / explainer / training video music
- Social media short-form content (TikTok hooks, Instagram reels, YouTube intros)
- Trailer music (film trailers, game trailers, app trailers)
- Production music libraries / stock music
- Promo music for radio, broadcast, streaming
- Branded content (sponsored video series, content marketing)

## What's distinctive about media music

Compared to film/TV scoring or concert music, media composition has these defining features:

| Feature | Implication |
|---------|-------------|
| Short duration (5–60 seconds typical) | The whole musical idea must land quickly; no long development |
| Functional / persuasive intent | Music serves a goal (sell, inform, brand, engage) — aesthetic value secondary |
| Tight client constraints | Briefs may specify mood, length, style, and required hits |
| Multiple deliverables | One piece often needs 60s, 30s, 15s, 6s versions; sometimes 5min full mix |
| Genre-fluent expectation | Composer must work in many idioms (orchestral, EDM, hip-hop, indie, folk) on demand |
| Quick turnaround | Days or hours, not weeks |
| Royalty / licensing structure | Different from film/TV; production music libraries are common |

## Advertising and commercial music

The largest category. Sub-types and their conventions:

### Brand identity / sonic logo

A 1–4 second musical signature for a brand. Examples: Intel "bong-bong-bong-bong" (5 notes), Netflix "ta-dum", McDonald's "ba-da-ba-ba-ba" (the "I'm Lovin' It" notes), THX deep note opener. These are intensively crafted and represent significant licensing budgets.

Compositional priorities:
- **Memorable from first listen** — the listener may hear this hundreds of times and must recognize it instantly each time
- **Distinct from competitors** — must not sound like another brand's signature
- **Adaptable** — the signature should work across contexts (TV, radio, web, in-store, on-hold, voice assistant)
- **Durable** — will be in use for years; must not feel "of its moment" too narrowly

Sonic logos are usually composed by sonic-branding specialists; budgets are substantial.

### TV / broadcast commercial (15s, 30s, 60s)

The classic format. Standard durations: 15 seconds, 30 seconds (most common), 60 seconds. Music is typically:

- **Underscore for VO**: bed under voice-over with controlled dynamic that doesn't overwhelm the words
- **Theme-driven**: a short identifiable theme that connects to the brand's emotional positioning
- **Genre-matched**: contemporary pop / hip-hop for younger demos; warmer / acoustic for family / lifestyle; orchestral for prestige (luxury, automotive, finance)
- **Climactic resolution**: builds to a moment of emotional payoff aligned with the brand reveal or call to action

Common structure for a 30-second spot:

| Time | Function |
|------|----------|
| 0:00–0:05 | Setup / attention grab; sparse music or sonic hook |
| 0:05–0:15 | Story development / VO; underscore (low dynamic) |
| 0:15–0:25 | Emotional turn / product reveal; music swells |
| 0:25–0:30 | Brand stamp / sonic logo; final hit / sustain |

### Online / digital advertising (varies, often 6s to 30s)

YouTube pre-roll, social ads, programmatic display. Increasing use of short-form formats:

- **6-second bumpers** (YouTube): the entire music idea has to land in 6 seconds. Often a single sonic gesture: a hook, a stinger, a sound-design swell.
- **15s and 30s variants**: similar to TV but optimized for muted-by-default playback (much social viewing has sound off until a click). Music plus visual must work together; music alone often can't carry the spot.
- **Auto-play with sound on tap**: music designed to "earn" the unmute — strong sonic hook in first second.

### Long-form branded content (1–10 minutes)

Brand documentaries, sponsored video series, "branded entertainment". Closer to film/TV scoring in length but with brand objectives in mind. Often original orchestral or hybrid scores.

## Podcast music

A growing area. Sub-types:

### Theme music (intro / outro)

The signature musical identity of a podcast. Typical: 15–60 seconds, distinctive instrument or sonic palette, identifiable melodic hook.

Compositional priorities:
- **Captures the show's tone** — comedic vs. serious, intimate vs. broadcast-style, niche vs. mass
- **Sets up the host's voice** — the music should leave a clean handoff to spoken word, often at a medium-low dynamic
- **Tolerates repetition** — listeners hear this every episode; it should reward repeated listening
- **Recognizable instantly** — listeners should know which podcast they're hearing within 2–3 seconds

### Bumpers and transitions

Short musical cues (2–10 seconds) used to mark section breaks within an episode. Often variations on the main theme; sometimes wholly different cues for specific recurring segments.

### Underscore (for narrative / interview podcasts)

Some podcasts (especially documentary-style) use underscore similarly to film. *Serial*, *This American Life*, *S-Town*, and similar narrative podcasts have substantial musical content.

Compositional priorities:
- **Subordinate to voice** — the listener is there for the words; music serves them
- **Atmospheric over melodic** — sustained pads, ambient textures, restrained motion
- **Emotionally specific** — music tells the listener how to feel about the moment without overstating

### Sponsored / advertising spots within podcasts

Mid-roll or pre-roll ads with their own music. Same conventions as broadcast advertising but compressed (often 30–90 seconds total ad with 10–20 seconds of musical content).

## Corporate / explainer / training video

A vast volume of content: product explainer videos, internal training, corporate communications, conference videos. Music typically:

- **Neutral-positive emotional palette** — uplifting without being saccharine, professional without being clinical
- **Genre-restrained** — usually contemporary pop-influenced or hybrid orchestral/electronic; rarely strongly genre-specific (no metal, no avant-garde)
- **VO-friendly** — designed to sit under voice-over comfortably
- **Long enough to cover full duration** — often 2–10 minutes; loops or extended composition

This category often uses production music libraries rather than custom composition. Custom composition for corporate work tends to be for higher-budget brand films, executive presentations, and major launch campaigns.

## Social media short-form

TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, Twitter/X video. Distinctive features:

- **Music is often pre-existing** (TikTok's library of licensed tracks is the music; users select from it) rather than composed for the post
- **For branded social content**: short hooks (3–15 seconds) that work as standalone musical ideas
- **Trend-responsive**: musical styles on social media shift quickly; what was a hit hook 3 months ago may feel dated
- **Often sample-based or AI-assisted** in indie / creator contexts

For the composer, social media is mostly a distribution channel for licensed or library music. Custom composition for social media is mostly for brands with their own sonic identity to maintain.

## Trailer music

Trailer music is its own micro-industry, distinct from film scoring (even though trailers exist alongside films).

### Why trailer music is different

Film scores are written *for* the film, generally after picture lock; trailers are typically cut *to* pre-existing music. The trailer editor selects from a music library, finds tracks that match the desired emotional arc, and cuts picture to fit the music's rises and drops.

Trailer music libraries (Two Steps from Hell, Audiomachine, Position Music, etc.) are entire businesses producing tracks specifically for trailer use — designed with trailer cutting in mind.

### Trailer music conventions

Trailer cues are highly conventional. Typical structures:

- **Build-up**: slow, atmospheric opening (10–30s)
- **First drop / hit**: a major impact moment with thematic statement
- **Continued build with thematic development**
- **Climax / final drop**: maximum intensity, full orchestration
- **Tag / resolution**: short conclusion, often softer

Common trailer music elements:
- "Big" orchestral percussion (taikos, large drums, stadium-percussion)
- Choral pads (often Latin or vocalese, no specific text)
- Synth bass / sub bass for impact
- Risers, sweeps, sound-design transitions
- Brass / horn calls for hero / heroic feel
- String ostinatos for tension

### Trailer hits

Short percussive / harmonic accents (impacts, "whooshes", boom hits) used at picture cuts. These are essentially sound design and are usually part of trailer music tracks rather than separate elements.

### Studying trailer music

The "Inception trailer" (2010) — using "Mind Heist" by Zack Hemsey — is widely cited as the moment trailer music conventions consolidated. Listening to many recent trailers and identifying the musical conventions is the best way to learn the idiom. The "BWAAAH" sound is now a trailer-music cliché — part of the genre vocabulary.

## Production music / library music

Pre-composed music licensed for media use. Composers write tracks for libraries rather than for specific projects; clients (filmmakers, ad agencies, corporate video producers) license tracks from the library.

### Library music conventions

- **Each track is generic enough to fit many uses** but specific enough to have a recognizable mood
- **Tracks often come in versions**: full mix, no melody (so VO can replace the lead), no drums, 60s/30s/15s edits, stinger/loop/stem packages
- **Genre / mood metadata** is essential for discoverability: tags like "uplifting corporate piano", "epic action cinematic", "indie folk acoustic", "dark electronic tension"
- **Royalty structure**: composers earn writer's share + (sometimes) publisher's share; libraries typically own / co-own the publishing
- **Volume composition**: library composers often write hundreds of tracks per year

This is a viable composition career path with its own craft — writing efficiently, hitting briefs precisely, producing professional masters quickly.

### Library music vs. custom composition

| Aspect | Library | Custom |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Cost | Lower (licensing fee) | Higher (composer fee + recording) |
| Specificity | Generic / multi-use | Tailored to project |
| Speed | Instant (license and use) | Days to weeks |
| Identity | Shared with other users of the same library | Unique to project |
| Best for | Lower-budget productions, "background" needs | Hero spots, signature campaigns, branded content |

## Composing for media — practical workflow

1. **Read the brief carefully.** Briefs usually specify: duration, emotional / mood targets, genre / style, reference tracks, deliverables (multiple lengths), deadline, technical specs (file format, sample rate, loudness target).
2. **Identify the function.** What does the music need to *do*? Set mood? Build to a CTA? Underscore VO? Each role has different conventions.
3. **Match the duration target precisely.** If the brief says 30 seconds, the piece is exactly 30 seconds. Not 28, not 33.
4. **Plan the emotional arc within the duration.** A 30-second piece has roughly 3–4 emotional beats; a 6-second bumper has 1–2.
5. **Identify "sync points"** — moments in the visuals or VO where music must land specifically (logo reveal, product shot, key word in the script).
6. **Compose with the cut in mind.** Music for media is designed to support cuts; don't ignore where the visual editor needs the music to breathe or hit.
7. **Deliver multiple versions when required.** A standard ad music delivery often includes: 60s, 30s, 15s, 6s, and stems (drums-only, music-only, etc.).
8. **Master to broadcast / streaming standards.** Different platforms have different loudness targets (LUFS); the brief usually specifies. Common targets: -23 LUFS for European broadcast (EBU R128), -24 LKFS for US TV (CALM Act), -16 to -14 LUFS for streaming services and YouTube, varies for social media.

## Working with clients

Media composition is intensely client-driven. A few patterns:

### Brief interpretation

Briefs use evocative language ("uplifting", "modern", "human", "cinematic") that's open to interpretation. The first draft is often "wrong" — the client knows what they don't want but couldn't say what they did want until they hear something. Multiple revisions are normal.

### Reference tracks

Clients often provide reference tracks ("we want it to feel like X"). Treat these as goals, not as templates. The actual composition should be inspired by but not derivative of the reference.

Watch out: some clients want you to *exactly* match a reference, which can risk copyright issues. Push back diplomatically: "We can capture this energy / feel without being legally exposed." See `../production-aware/pre-production-decisions.md` for reference-track best practices.

### Revisions

Revisions are part of the work. Build them into the timeline and contract. Common revision types:

- **Tonal**: "more uplifting", "less dark"
- **Structural**: "build the chorus earlier", "we need to extend before the logo"
- **Genre**: "less orchestral, more electronic"
- **Mix**: "lower the bass", "drum hits should be punchier"
- **Sync**: "the swell needs to land 2 seconds later"

Each kind of revision has different implications for cost and time. A "tonal" revision might be a melodic adjustment; a "genre" revision might be a complete redo.

### Approval and ownership

Before final delivery, confirm:
- Who has final approval (creative director, brand manager, client lead)?
- What's the deliverable specification (file format, length, masters needed)?
- What rights are being purchased (broadcast rights only, perpetual rights, exclusive vs. non-exclusive)?
- Are there multiple usage windows (initial campaign + longer-term licensing)?

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't ignore the brief.** Media composition is brief-driven. A piece that ignores the brief, no matter how musically excellent, is a failure.
- **Don't make the music more important than the message.** In ads, podcasts, corporate video, the music serves the communication. Distinctive music that overwhelms the script or visuals is a problem.
- **Don't ignore duration constraints.** A 30-second piece must be 30 seconds. Adjusting the visual to fit the music is rarely an option.
- **Don't compose without the sync points.** If a logo reveals at 28 seconds, the music must build to land at 28 seconds.
- **Don't underestimate revisions.** Plan for 2–5 revision rounds on most ad work.
- **Don't deliver poorly mastered files.** Broadcast and streaming have specific loudness targets; missing them creates extra work for the post-production team.
- **Don't ignore VO.** Most media music shares space with voice-over; the music must duck appropriately and leave frequency room for the voice.
- **Don't use copyrighted material as reference too literally.** "Make it sound exactly like Track X" risks legal issues. Capture the *feel* without copying the content.
- **Don't apply concert-music aesthetic priorities.** Commercial music is functional. The composer's pride is in the work serving the brief well, not in the abstract musical qualities.
- **Don't underprice your work.** Media composition has standard rates by category (broadcast TV ad, online video, corporate video, etc.). Know the going rates for your market.
- **Don't ignore loudness creep.** Mastering louder than the brief specifies to "make it sound bigger" causes broadcast / streaming compliance issues.

## Quick decision matrix

| Want | Try |
|------|-----|
| 30-second TV ad | Build through 3 emotional beats (setup / development / payoff); land brand stamp on final beat; underscore volume for VO |
| Podcast theme | 15–60s; distinctive sonic palette; clear handoff to spoken word; tolerates repetition |
| Sonic logo | 1–4 seconds; intensively memorable; distinct from competitors; works across delivery contexts |
| Corporate explainer | Neutral-positive palette; loops or extends to needed length; VO-friendly low dynamic |
| YouTube 6-second bumper | One sonic gesture / hook; lands instantly; "earns the unmute" |
| Trailer cue | Build-up + first drop + climactic drop + tag; cinematic orchestral or hybrid; lots of percussion / sound-design impact |
| Library track for "uplifting corporate" | Major key, mid-tempo (110–125 BPM), piano/strings/light electronic, lyric-free, easy-to-cut sections |
| Library track for "epic action cinematic" | Heavy percussion, brass, choral pads, building intensity, multi-section structure |
| Branded content longform | Closer to film scoring; full original score; brand emotional positioning shapes thematic palette |
| Social media custom | Short hook focused on instant recognition; plan for muted playback (visuals carry weight too) |
| Reference track says "feel like X" | Capture energy/mood; don't copy melodic content; check legal exposure |
| Multiple deliverables (60/30/15/6) | Compose the full piece first; create shorter versions by selecting strongest moments rather than time-compression |
