This file covers composition-facing conventions in Latin pop, reggaeton, dembow-derived pop, bachata-pop, urbano crossover, and adjacent Latin mainstream styles. It is **not** a complete guide to Latin American or Afro-Caribbean music. For tradition-specific work, study deeper sources and work with practitioners.

- The user wants Latin pop, reggaeton, urbano, dembow, bachata-pop, or Spanish-language pop energy

- The user asks for "current Latin" style; pair this file with active web research

## Core warning

Do not reduce Latin music to one rhythm. Reggaeton, bachata, salsa, cumbia, merengue, Latin trap, regional Mexican, Brazilian pop, and Latin rock are different traditions with different histories, grooves, instruments, and language conventions.

If the user says only "Latin feel," ask or infer a narrower frame:

- reggaeton / urbano
- Latin pop ballad
- bachata-pop
- salsa-influenced pop
- cumbia-influenced pop
- Latin trap
- regional Mexican / corridos tumbados influence
- Brazilian pop/funk/sertanejo axis, which deserves separate treatment

## Reggaeton / urbano composition priorities

Reggaeton is usually defined more by groove, vocal delivery, and production density than by complex chord progressions.

**Typical priorities**:

- 4/4 dance grid with dembow-derived groove
- repetitive, body-readable percussion pattern
- strong bass/kick relationship
- short, chantable hook phrases
- Spanish prosody and vowel clarity
- loop-based harmony, often two to four chords
- sparse verses and denser chorus/post-chorus
- call-response or crowd-response hook design

## Dembow-derived groove, composition view

Dembow is a family of related patterns, not a single immutable MIDI loop. A composition-facing shorthand:

```text
Feel: 4/4, tresillo / 3+3+2 energy inside a steady dance pulse
Core job: make the body know where the hook lands
```

Practical writing moves:

- Keep the vocal hook rhythm simple enough to sit on top of the groove.
- Let the bass reinforce the kick pattern; avoid busy harmonic bass motion.
- Use percussion fills to mark 4-bar and 8-bar phrase boundaries.
- Put hook words on open vowels and strong groove accents.
- If the verse is rhythmically dense, make the chorus more chant-like.

## Harmony

Latin pop and urbano harmony can be simple or lush. The groove often carries identity, so harmony should support rather than overcomplicate.

### Common loops

| Mood | Progression | Example in C / A minor |
|---|---|---|
| Bright pop | I-V-vi-IV | C-G-Am-F |
| Emotional minor-first | vi-IV-I-V | Am-F-C-G |
| Dark urbano | i-♭VI-♭III-♭VII | Am-F-C-G |
| Tense minor | i-♭VII-♭VI-V | Am-G-F-E |
| Romantic / bachata-pop | i-iv-♭VII-♭III | Am-Dm-G-C |
| Lift into chorus | iv-V-i or iv-VI-V | Dm-E-Am / Dm-F-E |

### Color tones

- add9 and sus chords keep loops modern without distracting from the groove
- minor 9 and maj7 can push toward R&B/Latin pop ballad
- dominant V in minor gives a more dramatic or classic pull
- guitar/keyboard montuno-like syncopation can imply harmony rhythmically

## Melody and vocals

Spanish-language hooks often benefit from vowel-forward writing and clear stress placement.

Useful moves:

- repeat a short hook cell with slight rhythmic variation
- leave space after the hook for percussion response
- alternate rap-like verse flow with sung chorus
- use melodic range sparingly in verse, then widen chorus or post-chorus
- build hooks around title repetition, call-response, or a memorable final vowel

Pair with `../fundamentals/prosody-and-language.md` for Spanish stress and syllable placement.

## Form

Common pop-urbano forms:

```text
Intro hook / tag → Verse → Pre or lift → Chorus → Post-chorus / dance hook → Verse 2 → Chorus → Outro tag
```

or:

```text
Intro → Chorus → Verse → Chorus → Feature / rap verse → Chorus → Outro
```

Many tracks reveal the groove and hook very early. If writing for streaming or short-form discovery, avoid long intros unless the texture itself is the hook.

## Arrangement

Common layers:

- drums: kick, snare/clap, dembow-derived percussion, hats/shakers
- bass: sub or electric/synth bass locked to kick
- harmonic bed: guitar, piano, synth pluck, pad, or sample-like loop
- lead vocal with doubles/ad-libs
- group chants or response vocals
- short ear-candy fills at phrase boundaries

The arrangement should make the dance pulse obvious even when the vocal is rhythmically dense.

## Bachata-pop crossover

Composition cues:

- guitar arpeggios or requinto-like figures
- romantic minor/major progressions
- syncopated percussion feel, often lighter than reggaeton
- vocal-forward topline with expressive ornaments
- duet or call-response format works well

Do not fake traditional bachata with a generic nylon guitar patch alone. The rhythmic guitar pattern and bass/percussion relationship are central.

## Salsa / cumbia / tropical pop influence

For salsa/cumbia-influenced pop, the rhythmic pattern and percussion architecture matter more than throwing in horns.

- Salsa influence: clave awareness, montuno-like piano/guitar patterns, horn responses, call-response vocals
- Cumbia influence: steady dance pulse, offbeat guitar/keyboard patterns, simple hook, accordion or synth-accordion color in some regions
- Merengue influence: fast duple energy, driving percussion, bright horn/synth hits

If the user wants authentic salsa/cumbia arrangement, this file is only a starting point.

## Latin trap / urbano crossover

Latin trap often blends trap drums and sub-bass with Spanish-language rap/sung flows.

Composition moves:

- half-time feel, 808 slides, dark minor loops
- sparse melody/chord sample or guitar loop
- melodic rap with Auto-Tune-like contour
- chorus may be more rhythmic than melodic
- leave room for ad-libs and vocal texture

## Quick recipes

### Reggaeton-pop chorus

```text
Key: A minor
Loop: | Am | F | C | G |
Topline: 3-note chant around ^5-^6-^5, answer phrase drops to ^3
Form: chorus hook by bar 1, post-chorus repeats title rhythm
Arrangement: dembow groove + sub + bright pluck + group vocal doubles
```

### Romantic Latin pop pre-chorus

```text
Key: C major / A minor
Loop: | Dm | E7 | Am | G |
Move: rising vocal sequence, then hold the last word over E7
Arrival: chorus starts on C or Am depending on brightness target
```

### Urbano bridge contrast

```text
Drop drums for 2 bars, keep filtered guitar/piano loop.
Use iv-V-i in minor, then bring dembow groove back on the chorus title.
```

## Common mistakes

- Calling any Spanish-language pop "reggaeton"
- Using dembow rhythm but writing a vocal that fights the groove
- Adding horns/guitar/accordion as surface markers without groove function
- Overcrowding the low end with bass, kick, and low piano
- Treating Latin America as one market or one rhythm
- Copying a current hit's hook rhythm too closely
