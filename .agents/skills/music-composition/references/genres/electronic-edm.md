# Electronic and EDM

This file covers the broad family of electronic and dance music — from early synthesizer pioneers to modern EDM, ambient, and experimental electronic music. The variety is enormous; this file maps the territory and identifies the key conventions of major subgenres.

For form (build, drop, breakdown), see `form/popular-song-forms.md`. For groove and rhythmic conventions, see `rhythm-groove/groove-and-feel.md`. For arrangement density, see `orchestration/arrangement-density.md`.

## When to consult this file

- The user is producing electronic/dance music
- Identifying conventions of a specific subgenre (house, techno, dubstep, trance, etc.)
- Understanding tempo, structural, and sonic conventions
- Cross-genre work involving electronic elements

## Electronic music — broad categories

Electronic music encompasses three rough categories:

1. **Dance music (EDM and predecessors)**: meant for the dancefloor. House, techno, trance, drum and bass, dubstep, etc.
2. **Listening / experimental electronic**: not primarily for dancing. Ambient, IDM, glitch, electro-acoustic, etc.
3. **Electronic-influenced popular music**: pop, rock, hip-hop with electronic elements. Synth-pop, electro-pop, electronic R&B, etc.

This file focuses primarily on (1), with sections on (2) and references to (3) elsewhere.

## Early electronic music (c. 1948–1975)

Before commercial EDM existed, electronic music was an experimental art form.

**Musique concrète (1948–onward)**: Pierre Schaeffer, GRM in Paris. Music made from manipulated recordings of real-world sounds.

**Elektronische Musik (1950s)**: Stockhausen, Cologne studio. Synthesized sounds from oscillators and tape manipulation.

**Tape music**: cut-and-splice composition with magnetic tape.

**Early synthesizers**: Moog, Buchla, Arp. Wendy Carlos's *Switched-On Bach* (1968) brought synthesizers to popular awareness.

**Krautrock (early 70s)**: Kraftwerk, Tangerine Dream, Klaus Schulze, Can. German rock with electronic instruments and ambient leanings.

**Prog electronic**: Vangelis, Jean-Michel Jarre. Electronic albums for listening.

## Disco (c. 1972–1980)

The first dance music with strong electronic influence. Club-driven, with DJs becoming central.

**Idiomatic features**:
- 4-on-the-floor kick
- 110–125 BPM
- Strings, horns, syncopated bass
- Prominent hi-hat 8ths or 16ths
- Singer-driven, often with backing vocal arrangements

**Artists**: Donna Summer, the Bee Gees, Chic, Earth Wind & Fire, Saturday Night Fever soundtrack.

Disco evolved into Italo disco, hi-NRG, and gave birth to house and freestyle.

## House (c. 1984–onward)

Born in Chicago (Frankie Knuckles, the Warehouse club). The foundational genre of modern dance music.

**Idiomatic features**:
- 4-on-the-floor kick
- 118–135 BPM
- Drum machine percussion (TR-808, TR-909)
- Soulful vocals (often gospel-derived)
- Funky bass lines (often stepwise or pentatonic)
- 32-bar phrasing
- Long DJ-friendly arrangements (6–8 minutes)

**Sub-traditions**:
- **Chicago house**: gritty, raw (Frankie Knuckles, Marshall Jefferson, Larry Heard)
- **Deep house**: jazzy, soulful, downtempo (Larry Heard "Mr. Fingers")
- **Acid house**: TB-303 squelch (Phuture's "Acid Tracks", classic London/Madchester scene)
- **Garage / UK garage**: swung 16ths, faster, often with R&B vocal samples
- **Tech house**: techno + house hybrid, minimal
- **Tropical house**: midtempo, summery, Kygo-style 2010s mainstream
- **Future house**: 2010s mainstream EDM-house hybrid

## Techno (c. 1985–onward)

Born in Detroit (Juan Atkins, Derrick May, Kevin Saunderson — "the Belleville Three"). More machine-focused than house.

**Idiomatic features**:
- 4-on-the-floor kick
- 120–140 BPM (some subgenres faster)
- Synthesized, futuristic textures
- Less vocal-driven than house
- Hypnotic, repetitive structures
- Sound design as composition

**Sub-traditions**:
- **Detroit techno**: original, soulful, Afrofuturist
- **Berlin techno**: minimal, dark, club-oriented (Hardwax, Berghain scene)
- **Minimal techno**: stripped-down, micro-focused (Richie Hawtin, Plastikman)
- **Industrial techno**: aggressive, distorted (Ancient Methods, Surgeon)
- **IDM-adjacent techno**: experimental, listening-focused

## Trance (c. 1992–onward)

Emerged from German techno scene. Melodic, euphoric, club-oriented.

**Idiomatic features**:
- 130–150 BPM (sometimes slower for vocal trance)
- 4-on-the-floor kick
- Long build-ups (32+ bars)
- Big, emotional drops with arpeggiated synths
- Melodic, often diatonic chord progressions (i-VI-III-VII or similar)
- Vocal trance subgenre features female vocals

**Artists**: Paul van Dyk, Tiësto (early), Armin van Buuren, Above & Beyond, ATB.

## Drum and bass / jungle (c. 1992–onward)

Fast breakbeat-driven UK dance music.

**Idiomatic features**:
- 160–180 BPM
- Sampled "Amen break" or similar breakbeats (often heavily sliced and rearranged)
- Sub-bass-heavy bass lines
- Dub and reggae influences
- Half-time feel often present (snare on 3 of half-time = 3 of full time)

**Sub-traditions**:
- **Jungle (early 90s)**: ragga vocals, dub influence, raw breaks
- **Drum and bass (mid 90s onward)**: more polished, often technical
- **Liquid funk / liquid drum and bass**: melodic, jazzy
- **Neurofunk**: dark, technical, sci-fi
- **Jump-up**: party-oriented, simple basslines
- **Dubstep precursors**: 2-step garage and breakbeat

## UK garage (c. 1995–onward)

UK speed garage, 2-step garage, and post-garage developments.

**Idiomatic features**:
- ~135–140 BPM
- Swung 16th-note hi-hats
- Skippy, syncopated drum patterns
- Sub-bass-heavy
- 2-step: emphasis on 1 and 3 with snare displacement

## Dubstep (c. 2003–onward)

Originated in UK (Croydon) from garage and dub influences. Later split into UK underground (still close to roots) and brostep (commercial American/EDM dubstep).

**Idiomatic features**:
- 138–142 BPM (felt as half-time)
- Snare on 3 (half-time feel)
- Sub-bass with "wobble" (LFO-modulated low-frequency synth bass)
- Sparse, often spacious arrangements (UK style)
- Modulated bass design as primary musical content
- "Drop" with intense bass design (especially brostep)

**Artists**: Skream, Benga, Kode9, Burial (UK underground); Skrillex, Excision (brostep).

## Future bass / chillstep / vaporwave (2010s)

A loose family of genres mixing dubstep, trap, and emotional/atmospheric production.

**Future bass features**:
- Pitched-up vocal samples
- Saw-wave lead synths with side-chained pumping
- Trap-influenced drums
- Heavy use of arpeggiators and pitch automation
- Emotional, often nostalgic feel

**Artists**: Flume, Cashmere Cat, ODESZA, San Holo.

## Trap (electronic, distinct from hip-hop trap)

Hip-hop trap (covered in `genres/hip-hop-rnb.md`) crossed into EDM around 2012–2014. EDM trap producers borrow trap drum patterns and 808s but apply them to instrumental dance tracks.

**Artists**: RL Grime, Baauer ("Harlem Shake"), Flosstradamus, Diplo (some work).

## Big room / EDM (mainstream, c. 2010–2018)

The mainstream EDM era. Massive festivals and stadium-sized tracks.

**Idiomatic features**:
- 128 BPM
- Big lead synths (often saw-wave)
- Long build-ups with snare rolls and risers
- Massive, often simple drops
- 4-on-the-floor kick

**Artists**: Avicii, Tiësto (later career), David Guetta, Calvin Harris, Hardwell, Martin Garrix, Zedd.

## Tech house, deep house, future house (modern mainstream)

After big room peaked, mainstream EDM shifted toward deeper, more grooveable styles.

**Tech house artists**: Fisher, Chris Lake, CamelPhat. 124–128 BPM. Minimal but groovy.

**Deep house in mainstream**: Disclosure ("Latch"), CamelPhat, MK. Vocal-driven, soulful.

**Future house artists**: Don Diablo, Tchami, Oliver Heldens. Hybrid of house and big room.

## Hardstyle, gabber, hardcore, frenchcore

Faster, harder dance subgenres. 150–250 BPM. Distorted kicks, aggressive sound design. Mostly Northern European in origin and audience.

## Ambient (c. 1978–onward)

Brian Eno coined "ambient" with *Music for Airports* (1978). Music designed to be listened to peripherally; atmosphere over rhythm.

**Idiomatic features**:
- Often beat-less or very slow rhythm
- Sustained pads, drones, evolving textures
- Long durations (10+ minute tracks common)
- Generative or process-based composition often
- Spatial design (reverb, delay, panning) as primary musical content

**Artists**: Brian Eno, Aphex Twin (Selected Ambient Works), Stars of the Lid, Tim Hecker, Grouper, William Basinski, Hiroshi Yoshimura.

**Sub-traditions**:
- **Dark ambient**: brooding, often with field recordings
- **Drone**: sustained, slowly-evolving
- **New age**: more melodic, often spiritual
- **Ambient techno**: ambient with techno-derived rhythmic elements
- **Lowercase / quiet music**: extremely soft, attention-demanding

## IDM (Intelligent Dance Music, c. 1992–onward)

A label coined for early Aphex Twin / Autechre — electronic music too complex/idiosyncratic for the dancefloor.

**Idiomatic features**:
- Complex rhythms (often non-quantized, polyrhythmic, or breakbeat-derived)
- Glitchy, cut-up sound design
- Melodic experimentation
- Listening rather than dancing focus

**Artists**: Aphex Twin, Autechre, Squarepusher, Boards of Canada, μ-Ziq, Plaid, Oneohtrix Point Never.

## Glitch / clicks-and-cuts

Electronic music using digital errors as compositional material.

**Artists**: Oval, Fennesz, Pole, Alva Noto, Ryoichi Kurokawa.

## Synthwave / retrowave (c. 2010–onward)

Nostalgia for 80s film soundtracks and synth-pop. Online, especially streaming-friendly.

**Idiomatic features**:
- 80s synth palette (Yamaha DX7, Juno, etc.)
- Driving 4-on-the-floor or breakbeat
- Major-key melodic content with reverb
- Cinematic feel

**Artists**: Kavinsky, Carpenter Brut, Mitch Murder, Power Glove.

## Vaporwave / mallsoft

Internet-born genre. Slowed-down 80s pop and muzak samples, ironic nostalgia.

**Artists**: Macintosh Plus, Saint Pepsi, James Ferraro.

## Production conventions for EDM

EDM is production-driven. Composition is inseparable from sound design and arrangement.

### Sound design

- **Synthesizers**: Serum, Massive, Sylenth (popular soft synths). Hardware: Moog, Roland, Korg.
- **Drum machines / samplers**: Maschine, MPC, Battery.
- **Sample libraries**: Splice, Loopmasters; large genre-specific sample packs.
- **Sound design as composition**: many EDM tracks' "main hook" is a sound, not a melody. The lead synth's timbre is the song's identity.

### DAW-centric workflow

- Most EDM is produced in DAWs (Ableton, FL Studio, Logic, Cubase).
- Arrangement is constructed visually; loops are built and combined.
- Side-chain compression on bass and pads (against kick) creates "pumping" — a signature EDM technique.
- Heavy use of automation (filter sweeps, reverb sends, etc.) for build-ups.

### Mixing for clubs vs. listening

- Club tracks are mixed for big systems with sub-bass — kicks need to thump, bass needs to be felt.
- Radio edits often have less low-end emphasis.
- Mastering levels are typically loud (heavy compression and limiting).

## EDM form and structure

Covered in `form/popular-song-forms.md`. Brief recap:

```
Intro (16-32 bars)
Build 1 (16 bars)
Drop 1 (16-32 bars)
Breakdown (16-32 bars)
Build 2 (16 bars)
Drop 2 (16-32 bars; often varied)
Outro (16 bars)
```

Long version: 5–7 minutes. Radio edit: 3–4 minutes. DJ-friendly intros and outros (long, beat-only) facilitate mixing.

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't generalize across EDM subgenres.** Trance is melodic; techno is hypnotic; dubstep is bass-driven; ambient is atmospheric. The sub-genre dictates everything.
- **Don't ignore tempo.** Each subgenre has a tempo range; deviating from it usually doesn't make sense within the genre.
- **Don't underrate sound design.** In EDM, the synth patches are the song's musical content as much as the notes.
- **Don't apply pop song-structure to all EDM.** A 7-minute techno track has different pacing than a pop song — a single idea sustained for many bars is intentional, not a flaw.
- **Don't forget the dancefloor function for dance subgenres.** Tracks for clubs serve a function (DJ mixing, dancing). Listening-focused EDM (IDM, ambient) has different priorities.
- **Don't ignore the cultural context.** EDM scenes (rave, club) have deep cultural histories; understanding the context illuminates the aesthetic.

## Quick decision matrix

| Want | Try |
|------|-----|
| Dancefloor classic | House, 124 BPM, 4-on-the-floor, soulful vocal, repetitive structure |
| Hypnotic, machine-driven | Techno, 128-130 BPM, minimal melodic content, emphasis on sound design |
| Euphoric, melodic dance | Trance, 138 BPM, big build-up, big melodic drop, arpeggios |
| Bass-heavy, half-time | Dubstep, 140 BPM, snare on 3, modulated sub-bass |
| Fast, breakbeat-driven | Drum and bass, 174 BPM, sampled breaks, sub-bass |
| Emotional, modern dance pop | Future bass, pitched-up vocal samples, side-chained saws, trap drums |
| Mainstream EDM festival | Big room, 128 BPM, simple drop, big lead synth |
| Ambient / atmospheric | Ambient, beat-less or slow, sustained pads, long form |
| Experimental / glitchy | IDM, complex rhythms, cut-up sound design |
| 80s nostalgia | Synthwave, 80s synth palette, 4-on-the-floor, reverb-heavy |
| Tech house / modern groove | 124-128 BPM, minimal, groovy bass, vocal samples |
