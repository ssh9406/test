Hip-hop and R&B are deeply interrelated traditions in African-American popular music — distinct genres but constantly cross-pollinating. This file covers both, their subgenres, harmonic and rhythmic conventions, and the elements that distinguish them from pop and rock.

## When to consult this file

- The user is writing in hip-hop or R&B
- Producing beats, vocal lines, or arrangements in these styles
- Understanding the rhythmic and harmonic foundations
- Cross-genre work involving these traditions

## Hip-hop — origins and elements

Hip-hop emerged in the South Bronx in the early 1970s as a culture with four elements: DJing, MCing (rapping), graffiti writing, and breakdancing. Musically, hip-hop is built on:

1. **The beat**: drum patterns + bass + samples and/or original instrumentation. The rhythmic and harmonic foundation.
2. **The vocal**: rapping (rhythmic speech to a beat) is the primary vocal mode; melodic singing increasingly common.
3. **The hook**: a memorable chorus or refrain, often sung or chanted.

Verses typically 16 bars; hooks 4–8 bars. See `form/popular-song-forms.md` for hip-hop form.

## Hip-hop subgenres

### Old school / golden age (c. 1979–1989)

- **Old school**: Sugar Hill Gang, Grandmaster Flash, Run-DMC, LL Cool J. Funk samples, drum machine beats, simple rhymes.
- **Golden age (late 80s)**: Public Enemy, Eric B. & Rakim, KRS-One, EPMD, Beastie Boys. More complex sampling, denser production.

**Idiomatic features**: 808 drum machine, James Brown samples, simple rhyme schemes, often minor-key minor-pentatonic samples.

### East Coast (c. 1985–onward)

Lyricism-focused, sample-based.

**Boom-bap (early 90s NY)**: Wu-Tang Clan, Nas, The Notorious B.I.G., Mobb Deep, A Tribe Called Quest, De La Soul.

**Idiomatic features**:
- Boom-bap drum pattern (kick on 1, snare on 2 and 3 — actually on 2 and 4 with heavy kick on 1 and lighter kick on the upbeat)
- Loose, behind-the-beat groove
- Jazz and soul samples
- Dense, multisyllabic rhyme schemes
- 4-bar loops repeated throughout

### West Coast (c. 1989–onward)

**G-funk**: Dr. Dre, Snoop Dogg, Warren G. Funk samples, talk-box, smoother feel than East Coast boom-bap.

**Idiomatic features**:
- Whining synths (Moog leads)
- Heavy bass
- Slower tempos than NY (~85-95 BPM)
- Minor key, often mode-mixed
- Talk-box and vocoder effects

### Southern hip-hop (c. 1990s–onward)

**Houston (Geto Boys, UGK)**, **Atlanta (Outkast, Goodie Mob)**, **Miami bass**, **New Orleans bounce** (Master P, Cash Money). Diverse but emphasizing groove and party feel.

### Trap (c. 2003–onward)

Originally from Atlanta. Now the dominant subgenre globally.

**Idiomatic features**:
- 808 sub-bass with sliding pitches
- 16th-note hi-hat patterns with rolls (32nd-note triplet rolls common)
- Snare on beat 3 (half-time feel)
- Tempos 130–160 BPM (felt as 65–80 in half-time)
- Minimalist, dark melodic content
- Triplet flow rapping (Migos popularized)
- Heavy use of Auto-Tune
- Sparse sound design with cinematic samples

**Artists**: T.I., Gucci Mane, Young Jeezy (originators); Migos, Future, Travis Scott, Drake (modern); 21 Savage, Lil Baby, Roddy Ricch.

### Drill (c. 2012–onward)

Originating in Chicago (Chief Keef), spreading to UK and global. Aggressive subgenre of trap.

**Idiomatic features**:
- Even more sub-bass-heavy
- Sliding 808s with detuning effects
- Tempos around 140 BPM (often half-time)
- Dark, minor-key minor melodic content
- Aggressive lyrics and vocal delivery

### Cloud rap / lo-fi hip-hop / chillhop

**Idiomatic features**:
- Atmospheric, hazy production
- Pitched-down vocals or instrumentals
- Lo-fi, vinyl-crackle textures
- Slower tempos, often 70–85 BPM
- Jazz-influenced harmony

**Artists**: Lil B (proto), Earl Sweatshirt, MF DOOM (lo-fi influences), J Dilla (influence), Madlib, Knxwledge.

### Conscious / political / indie hip-hop

Lyrics-focused, often less commercial in production. Run the Jewels, Kendrick Lamar (transcending categories), Killer Mike, Talib Kweli, Mos Def, and many indie artists.

### Mumble rap / melodic rap

A 2010s shift toward melodic, often Auto-Tuned vocal styles. Future, Lil Uzi Vert, Lil Wayne (precursor), Travis Scott, Post Malone.

The melodic-vs-rapping line has blurred — many modern artists move freely between rapping and singing within a single song.

## Hip-hop rhythmic conventions

### The boom-bap groove

Originally from sampled funk and soul drums. Characterized by:
- Kick on 1 (and possibly off-beats)
- Snare on 2 and 4 (the "bap")
- Hi-hats on 8th notes (or 16ths with selective accent)
- Slightly behind-the-beat feel ("MPC swing")

### The trap groove

- Kick on 1, with additional kicks on tresillo (3+3+2) positions
- Snare on 3 only (half-time feel)
- 16th hi-hats with rolls (32nd, even 64th notes during fills)
- Sub-bass following the kick or with sliding 808s

### The flow

Rappers' "flow" is their rhythmic style — how they place syllables against the beat. Different rappers have different flow signatures. Triplet flows, double-time flows, syncopated flows, on-the-beat flows all coexist.

### Tempo and feel

- Old school / boom-bap: 80–100 BPM
- West Coast G-funk: 85–95 BPM
- Trap: 130–160 BPM (felt as half-time, so 65–80)
- Drill: 140–150 BPM (half-time)
- Cloud rap: 70–85 BPM

## Hip-hop harmonic conventions

Hip-hop harmony is sample-driven and varies enormously:

- **Sample-based**: harmony comes from the source recording (jazz, soul, funk samples).
- **Loop-based**: a 4-bar loop with 1–4 chords, repeated.
- **Trap minimalism**: often a single chord or vamp; sometimes just a bass-and-melody motif with no harmonic motion.
- **Modal**: minor pentatonic, Aeolian, Phrygian (especially drill and dark trap).

Common harmonic patterns:
- i–♭VI–♭VII–v (Aeolian, common in trap)
- i–♭VI in vamp
- i–iv–V7 (with the V7 from harmonic minor)
- Phrygian: i–♭II–i (especially drill)
- Static modal vamps (one chord for entire sections)

## R&B — origins and traditions

R&B (rhythm and blues) developed in the 1940s–50s as African-American popular music, encompassing many styles:

- **Early R&B (40s-50s)**: Louis Jordan, Ruth Brown, Big Joe Turner. Dance-oriented blues with horn sections.
- **Doo-wop (50s-60s)**: vocal group harmony (The Drifters, The Platters, The Shirelles).
- **Soul (60s)**: Aretha Franklin, Otis Redding, Sam Cooke, Marvin Gaye, Stevie Wonder. Gospel-derived vocals over R&B grooves.
- **Funk (late 60s onward)**: James Brown (the originator), Sly & The Family Stone, Parliament-Funkadelic. Heavily syncopated, "on the one".
- **Disco (70s)**: dance-floor R&B with strings, "four-on-the-floor" kick.
- **Quiet storm / smooth R&B (70s-80s)**: Luther Vandross, Anita Baker, Sade. Lush, slow-tempo R&B.
- **New jack swing (late 80s-early 90s)**: Teddy Riley, Bobby Brown, Bell Biv DeVoe. R&B + hip-hop drum programming.
- **90s R&B**: Boyz II Men, Mariah Carey, TLC, Mary J. Blige, R. Kelly, Brandy. Layered vocals, swung 16ths, heavy production.
- **Neo-soul (late 90s-onward)**: D'Angelo, Erykah Badu, Lauryn Hill, Maxwell. Live instrumentation, jazz harmony, retro feel.
- **Modern R&B (2010s-onward)**: Frank Ocean, The Weeknd, SZA, Daniel Caesar, H.E.R., Brent Faiyaz. Genre-blending, often atmospheric.

## R&B harmonic conventions

R&B uses richer harmony than most pop or hip-hop:

- **Extended chords**: 9ths, 11ths, 13ths used liberally
- **Modal mixture**: borrowed iv (minor IV), ♭VII, ♭III, ♭VI in major keys
- **Secondary dominants**: especially V/ii (V7 of ii) for emotional lift
- **Tritone substitutions**: occasional, especially in neo-soul
- **ii-V-I**: extensive jazz harmonic vocabulary
- **Sus chords**: sus4 and sus2 for color
- **Slash chords**: V/3, IV/5, etc., for smooth bass motion
- **Two-chord vamps**: a single i-iv vamp can sustain an entire section

Neo-soul especially borrows jazz voicings — quartal voicings, rootless voicings, complex extensions.

## R&B rhythmic conventions

- **Swung 16ths**: most R&B (especially 90s and onward) uses swing on 16th notes.
- **Heavy syncopation**: vocal lines and rhythm parts heavily syncopated.
- **Ghost notes**: common in drums and bass.
- **Half-time choruses**: many modern R&B songs use half-time feel for sectional contrast.
- **Hip-hop drum programming**: post-90s R&B often uses drum machines and samples like hip-hop.

## R&B vocal conventions

R&B vocals are central. Conventions:

- **Melisma**: stretching one syllable across many notes. Heavy in 90s R&B and gospel-influenced styles.
- **Vocal runs**: rapid melodic improvisations, often at the ends of phrases.
- **Ad-libs**: vocal interjections in the second half of choruses or verses.
- **Vocal layering**: lead + multiple harmonies, doubles, ad-libs, often heavily produced.
- **Auto-Tune (modern)**: pitch correction or stylized auto-tune effects.
- **Whisper / falsetto / chest mix**: skilled R&B singers move freely between registers.

## Production conventions

Hip-hop and R&B production has evolved:

- **Sampling**: early hip-hop and R&B sampled extensively. Now constrained by copyright; many producers craft samples themselves.
- **Drum machines**: 808 (Roland TR-808), 909, MPC samplers — foundational tools.
- **Sub-bass**: low frequencies are central. Trap especially is bass-driven.
- **Side-chain compression**: pumping bass and pads against the kick.
- **Vocal processing**: extensive (compression, EQ, reverb, delay, Auto-Tune, doubling).
- **Beat construction**: typically built up — start with drums and bass, add melodic elements, add atmosphere and effects.

## Form conventions

### Hip-hop form

```
Intro (4-16 bars)
Verse 1 (16 bars)
Hook / Chorus (8 bars)
Verse 2 (16 bars)
Hook (8 bars)
Verse 3 / Bridge (8-16 bars)
Hook (8 bars)
Outro (variable)
```

Modern variations often shorter. Some songs are just 1-2 verses with multiple hook repetitions.

### R&B form

R&B usually follows pop verse-chorus form:

```
Intro
Verse 1 (8-16 bars)
Pre-Chorus (4-8 bars)
Chorus (8-16 bars)
Verse 2 (8-16 bars)
Pre-Chorus
Chorus
Bridge (8 bars, often modulated)
Final Chorus
Outro
```

Modern R&B often features extended bridges with vocal showcases.

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't conflate hip-hop and R&B.** They share roots but have different aesthetics, conventions, and audiences.
- **Don't ignore the beat as composition.** In hip-hop, the beat IS the song's compositional foundation. The vocal is layered on, but the beat carries the song.
- **Don't underestimate the lyric in hip-hop.** Lyric craft (rhyme schemes, wordplay, internal rhymes, double meanings) is the central artform. Generic lyrics fail.
- **Don't apply pop chorus logic to all hip-hop.** Many hip-hop songs don't have a traditional "chorus" — they have hooks that function differently.
- **Don't use only vintage R&B references.** Modern R&B (Frank Ocean, The Weeknd, SZA) sounds different from 90s R&B. Match the era.
- **Don't ignore the sub-bass.** Especially in trap, the sub-bass is the song's primary low-end content — the kick supports it, not vice versa.
- **Don't forget production.** Hip-hop and R&B are inseparable from production. The same song with different production is a different song.
