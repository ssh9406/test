# Modal Harmony

Modal harmony is what happens when music is organized around a tonal center but doesn't use functional dominant→tonic logic. Instead of leading-tone resolution and tritone gravity, modal music establishes its center through repetition, drone, characteristic notes, and a handful of mode-specific chord moves. It's a fundamentally different system from functional harmony — not a degraded version of it.

## When to consult this file

- The user describes music that "doesn't seem to land" or "hovers"
- Mixolydian rock, Dorian jazz, Phrygian flamenco, Lydian film music
- Any music in a church mode, with a drone, or with a static modal vamp
- Modal jazz (Miles Davis, Coltrane modal era)
- Folk traditions that don't use Western tonal harmony
- The difference between modal music and modal mixture

For functional harmony (and modal mixture as a coloring of functional harmony), see `functional-harmony.md` and `chromatic-harmony.md`. For the modes themselves as scales, see `fundamentals/pitch-intervals-scales.md` and `assets/modes-cheatsheet.md`.

## Modal vs. functional vs. modal mixture — three different things

It's easy to conflate these. They're distinct:

| Concept | What it means |
|---------|---------------|
| **Modal harmony** | Music organized around a tonal center without relying on functional dominant→tonic motion. It often uses non-Ionian modes, but Aeolian and some major-like modal contexts can also be modal when the center is established by repetition, drone, characteristic notes, or vamps rather than leading-tone cadence. |
| **Modal jazz** | A specific mid-20th-century jazz movement where each section uses a single mode for extended periods. Subset of modal harmony. |
| **Modal mixture** | Borrowing chords from a parallel mode while staying in functional harmony. Lives inside functional logic. |

A song can use modal mixture without being modal. A modal song doesn't necessarily use modal mixture (it doesn't borrow from anywhere — it's wholly in one mode).

## The seven diatonic modes

All built from the same set of pitches (the white keys of the piano) but starting on different notes. Each has a distinct character.

| Mode | Starts on | Formula (whole/half steps from tonic) | Character |
|------|-----------|---------------------------------------|-----------|
| Ionian | C | W W H W W W H | Major; functional default |
| Dorian | D | W H W W W H W | Minor with raised ^6; cool, jazzy |
| Phrygian | E | H W W W H W W | Minor with lowered ^2; dark, half-step-above-tonic color |
| Lydian | F | W W W H W W H | Major with raised ^4; floating, otherworldly |
| Mixolydian | G | W W H W W H W | Major with lowered ^7; rock, blues, Celtic |
| Aeolian | A | W H W W H W W | Natural minor; reflective |
| Locrian | B | H W W H W W W | Diminished tonic; rarely used as tonal center |

For any reference key (e.g., C as the tonic of every mode), the mode formulas in scale-degree terms:

| Mode (root C) | Notes | Distinguishing alterations |
|---------------|-------|----------------------------|
| C Ionian | C D E F G A B | (default major) |
| C Dorian | C D E♭ F G A B♭ | ♭3, ♭7, natural 6 |
| C Phrygian | C D♭ E♭ F G A♭ B♭ | ♭2, ♭3, ♭6, ♭7 |
| C Lydian | C D E F♯ G A B | ♯4 |
| C Mixolydian | C D E F G A B♭ | ♭7 |
| C Aeolian | C D E♭ F G A♭ B♭ | ♭3, ♭6, ♭7 (natural minor) |
| C Locrian | C D♭ E♭ F G♭ A♭ B♭ | ♭2, ♭3, ♭5, ♭6, ♭7 |

## Characteristic notes

The "characteristic note" of a mode is the scale degree that distinguishes it from the more familiar major or minor scale. Establishing the mode means *featuring* this note — placing it on strong beats, in the melody's contour, in the bass, or in characteristic chords.

| Mode | Characteristic note (vs. major/minor) | What it does |
|------|---------------------------------------|--------------|
| Dorian | natural ^6 (vs. minor's ♭6) | Brightens the minor scale |
| Phrygian | ♭2 | Flamenco-/Andalusian-adjacent half-step color |
| Lydian | ♯4 | Otherworldly lift |
| Mixolydian | ♭7 (vs. major's ^7) | Rock/blues/Celtic flavor; no leading tone |
| Aeolian | ♭6 + ♭7 (no leading tone) | Pure natural minor; modal/folk |

Without the characteristic note, the music will collapse perceptually into the closest major or minor mode. A Dorian piece without the natural 6 sounds like minor. A Mixolydian piece without the ♭7 sounds like major.

## How modal music establishes its tonal center (without a leading tone)

Functional harmony gives you the leading tone and the V→I cadence as your closure mechanism. Modal music has none of that — Mixolydian's ^7 is ♭7, Dorian's ^7 is ♭7, etc. So how does the listener know what the tonic is?

Several mechanisms, used singly or in combination:

1. **Drone or pedal tone.** A held bass note on the tonic (or tonic + fifth) anchors the mode. Common in folk, raga, certain rock (hurdy-gurdy traditions, Sabbath riffs).
2. **Repetition and metric placement.** The tonic falls on downbeats, opens phrases, ends them. Repetition builds expectation of return.
3. **Characteristic chord move.** Each mode has a signature chord move that confirms the modal center (see below).
4. **Modal vamps.** A short repeating chord progression that loops between the tonic and a characteristic chord. The tonic's primacy is established by repetition.
5. **Static harmony on the tonic chord.** Long stretches of unchanging harmony allow the melody to explore the mode without competing pulls.

## Characteristic chord moves per mode

Each mode has signature chord progressions that establish modal identity without functional drive.

### Dorian (natural ^6 in a minor frame)

The defining chord move: **`i – IV`** (because IV is *major* in Dorian — it contains the natural ^6).

In D Dorian: `Dm – G – Dm – G` is the prototypical Dorian vamp. The G chord (containing B, the natural 6 of D) confirms Dorian.

Other Dorian moves:
- `i – IV – ♭VII – i` (Dm – G – C – Dm)
- `i – ♭III – IV – ♭VI`
- "So What" by Miles Davis: pure D Dorian with `Dm7` as the home chord.

### Phrygian (♭2 in a minor frame)

The defining move: **`i – ♭II`** (because the ♭II is so distinctive). The bass moves down by half-step from the tonic.

In E Phrygian: `Em – F – Em – F` is the basic vamp. The F chord (containing the ♭2 of E) sets it up.

Phrygian dominant (♭3 raised — see below) is more common than pure Phrygian in Western music. The Andalusian cadence `i – ♭VII – ♭VI – V` lives at the boundary of Phrygian and Phrygian dominant.

### Lydian (♯4 in a major frame)

The defining move: **`I – II`** (because II is *major* in Lydian — it contains the ♯4).

In F Lydian: `F – G – F – G`. The G chord (containing B, the ♯4 of F) is what makes it Lydian instead of major.

Lydian gives a floating, "looking up" quality. Common in film (Williams' "E.T."), jazz (`Maj7♯11` chord), and some prog.

### Mixolydian (♭7 in a major frame)

The defining move: **`I – ♭VII`**.

In G Mixolydian: `G – F – G – F`. The F chord (containing the ♭7 of G) confirms Mixolydian.

This is the bedrock of rock — `D – C – G – D` (Mixolydian in D), Beatles' "Norwegian Wood" (Mixolydian-flavored), Stones' "Sympathy for the Devil", AC/DC, much of Led Zeppelin. Also Celtic music.

Other Mixolydian moves:
- `I – ♭VII – IV – I`
- `I – v – ♭VII – IV`

### Aeolian (natural minor — ♭3, ♭6, ♭7, no leading tone)

The defining move: **`i – ♭VII`** (similar to Mixolydian's I-♭VII but in a minor frame). Without a leading tone, Aeolian feels "modal minor" rather than functional minor.

In A Aeolian: `Am – G – Am – G`. Or the famous rock progression `Am – F – C – G` (i – ♭VI – ♭III – ♭VII).

Most "minor key rock" is Aeolian rather than functional minor. The progression `i – ♭VII – ♭VI – ♭VII` is ubiquitous.

### Locrian (diminished tonic)

Rarely used as a true tonal center. The tonic chord is diminished and unstable, so it's hard to feel as "home". When Locrian is used, it is usually a momentary color, a metal sonority, or a highly dissonant film/avant-garde device.

### Ionian (major) and "modal major"

Strictly, Ionian is just major and operates with full functional logic. But "modal major" is sometimes invoked to describe major-key music that avoids strong V→I cadences and leans on plagal motion (`I – IV – I – IV`) and parallel motion. Some folk and pop fits this description.

## Modal mixture vs. modal music

Modal mixture borrows chords from parallel modes while staying in functional harmony. It uses borrowed chords for color and resolves them functionally. The home key is unambiguous; the borrowing is local.

Modal music doesn't have a home key in the functional sense — it has a *modal center*. The mode is the system, not a borrowing from one.

A song that opens with `Cmaj7 – B♭maj7 – Cmaj7 – B♭maj7` (I and ♭VII alternating) is Mixolydian if it stays there. If the same chords show up in a song that's clearly in C major and resolves through `G7 – C` at cadences, the `B♭` is borrowed (modal mixture) and the song is functional.

## Modal jazz

A specific genre, distinct from "jazz with modal flavor". Modal jazz typically:

- Uses a single mode for extended sections (often 8 or 16 bars per mode)
- Replaces ii-V-I motion with static modal vamps
- Frees the soloist to explore one mode at length, building intensity through dynamic and rhythmic development rather than through chord-change navigation
- Was pioneered around 1959 with Miles Davis's *Kind of Blue*, John Coltrane's modal period (*Impressions*, *India*), and continued by McCoy Tyner, Herbie Hancock, and many others.

The Mode-of-the-moment in modal jazz is *the* harmony — there's no chord-scale mismatch because there's no chord change against which to be wrong.

Common modal jazz forms:

- **AABA with modal sections**: e.g., 16 bars D Dorian, 8 bars E♭ Dorian, 8 bars D Dorian.
- **Two-mode swap**: e.g., 8 bars D Dorian, 8 bars E♭ Dorian, repeat.
- **Single mode for the whole tune**: rare in jazz; common in raga-influenced jazz fusion.

Voicings in modal jazz are typically quartal (stacked 4ths), pentatonic-derived, or open with extensions emphasizing the characteristic note.

## Modal harmony in pop

Pop uses modes constantly without naming them as such:

- **Mixolydian**: blues-influenced rock and folk-rock — Beatles, Rolling Stones, much of 70s rock.
- **Dorian**: cool jazz-pop, certain Beatles songs ("Eleanor Rigby"), darker indie.
- **Aeolian**: most "minor key" pop and rock, especially since the 80s. The whole indie-rock/alt-rock world.
- **Phrygian**: occasional flamenco-adjacent or metal-adjacent moments. Less common in pop.
- **Lydian**: rare in pop verses; common as a brief uplift, often via a Lydian IV moment.

The dominant pop tonalities are Aeolian and Mixolydian-flavored major. Most pop today is closer to modal than to functional.

## Modal harmony in film and TV

Film composers reach for modes constantly:

- **Lydian**: wonder, magic, awe (Spielberg/Williams territory)
- **Mixolydian**: heroism, adventure
- **Dorian**: cool, urban, ambiguous
- **Phrygian**: severity, starkness, claustrophobic tension; avoid using it as shorthand for cultural otherness
- **Aeolian**: melancholy, loss
- **Locrian**: alien, deeply unsettling

Combined with characteristic instrumentation (high strings + woodwinds for Lydian, low strings + horns for Phrygian, etc.), modes give film composers an almost color-palette use of harmony.

## Special modal scales

Beyond the seven diatonic modes, several non-diatonic modes are common:

### Phrygian dominant

Phrygian with a *raised* 3rd. In E: E F G♯ A B C D. The 5th mode of harmonic minor.

Sounds: flamenco-adjacent, klezmer-adjacent, maqam-adjacent in equal temperament, and certain metal idioms. The combination of the ♭2 and the major 3rd creates a strongly marked augmented second (between F and G♯ here). Use the label as a pitch-color shortcut only, not as a claim that the music is authentically flamenco, klezmer, or maqam-based.

### Lydian dominant (Lydian ♭7)

Lydian with a lowered 7th. In F: F G A B C D E♭. The 4th mode of melodic minor.

Sounds: jazzy, "the bright dominant". Common over `V7♯11` chords. Steely Dan, fusion.

### Altered scale (super Locrian)

The 7th mode of melodic minor. All the alterations stacked: ♭2, ♭3, ♭4 (= M3 enharmonically), ♭5, ♭6, ♭7. Used over altered dominant chords (`7alt`).

### Hungarian / gypsy minor

Harmonic minor with a raised 4th. Strong augmented-2nd color; highly marked and dramatic.

### Melodic minor (jazz minor, ascending only)

Minor with raised 6 and 7. Often used in jazz over minor-major 7 chords. Each of its modes has a name and a use: Dorian ♭2, Lydian augmented, Lydian dominant, Mixolydian ♭6, Locrian ♮2, altered.

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't impose functional logic on modal music.** A Mixolydian tune doesn't need a leading tone "for resolution" — it resolves by other means. Suggesting one will destroy the modality.
- **Don't confuse modal mixture with modal music.** A pop song with a borrowed `iv` is functional with mixture. A pop song built on `i – ♭VII – ♭VI – ♭VII` is Aeolian.
- **Establish the mode early.** A modal piece needs the characteristic note in the first phrase; otherwise the listener defaults to major or minor and feels confused when the mode reasserts itself.
- **Use the characteristic chord moves.** Telling the user "write in Dorian" is vague. Telling them "alternate i and IV — your IV will be major because of the natural 6th" is actionable.
- **Don't overload on alterations.** A piece in pure Phrygian is rare and difficult; most "Phrygian" tracks use Phrygian color on top of an underlying major or minor system. Pick the level of modal commitment that fits.

## Quick decision matrix — mode by feeling

| Want | Try |
|------|-----|
| Cool, ambiguous, mature | Dorian |
| Dark half-step-above-tonic color, flamenco-adjacent or metal edge | Phrygian or Phrygian dominant |
| Magical, floating, wonder | Lydian |
| Rock, blues, anthemic without leading tone | Mixolydian |
| Reflective minor, folky | Aeolian |
| Alien, dread | Locrian |
| Klezmer-adjacent, flamenco-adjacent, or maqam-adjacent pitch color | Phrygian dominant, plus idiom-specific rhythm, ornament, phrasing, and listening study |
| Jazzy bright dominant | Lydian dominant |
| Tense, fully altered dominant | Altered scale |
