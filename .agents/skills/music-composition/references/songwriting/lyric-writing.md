# Lyric Writing

This file is about the craft of lyrics — what makes them work, what makes them fail, and how they interact with melody, rhythm, and the song's emotional architecture. It covers prosody, rhyme, imagery, structure, point of view, and the specific demands of lyrics for different genres.

For melody-as-vocal-line, see `topline-craft.md`. For the hook (which is partly lyrical), see `hooks-and-memorability.md`. For phrase structure (which constrains lyric phrasing), see `melody/phrase-structure.md`. For lyric writing in musical theatre context, see `genres/musical-theatre.md`.

## When to consult this file

- The user is writing lyrics or wants feedback on lyrics
- A song's words feel "off" or unmemorable
- Questions about prosody (matching syllables to melody)
- Rhyme scheme decisions
- Imagery, metaphor, specificity
- Point of view, narrator, address
- "I have a great melody but the lyrics aren't working"

## What lyrics do

Lyrics carry several burdens simultaneously:

1. **Communicate meaning** — what the song is "about"
2. **Provide rhythmic syllables** for the melody to ride
3. **Provide vowels and consonants** that create singable, memorable sound
4. **Express character or persona** — who is singing
5. **Create emotional arc** — words that intensify alongside the music

A great lyric does all five. A passable one does three. A failed one might do only one.

## The prosody problem

Prosody is the match between word stress and musical stress. English words have natural stress patterns ("ELephant" not "eleEPHant"; "comPUter" not "COMputer"). When lyrics are set to music, the strong beats of the melody should fall on the strong syllables of the words.

When prosody is wrong, the listener hears it immediately:

- Word stress on weak beats: sounds awkward, like the singer is mispronouncing.
- Polysyllabic word with stress mid-word landing on a weak beat: classic mismatch.
- Articles ("the", "a") falling on strong beats: rhythmic emphasis on grammar instead of meaning.

Examples of bad prosody:

- "I am DOWN" instead of "I AM down" — mismatch.
- "she SAID it WAS just a JOKE" with "was" on the strong beat — bad.

Fixes:

- Rewrite the lyric so the strong-beat syllables are the stressed ones.
- Adjust the melody's accent pattern to match the lyric.
- Change which note holds longer (longer notes feel emphasized).

Prosody is the most common technical lyric mistake. Good songwriters fix prosody constantly during writing.

## Imagery and specificity

The "show, don't tell" rule from prose applies doubly to lyrics. Generic emotional words ("I'm sad", "I love you", "we're forever") are forgettable; specific images and sensory detail are memorable.

Compare:

- Bad: "I miss you so much"
- Better: "Your coffee cup is still on the kitchen table"
- Bad: "I feel free"
- Better: "Riding shotgun with the windows down on the way to nowhere"

Specificity creates the mental image. The listener fills in their own version. The general statement ("I miss you") doesn't conjure anything.

### When to be general

Choruses can be more general than verses — the chorus is the universal statement, the verses are the specific story. "Don't Stop Believin'" — verses describe a small-town girl and a city boy; the chorus is universal advice.

But even chorus lyrics benefit from imagery. "Bohemian Rhapsody"'s "Mama, just killed a man" is visceral; "Bohemian Rhapsody"'s lyrics generally are filled with strange specifics that make it unforgettable.

### Sensory detail

Lyrics work when they evoke senses:

- Sight: colors, light, motion, place
- Sound: voice, weather, environment
- Touch: skin, fabric, temperature
- Smell, taste: less common but powerful when used

A song with no sensory imagery is purely conceptual; the listener doesn't "see" anything.

## Rhyme

Rhyme is a tool, not a requirement. Used well, it provides structure and pleasure; used badly, it hijacks the lyric's meaning.

### Types of rhyme

| Type | Example | Effect |
|------|---------|--------|
| Perfect rhyme | cat / hat | Strong, classical, satisfying |
| Slant / near rhyme | mind / time | Subtle, conversational, modern |
| Internal rhyme | "I tried to ride the tide" | Within a line; adds density |
| Multisyllabic rhyme | "complicated / animated" | More elaborate; common in hip-hop |
| Eye rhyme | love / move | Visually rhymes but doesn't sound rhyme — usually a mistake |
| Mosaic rhyme | "tomorrow" / "borrow" — multi-word | Clever; common in patter songs and rap |
| Identity rhyme | bear / bear (same word) | Usually weak; sometimes deliberate for emphasis |

### Rhyme schemes

Common patterns:

- **AABB**: couplet rhyme. Simple, often country/folk feel.
- **ABAB**: alternating. Standard for verses; gives forward momentum.
- **ABBA**: enclosed. Less common; feels reflective.
- **AAAA**: monorhyme. Dense; common in hip-hop and certain blues.
- **No rhyme**: free verse. More common in modern indie, rap (less rhymed), confessional songwriting.

### When rhyme hurts

- **Forced rhyme**: changing the lyric's meaning to fit a rhyme. The listener notices when "moon" appears just because "soon" rhymed.
- **Predictable rhyme**: easy rhymes (heart/start, time/mine, love/above) feel cliché.
- **Rhyme over sense**: prioritizing rhyme over what you're trying to say.

Sondheim's principle: never sacrifice meaning for rhyme. If you can't find a rhyme that maintains the lyric's logic, rewrite the line or use slant rhyme.

### Modern rhyme conventions

Modern pop and indie often use slant rhyme exclusively. The classical "perfect rhyme = good" rule is loosened. Slant rhyme sounds more conversational and less songy.

Hip-hop has the most elaborate rhyme conventions in popular music — multisyllabic rhymes, internal rhymes, complex schemes. See `genres/hip-hop-rnb.md`.

## Point of view and address

Who is the speaker? Who are they speaking to?

| POV | Example | Use |
|-----|---------|-----|
| First person ("I") | "I will always love you" | Personal, intimate; the default |
| Second person ("you") | "You make me feel like a natural woman" | Direct address; speaks to listener as if to a partner |
| Third person ("he/she/they") | "Eleanor Rigby picks up the rice" | Narrative; observational |
| Mixed POV | "I see you" + "She didn't know" | Storytelling with multiple characters |

The choice of POV shapes the song's relationship to the listener. First-person draws the listener in; third-person creates narrative distance.

### Direct address

Many great lyrics speak directly to a "you" who is implicitly the listener (or an absent person).

- "Lay, Lady, Lay" — Dylan addresses his lover.
- "Sound of Silence" — Simon addresses an abstraction.
- Most love songs are direct address.

This creates intimacy. The listener identifies with either the "I" or the addressed "you" — sometimes both.

### Persona and unreliable narrator

Lyrics don't have to be sincere expressions of the writer's feelings. The narrator can be a character:

- "Stan" (Eminem) — the narrator is a deranged fan.
- "Hotel California" (Eagles) — the narrator is in an allegorical situation.
- Many country and storytelling songs use first-person narrators who aren't the writer.

This freedom lets songwriters explore territory they couldn't approach as themselves.

## Structure across the song

Each section of a song has different lyric demands.

### Verse lyrics

- **Different content each verse**: each verse usually has different lyrics (advancing the story or providing different angles).
- **Specific, narrative**: where imagery and detail live.
- **Rhythmically denser** than chorus: more syllables per bar.
- **Usually a build** within the verse: opening lines are quieter, later lines build toward the pre-chorus or chorus.

### Pre-chorus lyrics

- **Bridges verse and chorus**: lyrically transitional.
- **Often shorter, more intense**: "I keep going / I keep falling / I keep losing"
- **Usually leads to a question, declaration, or readiness statement**: "and now I know..." "and so..."

### Chorus lyrics

- **Repeated each time**: usually identical.
- **The song's main statement**: contains the title and central message.
- **Universal rather than specific**: applies to many situations, not just one.
- **Sing-able**: vowels that resonate; consonants that don't trip the singer.
- **Title hook**: typically the title appears in the chorus, often at the start or end of the chorus phrase.

### Bridge lyrics

- **New perspective**: a different angle on the song's theme.
- **Often a turn**: realization, decision, change of heart.
- **Builds back into the final chorus**.

### Outro lyrics

- **Resolution or open-ended**: depends on song.
- **Often a tag**: short repeated phrase that fades.

## The title and hook

The title is usually the most-repeated phrase in the song, almost always in the chorus. Where it appears matters:

- **Title at start of chorus**: makes it the launching point. "I Want to Hold Your Hand" — title is the hook line.
- **Title at end of chorus**: makes it the punchline. "She Loves You" — closes the chorus phrase.
- **Title throughout chorus**: maximally memorable. "Yeah Yeah Yeah" — title and chorus are nearly identical.
- **Title in pre-chorus or bridge**: less common; usually a deliberate choice.

A song without its title in the chorus is rare and usually a deliberate stylistic choice.

## Common lyric techniques

### Anaphora (repeated opening)

Multiple lines starting with the same words. "Don't stop / Don't stop believing / Don't stop holding on..."

Effect: incantatory, building. Common in choruses and bridges.

### Epistrophe (repeated ending)

Multiple lines ending with the same words. "I would do anything for love / I would do anything for love..."

### Listing

A series of items, often with a refrain. "Cigars, cigarettes, ricky-ticky-stickies..." (silly) or "I think I'll go to Boston / I think I'll start a new life..." (meditative).

### Repetition with variation

Same line returned with one word changed. "I love you in a different way / I love you in a different way today."

### Metaphor and simile

Turning the abstract into the concrete. "My heart is a record on repeat" instead of "I keep thinking about you".

### Negation

Saying what something isn't can be more powerful than what it is. "She wasn't crying when she said goodbye" implies all the unspoken.

## Genre-specific lyric conventions

### Pop

- Direct, accessible, universal.
- Strong title hook in chorus.
- Verse lyrics tell a story or set up the chorus's emotional payoff.
- Slant rhyme common.

### Country

- Story-focused. Specific narrative detail.
- Strong characters (often with names).
- Rural or working-class settings common.
- Concrete, sensory imagery.
- Title hook at end of chorus phrases.

### R&B

- Emotional, often romantic or sensual.
- Vocal melisma carries some "lyric" via vocal expression.
- Direct address to a romantic partner common.
- Confessional, vulnerable.

### Hip-hop

- Highest-density wordplay in popular music.
- Multisyllabic and internal rhymes.
- Storytelling, autobiography, social commentary, boasting.
- Specific cultural and personal references.
- Verse lyrics dominate; choruses often shorter and simpler.

### Indie / folk

- Conversational tone.
- Often confessional or observational.
- Minimal rhyme, slant rhyme common.
- Specific imagery and detail.
- Less reliance on commercial title-hook structure.

### Rock

- Wide range from confessional ballad to bombastic anthem.
- Often uses imagery and metaphor more than direct statement.
- Can be political, personal, fantasy.

### EDM

- Often minimal lyrics. Verses and choruses are simpler.
- Hooks are often vowel-heavy phrases ("oh oh oh", melodic interjections).
- Drop sections are often instrumental or have short vocal samples.

### Musical theatre

- Lyrics serve dramatic function (see `genres/musical-theatre.md`).
- Specific, character-revealing.
- Higher tolerance for elaborate rhyme and complexity.
- Sondheim is the gold standard.

### K-pop / J-pop

- Often mixes languages (Korean/Japanese with English phrases).
- Vibrant imagery, often abstract.
- Multiple sections may have different lyrical focuses.
- Choruses often have catchy English phrases as global hooks.

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't ignore prosody.** A beautiful line that doesn't sing well is a problem. Test by singing the lyric to the melody.
- **Don't write generalities when specifics would work.** "I'm sad" tells; "the rain matches my mood" shows.
- **Don't force rhyme.** A clunky rhyme is worse than slant rhyme or no rhyme. The meaning matters more.
- **Don't forget the singer's vowels.** High belted notes are easier on open vowels (ah, oh, ee). Sustained notes on closed consonant clusters (-st, -nch) are awkward.
- **Don't write lyrics without considering rhythm.** Lyrics have rhythm even before they have melody. The rhythmic pattern of the syllables shapes the song.
- **Don't ignore the title.** A song without a clear title hook is hard to remember and hard to market.
- **Don't write didactic lyrics.** A song that lectures the listener is preachy. Imply rather than declare.
- **Don't ignore the listener's experience.** Lyrics that are private to the writer (in-jokes, references only the writer understands) alienate the audience. Universal hooks let the listener in.

## A workflow for lyric writing

If the user is writing lyrics from scratch:

1. **Find the central image or feeling.** What's the song actually about? In one sentence.
2. **Generate the title or hook.** A specific phrase that captures the central idea.
3. **Sketch the chorus.** Often 2–4 lines that include the title. Don't overthink it.
4. **Write verses that earn the chorus.** Each verse should set up the chorus emotionally — what situation makes the chorus statement true?
5. **Test prosody.** Sing the lyric to the melody; if anything sounds awkward, adjust.
6. **Polish for specifics.** Replace generalities with sensory details. "I'm tired" → "I haven't slept since Tuesday".
7. **Read the lyric aloud, no melody.** Does it make sense as a piece of writing? If not, fix.
8. **Test for cliché.** Replace anything that's been heard a hundred times.

## Quick decision matrix

| Want | Try |
|------|-----|
| Pop accessibility | Direct address ("you"), simple verse imagery, repeated chorus title hook |
| Country narrative | Story with named character, rural setting, sensory specifics |
| R&B intimacy | First-person, sensual or confessional, simple rhyme |
| Hip-hop density | Multisyllabic and internal rhyme; specific cultural references; long verses |
| Indie sincerity | Conversational, slant rhyme, specific imagery, minimal cliché |
| Musical theatre dramatic | Character-revealing, dramatic function, internal rhyme |
| EDM hook | Short vowel-heavy phrase, repeated, sometimes wordless |
| Anthem | Universal "we" or direct "you", build to title hook |
| Storytelling ballad | Linear narrative, specific details, named characters, third-person or first-person retrospective |
| Confessional | First-person, present-tense, vulnerable, specific |
