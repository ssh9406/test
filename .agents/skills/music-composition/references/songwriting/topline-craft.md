# Topline Craft

In modern songwriting (especially in pop, hip-hop, and EDM production), "topline" refers to the vocal melody and lyrics that sit on top of an instrumental track. A *topliner* is a songwriter who specializes in writing vocal melodies and lyrics, often working with producers who supply the instrumental.

This file covers the craft of topline writing: matching melody to lyric, designing vocal lines for memorability, constructing verses and choruses that build effectively, and the specific demands of writing for different genres and singers.

For lyric writing in detail, see `lyric-writing.md`. For hooks specifically, see `hooks-and-memorability.md`. For melodic construction at a technical level, see `melody/melodic-construction.md`. For phrase structure, see `melody/phrase-structure.md`. For vocal range, see `orchestration/instruments-ranges-character.md`.

## When to consult this file

- The user is writing vocal melody and lyrics over an instrumental
- "Topline" specifically (collaborative pop/EDM songwriting)
- Designing vocal lines for radio
- Working with a singer's range and ability
- A vocal melody that "doesn't go anywhere"
- The relationship between lyric prosody and melodic shape

## What topline craft involves

A topliner makes choices about:

1. **Melody**: pitch contour, rhythm, range, climax position
2. **Lyric**: words, prosody, image, story
3. **Phrasing**: how the line breathes, where it lands
4. **Vocal performance**: ad-libs, melisma, runs, character
5. **Sectional contrast**: how each section sounds different
6. **Hooks**: where the most memorable moments are placed

A great topline isn't just a "melody plus lyrics" — it's an integrated vocal performance designed for memorability and emotional impact.

## The relationship between topline and track

A topline is written *over* an instrumental track. The track imposes constraints:

- **Key and tempo** are usually set by the track.
- **Chord progression** dictates which notes will sound consonant or tense.
- **Rhythm** of the track (groove, syncopation) interacts with the vocal rhythm.
- **Production density** affects where the vocal can live (bright synths above, deep bass below — vocal sits in the mid).

A great topliner adapts to the track. A mediocre one writes a vocal that ignores the track and "fits" only by accident.

### Working with a producer

In a typical session:

1. **Producer plays a track** (often called a "beat" or "instrumental").
2. **Topliner listens** and feels the vibe — is it slow and emotional, or fast and energetic?
3. **Topliner improvises** — humming melodies, mumbling syllables, finding what fits.
4. **Topliner refines** — choosing the strongest melodic ideas, writing real lyrics.
5. **Recording** — the topline is recorded, often with the topliner as the demo singer.
6. **Pitching to artist** — the demo is pitched to a recording artist who may record the song.

The topliner's job is to write something that sounds like the artist (or the kind of artist who would sing it) and is irresistible to record.

## Melodic strategies for topline

### Range zones for sections

A standard pop topline uses different range zones for different sections:

| Section | Tessitura | Why |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| Verse | Low chest / low mix | Conversational, intimate |
| Pre-chorus | Mid mix, climbing | Building to the chorus |
| Chorus | Upper mix or belt | Anthemic, lifted |
| Post-chorus | Belt or wordless | Climactic peak |
| Bridge | Variable; often higher than verse | Contrast, intensity |
| Final chorus | Highest of all | Maximum payoff |

The verse-to-chorus lift is the primary mechanism of pop dynamics. A topline that lives at the same range throughout doesn't deliver the chorus as a destination.

### Verse strategies

The verse should:

- **Establish the song's character** through tone, vocabulary, and melodic identity.
- **Tell the story** — set up situation, character, feeling.
- **Build toward the chorus** — energy increases through the verse.
- **Stay singable but not too memorable** — the verse should serve the chorus, not compete with it.

Common verse melodic patterns:

- **Repetitive within the verse**: same melody for each line, like a chant. Lets the lyric carry the variation.
- **Building**: each line a step higher than the last. Creates motion toward the pre-chorus.
- **Conversational with rhythmic variation**: speech-like rhythm with melodic interest.

### Pre-chorus strategies

The pre-chorus exists to make the chorus land. It typically:

- **Lifts tessitura**: starts higher than the verse.
- **Increases rhythmic density**: more syllables, faster delivery.
- **Modulates harmonically**: chord progression moves toward V of the chorus key.
- **Climbs in steps**: multiple short phrases, each on a higher pitch, anaphoric.
- **Ends on V**: setting up the chorus's I.

A weak pre-chorus is one that's just "verse-extension". The pre-chorus needs distinct character.

### Chorus strategies

The chorus is the destination:

- **Highest tessitura** of the song so far.
- **Strongest hook** (lyrical, melodic, or both).
- **Repetition**: the title phrase repeats, often anchoring the chorus.
- **Open vowels** on sustained notes for sing-along ease.
- **Repeating melody** for memorability.
- **Distinct rhythmic identity** from the verse.

Common chorus structures:

- **Statement and repeat**: same melody/lyric phrase twice, with slight variation second time
- **Statement-response**: A line, then a "response" line, often by backing vocals
- **Build-and-peak**: melody builds within the chorus to a climactic note in the second half

### Post-chorus strategies

The post-chorus is a relatively new (2010s onward) addition that became dominant in modern pop. It typically:

- **Wordless or minimal lyric**: "oh-oh-oh", "ah-ah-ah"
- **Melodic hook**: a distinct melody from the chorus
- **Production peak**: drums/synths at maximum
- **Sing-along** quality: simpler than the chorus, easier to repeat

The post-chorus often becomes the song's most-remembered hook.

### Bridge strategies

The bridge provides contrast:

- **New harmonic area**: often modulates to relative minor, parallel minor, or distant key.
- **New melodic identity**: completely different shape from verse and chorus.
- **Often more intense or more reflective**: depending on song.
- **Builds back to the final chorus**: the last line of the bridge often functions as a launching point for the chorus return.

A bridge that's too similar to verse or chorus is wasted real estate. The bridge should feel like the song "looking sideways" before resolving.

## Prosody — the topline-specific concern

Prosody (matching word stress to musical stress) is central to topline craft. A topliner must:

- **Stress strong beats with stressed syllables**.
- **Avoid filler words on strong beats** ("the", "a", "and").
- **Match polysyllabic words** to multi-note phrases that respect the word's stress pattern.
- **Use vowel choices** to optimize sustained notes.

When prosody is right, the topline sounds inevitable. When wrong, it sounds awkward.

### Vowel choices

Sustained notes are easier on open vowels:

| Vowel | Best for |
|-------|----------|
| Ah, Aw | High belted notes; full resonance |
| Eh, Ay | Mid-range notes; bright |
| Oh, Oo | Lower or rounded notes; warm |
| Ee, Ih | Brighter, more piercing in high register |

A high belt on "ee" can be brilliant; a high belt on "ck" is impossible. The topliner shapes the lyric so that strong, sustained notes fall on suitable vowels.

### Consonants and articulation

- **Hard consonants** (k, t, p, b, d, g) can clip the start of a sustained note.
- **Sibilant consonants** (s, sh, z) can be problematic in the mix.
- **Soft consonants** (l, m, n, w, y, r) flow smoothly.
- **Plosive at the very start of a phrase** is usually fine and adds rhythmic punch.

A topliner thinks about consonant clusters when writing — "stretched" doesn't sing as well as "reached".

## Vocal melisma and runs

Melisma is multiple notes on a single syllable. Common in:

- R&B (extensively)
- Gospel
- Some pop (Mariah Carey, Whitney Houston)
- K-pop ballads
- Broadway

When writing toplines for genres that feature melisma, leaving room for runs is part of the craft. A topliner might write the basic melody but indicate "ad-lib" on certain notes, leaving the singer to embellish.

Conversely, in genres without much melisma (most rock, country, indie), runs feel out of place.

## Writing for a specific singer

If the topliner knows the artist who will record the song, choices follow:

- **Range**: the artist's comfortable range (not pushing into strain).
- **Vocal character**: the artist's distinctive sound — raspy, smooth, ethereal, etc.
- **Style preferences**: belted vs. mixed; melismatic vs. clean.
- **Lyric voice**: what kinds of words the artist is comfortable singing.

A topline written for Adele (rich, low-mid alto belter) is very different from one for Ariana Grande (high agile soprano with whistle register).

## Modern pop topline conventions

### Verse-chorus-postchorus structure

The dominant template since ~2015. Each section has its own hook.

### Vocal layering

Modern toplines are often heavily layered:

- Lead vocal
- Doubled lead (often pitched up an octave or down)
- Harmony vocals (3rd or 5th above)
- Ad-lib vocals (improvised flourishes)
- Stacked "oohs" and "aahs" as pads

The topliner often demos all these layers to show the producer the full vocal vision.

### Melodic minimalism

Modern toplines often use repetition and minimalism — the same 2- or 3-note motif repeated with slight variations. Less melodic content per bar than older pop.

This compensates for production density: when the track is busy, a busy vocal would compete; a sparse vocal floats.

### Dropouts

The vocal pulls out for a bar or two at structural moments — the "and then nothing" before the chorus, or a held note while the rhythm section solos.

## Genre conventions

### Pop topline

- Verse-chorus-postchorus
- Hook-forward
- Title in chorus
- Vocal layering
- Slight melismatic flourishes

### Hip-hop topline (for sung hooks)

- Often shorter than full pop choruses
- Melodic but rhythmically strong
- Catchy and singable as the contrast to the rapped verses
- Featured vocalist often handles the hook (e.g., R&B singer for rap song's chorus)

### EDM topline

- Often minimal lyrics
- Hook-driven, often wordless or short phrase
- Vocal sits in the breakdown; drop is instrumental
- Vocoded, autotuned, or processed vocals common

### R&B topline

- Heavy melisma
- Improvisational feel
- Vocal phrasing more conversational and less metric
- Multiple ad-libs throughout

### Country topline

- Storytelling verses
- Title at end of chorus phrase
- Conversational delivery
- Less melisma; cleaner melodic lines
- Authentic vocal character (less processed)

### K-pop topline

- Multiple hooks per song
- Mix of singing and rapping (depending on group)
- Belted choruses
- Often heavily layered

### Folk / singer-songwriter

- Less commercial structure
- Conversational melody
- Lyrics drive everything
- Minimal vocal layering

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't write toplines without listening to the track.** The vocal must match the track's energy, key, and groove.
- **Don't write a chorus that doesn't lift from the verse.** If the chorus has the same range, dynamics, and melodic identity as the verse, the listener won't perceive it as a chorus.
- **Don't write impossible vocal lines.** Demands that exceed normal vocal range (especially for a 4-minute pop song) won't make it past the demo stage.
- **Don't forget about prosody.** Singing the line out loud reveals problems that look fine on paper.
- **Don't ignore the track's rhythm.** The topline rhythm should complement the track — not fight it, not blandly match it. Find the rhythmic conversation.
- **Don't write generic toplines.** Distinctive melodic and rhythmic identity is what gets the song chosen by an artist.
- **Don't over-melismatic in genres that don't want it.** Ariana Grande can run; Bob Dylan can't and shouldn't.

## A workflow for topline writing

If the user is writing a topline over an instrumental:

1. **Listen to the track multiple times.** Internalize its key, tempo, groove, harmonic arc.
2. **Identify the song's energy**: ballad, mid-tempo, anthem, club, etc.
3. **Find the chorus first.** Improvise vocal melodies over the chorus chord progression. Find the strongest 2-bar idea.
4. **Develop the chorus.** Extend the strongest idea into a full chorus melody. Make sure it has range, climax, and a clear hook.
5. **Write verse melody that contrasts.** Lower in tessitura, more conversational, builds toward the chorus.
6. **Write pre-chorus to bridge them.** Climbing, building, ending on V or a clear preparation.
7. **Write lyrics.** Start with the title hook; write chorus around it; write verses to set up the chorus.
8. **Polish prosody.** Sing through; fix anything awkward.
9. **Consider bridge.** Does the song need a bridge? If yes, contrast harmonically and melodically.
10. **Add ad-libs and harmonies.** Layer the demo vocal to show the producer the full vision.

## Quick decision matrix

| Want | Try |
|------|-----|
| Pop topline | Verse low-mid, pre-chorus mid-climbing, chorus belt, postchorus wordless hook |
| Modern pop with multiple hooks | Distinct verse melody, distinct chorus melody, distinct post-chorus melody — three memorable moments |
| Anthemic chorus | Sustained notes on open vowels, range up to top of singer's belt, repeated title hook |
| R&B vocal feature | Leave room for melisma; less metrically rigid; embellishment-friendly |
| Hip-hop sung hook | 8-bar chorus by R&B vocalist; melodic, simple, contrasts with rapped verses |
| EDM vocal hook | Short 2-bar phrase; repeated; lives in the breakdown; lyric is sparse or wordless |
| Country topline | Conversational verse, title-end-of-phrase chorus, minimal vocal processing |
| K-pop topline | Multiple sections with distinct hooks; range-spanning; layered harmonies |
| Indie / singer-songwriter | Conversational throughout; minimal commercial structure; lyrics-driven |
| Bridge that contrasts | New key area or modal mixture; new melodic shape; introduces a question or realization |
