# Hooks and Memorability

This file is about what makes a song stick in the listener's head — the hook, the earworm, the moment that gets remembered. Hooks are the single most commercially important element of a song, and the most studied. This file covers the anatomy of a hook, the techniques for crafting one, and the kinds of hooks that work in different genres.

For lyric craft generally, see `lyric-writing.md`. For the vocal melody as a whole, see `topline-craft.md`. For melodic construction at a technical level, see `melody/melodic-construction.md`. For motivic identity, see `melody/motivic-development.md`.

## When to consult this file

- The user is writing a song and asks "what makes a hook"
- A song is missing memorability — what to add
- Brainstorming hooks, vocal phrases, post-chorus elements
- Why some songs are infectious and others aren't
- The craft of repetition, contrast, and surprise

## What a hook is

A hook is a short, memorable musical-lyrical phrase that the listener wants to repeat (in their head or out loud). It's the part of the song that's still echoing after the song ends.

Hooks can be:

- **Lyrical**: the title phrase ("I Want It That Way", "Bad Guy")
- **Melodic**: a specific tune ("Smells Like Teen Spirit" intro riff, "Seven Nation Army" bass riff)
- **Rhythmic**: a distinctive groove or beat ("We Will Rock You" stomp-clap)
- **Sonic**: a specific sound or production element (the synth sound in "Take On Me")
- **Combination**: most great hooks combine multiple of the above

A song can have multiple hooks — verse hook, chorus hook, post-chorus hook, instrumental hook. Modern hits often have 3+.

## What makes a hook memorable

Several factors contribute. The greatest hooks usually have most of them:

### 1. Repetition

The hook repeats within the song — usually multiple times in the chorus, plus appearances in verse hooks or post-choruses. Listeners can't memorize what they don't hear repeatedly.

A song that says its title once is harder to remember than one that says it five times.

### 2. Distinctiveness

The hook should be different from other songs. Cliché melodies (the same I-V-vi-IV pop progression, or the same hook melody as countless other pop songs) blur into the background.

Distinctiveness can come from:

- An unusual interval (a leap in an unexpected place)
- An unusual rhythm (a syncopation that feels new)
- An unusual chord change at the hook moment
- A specific timbral choice (vocoder, falsetto, distinctive vocal tone)
- Lyrical wordplay or unexpected phrasing

### 3. Simplicity

The hook is short — usually 2–4 bars, 4–10 syllables. Listeners can hold it in working memory and replay it.

A "hook" that's 16 bars long isn't a hook; it's a chorus or a section. The hook is the kernel inside.

### 4. Singability

A great hook is something an average untrained singer can hum or sing along to. This means:

- Limited range (typically a 5th or less for the core hook)
- Stepwise motion or simple leaps (fourths, fifths, octaves)
- Open vowels for sustained notes
- Strong beats land on memorable syllables

A "hook" with a 2-octave range and chromatic inflections is hard for the average listener to internalize.

### 5. Emotional resonance

The hook says something the listener wants to say. It captures a feeling — joy, longing, defiance, sadness — that resonates personally.

This is partly about lyrics (the words mean something) and partly about melody (the contour creates the feeling regardless of language).

### 6. The "earworm" effect

Some hooks are literally earworms — they get stuck in the head involuntarily. Researchers have studied this; common features:

- Predictable enough to sing along, surprising enough to be interesting
- A "gap" or "incompleteness" that the brain wants to resolve
- Rhythmic distinctiveness
- Repeated exposure

## Anatomy of a melodic hook

A typical melodic hook can be analyzed in terms of:

### Range

Usually a 5th or 6th. Wider hooks are harder to sing. Examples:

- "Yesterday" (Beatles): the chorus hook spans a 6th.
- "Hey Jude": the "na na na na" outro is within a 5th.
- "Happy" (Pharrell): the chorus moves within a 5th.

### Contour

The shape matters as much as the pitches. Common hook contours:

- **Arch** (rises, peaks, falls): "Yesterday", "Don't Stop Believin'" chorus
- **Descending step** (falls in a melodic gesture): "Stairway to Heaven" intro
- **Repeated note**: many rap and rhythmic hooks center on a single pitch with rhythmic interest
- **Three-note motif**: "Star Wars" theme, "Imperial March", "We Are the Champions"

### Rhythm

Often the rhythm of the hook is more memorable than the pitches. The dotted-quarter-eighth rhythm of "Hey Jude" (na-Na-na-na) is more iconic than its specific pitches.

A hook with a strong rhythmic identity is easier to remember.

### Pitch over chord

The relationship between the hook's pitches and the underlying chord matters. Hooks that land on the chord's 3rd or 5th feel sweet; ones that land on the 7th or extensions feel modern; ones that land on chord roots feel grounded.

The "lift" of a chorus often comes from the hook melody hitting the 3rd, 5th, or 9th of the chord (rather than the root).

## Lyrical hooks

The lyric hook is usually:

- The title of the song
- A short phrase (2–6 words)
- Repeated multiple times in the chorus
- Often the first or last line of the chorus

What makes a lyrical hook strong:

- **Concrete language**: "We Will Rock You" is more vivid than "We Will Influence You".
- **Emotional declaration**: "Bad Guy", "Single Ladies", "I Will Always Love You" — strong statements.
- **Wordplay or surprise**: "Bohemian Rhapsody" — the title doesn't appear in the song; the hook is "Mama, just killed a man".
- **Sing-along quality**: the hook should be easy to shout. "Hey Ya" is easy; complex lyrical hooks aren't.

## Hooks across the song

Different sections call for different hook types:

### Verse hook

A short repeated phrase in the verse — often the verse melody's distinctive opening or a recurring lyric across verses. Smaller and more subtle than the chorus hook.

### Pre-chorus hook (often the "build hook")

A rising melodic figure in the pre-chorus that's distinctive and helps build energy.

### Chorus hook

The main hook. The thing the song is famous for.

### Post-chorus hook

A second hook after the chorus. Often instrumental or wordless ("oh-oh-oh"). Modern pop relies on this.

Examples:

- "Bad Romance" (Lady Gaga): "ra-ra-ah-ah-ah" post-chorus
- "Levitating" (Dua Lipa): the "yeah-yeah-yeah" hook
- Many K-pop songs have post-chorus drops with vocal-vocoder hooks

The post-chorus often becomes the most-remembered part of the song. Modern hit-makers consciously design post-choruses as separate hook events.

### Instrumental hook (the riff)

A memorable instrumental phrase. Common in rock, funk, hip-hop, EDM:

- "Smoke on the Water" — the iconic guitar riff
- "Seven Nation Army" — the bass riff
- "Sweet Child O' Mine" — the guitar intro
- "Crazy Train" — riff
- "Funkytown" — the synth riff

A great instrumental hook can carry a song without strong vocals.

## Genre conventions

### Pop

The chorus hook is paramount. Pop relies on title hooks repeated multiple times. Increasingly, post-choruses provide a second hook event.

### Country

Title hook usually at the end of the chorus phrase. Storytelling verses lead to a clear, declarative chorus statement.

### Hip-hop

Hooks are often shorter and more rhythmic than pop. Producer-side hooks (sample loops, beat patterns) are as important as vocal hooks. The chorus hook is often a chant or sung phrase by a featured artist.

### R&B

Vocal hooks rely heavily on melisma and ad-lib variations. The hook is sometimes more about a vocal performance than a specific melodic shape.

### Rock

Instrumental hooks (guitar/bass riffs) often more iconic than vocal hooks. The chorus hook is usually anthemic and declarative.

### EDM

The "drop" is essentially a hook — an instrumental peak moment. Vocal hooks are often short and repeated through the build and drop.

### K-pop

Multi-hook approach. Distinctive verse hook, pre-chorus build, chorus hook, post-chorus drop hook — sometimes all in one song. A K-pop song with 4+ hooks is common.

### Folk / singer-songwriter

Less reliance on commercial hooks. The "hook" might be a striking image or a phrase that resonates emotionally. Songs that don't have radio-style hooks but still have memorable moments.

### Musical theatre

The "11 o'clock number" often has the show's most powerful hook moment. But theatrical hooks serve dramatic function rather than commercial radio play.

## Techniques for crafting hooks

### Start with the title

Many great songs start with a title or hook phrase. Then the writer builds melody, verses, and structure around that initial spark.

### Sing-then-write

Some songwriters develop hooks by humming melodies into a recorder, then transcribing the best ones. The hook comes from improvisation rather than analysis.

### Chord-forward

Other writers start with chord progressions, then find melodies that work over them. The chord progression suggests certain pitches as natural landing points.

### Lyric-forward

Some start with a phrase or feeling and build everything else around it. "Bohemian Rhapsody" likely started with the lyrical concept.

### Stealing structures, replacing content

Many hooks borrow structural patterns from existing hooks (e.g., "title phrase + descending pitch" is a common pattern). Listening to many hooks and analyzing their construction helps the writer build a vocabulary.

### Test, then refine

Sing the hook out loud; record it; listen back the next day. If it's still in your head, it's working. If not, revise.

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't over-engineer hooks.** Some great hooks are surprisingly simple. "We Will Rock You" is two notes. The hook doesn't need to be melodically complex.
- **Don't ignore rhythm.** A hook's rhythm is often more memorable than its pitches. Get the rhythm right; the pitches can vary slightly.
- **Don't make hooks too long.** A 16-bar "hook" isn't a hook. Trim it to 2–4 bars of pure essence.
- **Don't bury the hook.** It should appear early in the song (within the first minute) and recur often. A song that doesn't deliver its hook until 2:30 will lose listeners.
- **Don't ignore vocal range.** A hook that's not singable by the average listener won't get hummed. Keep range tight.
- **Don't assume distinctive = good.** Distinctive can also mean weird-in-a-bad-way. The hook should be distinctive AND immediately appealing.
- **Don't forget the chorus payoff.** A weak chorus melodically dooms the song no matter how strong the verses.

## A workflow for hook crafting

If the user wants to develop a hook:

1. **Start with the central feeling or message.** What's the song about? What's the emotional core?
2. **Write 3–5 candidate hook lines.** Short, evocative, sing-able.
3. **Test each by singing.** Which has the strongest melodic identity? Which makes you want to repeat it?
4. **Add melody.** The lyric and melody should fit together — strong syllables on strong beats, vowels that ring.
5. **Test the hook in context.** Sing the chorus over the chord progression. Does the hook land?
6. **Listen to it the next day.** Is it still memorable, or has it faded?
7. **Refine.** Adjust pitch, rhythm, or lyric for greater impact.
8. **Add a contrasting verse.** The hook should feel like a payoff after the verse.

## Quick decision matrix

| Want | Try |
|------|-----|
| Pop chorus hook | Title phrase, 4–8 syllables, repeated, on an arching melodic shape |
| Earworm hook | Short rhythmic figure with one unusual interval; repeats often |
| Anthem hook | "We" or "you" lyric, descending strong-beat line, sing-along |
| Modern post-chorus hook | Wordless or minimal-word vocal phrase ("oh-oh-oh") with synth lead |
| Instrumental riff hook | 2–4 bar guitar/bass/synth motif, distinctive rhythm, repeats throughout song |
| Hip-hop hook | Sung chant by featured vocalist, short phrase, melodic over the beat |
| Country hook | Title at end of chorus phrase, narrative buildup, conversational delivery |
| K-pop multi-hook | Verse hook + chorus hook + post-chorus drop hook; multiple memorable moments |
| Wordplay hook | Surprising rhyme or word combination at the hook moment |
| Visual / tactile hook | Specific imagery in the hook lyric ("rolling in the deep", "blurred lines") |
