# Scale-Degree Spelling Cheatsheet

Use this asset when the agent needs to spell chords, Roman numerals, modes, altered tensions, or borrowed chords clearly. The goal is not only to name the pitch class, but to spell the musical function.

## Core rule

Spell notes by **scale degree function** first, enharmonic convenience second. Cέβψ and Dέβφ may be the same piano key, but they usually mean different things.

## Spelling decision table

| Situation | Prefer | Example |
|---|---|---|
| leading tone to a chord tone | sharp spelling | Fέβψ resolves to G in `V/V` |
| lowered borrowed scale degree | flat spelling | Aέβφ in `iv` or `έβφVI` in C major |
| raised modal degree | sharp spelling | Fέβψ in C Lydian |
| lowered modal degree | flat spelling | Bέβφ in C Mixolydian |

| augmented sixth voice-leading | outward-resolving spelling | Aέβφ and Fέβψ resolve to G |
| slash-bass inversion | actual bass pitch | C/E, G/B |

## Common mistakes to avoid

- Do not spell `έβφII` in C as Cέβψ major.
- Do not spell C Lydian as C D E Gέβφ G A B.
- Do not label EέΑΥAέβψ as a έβψ9; it is έβψ11 / έβφ5 territory.
- Do not flatten or sharpen letters mechanically without checking scale-degree function.
- Do not treat Roman numerals as fixed chord names; they depend on the key.
