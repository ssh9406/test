# Cadence Reference

Quick lookup for cadence types. A cadence is how a phrase ends — the harmonic punctuation that signals "concluded", "paused", "surprised", or "continuing".

For *why* cadences work, see `references/harmony/functional-harmony.md`. For chromatic and modulation-driven cadences, see `references/harmony/chromatic-harmony.md` and `references/harmony/modulation.md`.

## Authentic cadences (V → I)

The default conclusive cadence. The dominant resolves to tonic.

| Cadence | Definition | Strength | Feeling |
|---------|------------|----------|---------|
| **Perfect Authentic Cadence (PAC)** | `V → I` (or `V7 → I`); both chords in root position; ^1 in the soprano of `I` | Maximum closure | Period at end of sentence |
| **Imperfect Authentic Cadence (IAC), root position** | `V → I` in root position, but ^3 or ^5 in the soprano of `I` | Strong closure, lighter | Comma at end of clause |
| **IAC, inverted** | `V → I` with one or both chords inverted | Mild closure | Soft pause |
| **Contrapuntal cadence (vii° → I, or V⁶ → I)** | Same dominant function, but with bass leading-tone motion | Mild closure | Smoother, less terminal |

**Use cases**: PAC at the end of a piece, end of a major formal section, end of an antecedent–consequent period (consequent ends PAC). IAC at the end of an antecedent (sets up further motion), internal phrase endings.

## Half cadence (HC)

Phrase ends on `V` (sometimes `V7`, but more often the bare triad).

| Variant | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| **Half cadence** | Phrase ends on `V`. Sounds like a question. |
| **Phrygian half cadence** (minor key) | `iv⁶ → V`; bass moves ♭^6 → ^5 by half step (diatonic in natural minor). Bittersweet, archaic; common in Baroque slow movements and Renaissance music. |

**Use cases**: end of an antecedent in a period; mid-piece pause; the "comma" before a chorus or a returning theme.

## Plagal cadence (IV → I)

Sometimes called the "amen cadence" because of its association with church endings.

| Variant | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| **Plagal cadence** | `IV → I`. Gentle, rocking. Doesn't have the leading-tone gravity of authentic cadences. |
| **Minor plagal** (in major) | `iv → I`. Borrowed iv. Wistful, "smiling-through-tears" effect. Common in The Beatles, modern pop, R&B. |
| **Double plagal** | `♭VII → IV → I`. Common in rock — the end of "Hey Jude". |

Plagal cadences are weaker conclusions than authentic. Often used as appendages after a PAC ("amen" tag) or as primary cadences in genres that want softness (gospel, rock ballad, lullaby, hymn).

## Deceptive cadence (V → vi, etc.)

The dominant resolves to something other than tonic — usually `vi` (in major) or `♭VI` (in minor or with modal mixture).

| Variant | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| **Standard deceptive** | `V → vi` in major. Bittersweet substitution; ^1 expected, ^6 delivered. |
| **Deceptive to ♭VI in major** | `V → ♭VI`. Modal mixture flavor — darker, more dramatic than `V → vi`. |
| **Deceptive in minor** | `V → ♭VI`. Diatonic in natural/harmonic minor; classical ear hears it as fully deceptive because the major-mode resolution to `i` was expected. |
| **Deceptive to IV** | `V → IV`. Rare in classical, used in pop. The dominant feels "redirected" plagally. |

**Use cases**: delay closure for dramatic effect; extend a phrase ("just one more time"); set up a new section by avoiding tonic; end a verse to push into a chorus.

## Modal and special cadences

Cadences that work outside (or alongside) functional harmony.

| Cadence | Definition | Where it's used |
|---------|------------|-----------------|
| **Phrygian cadence (modal, not classical Phrygian half)** | `♭II → I` (♭II as Neapolitan, or the modal supertonic in Phrygian); bass moves ♭^2 → ^1 | flamenco-adjacent writing, Spanish folk/dance-influenced contexts, certain film-scoring shorthand |
| **Lydian cadence** | `II → I` (II is a major chord on ^2, characteristic of Lydian) | Lydian-mode pieces; lifted/floating feel |
| **Mixolydian cadence** | `♭VII → I` (no leading tone) | Rock, Celtic music, some modal jazz |
| **Aeolian cadence** | `♭VII → i` or `iv → i` (no leading tone) | Minor-key rock, folk, modal pieces |
| **Picardy third (tierce de Picardie)** | A minor-key piece ends with a major tonic chord (i.e., `I` instead of `i`) | Baroque convention; modern uses for unexpected resolution |
| **Dorian cadence** | `IV → i` (in Dorian, IV is major) | Modal jazz, Celtic, prog rock |

## Jazz cadences

Functional cadences with jazz extensions and substitutions. See `references/harmony/jazz-harmony.md` for full treatment.

| Cadence | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| **ii–V–I** | The basic jazz cadence. Smoother and more colorful than V–I alone. Almost always with 7ths: `Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7`. |
| **ii–V–I with tritone sub** | Replace `V7` with `♭II7` (e.g., `Dm7 – D♭7 – Cmaj7`). The tritone of `D♭7` is the same as `G7`. Smooth chromatic bass. |
| **Backdoor ii–V** | `iv–♭VII7–I` (e.g., `Fm7 – B♭7 – Cmaj7`). Approaches tonic via the parallel minor's ii–V. |
| **Cycle cadence** | `iii–vi–ii–V–I`. Extends the cadential approach. |
| **Minor ii–V–i** | `iiø7 – V7♭9 – i`. The minor-key analogue. |

## Cadence strength roughly ranked

From most conclusive to least:

1. **PAC** with strong rhythmic placement — total closure
2. **IAC**, root position, ^3 or ^5 on top — closure but lighter
3. **IAC**, inverted — soft closure
4. **Plagal** — gentle closure
5. **Phrygian half cadence (minor key)** — strong pause
6. **Half cadence** — open question
7. **Deceptive cadence** — surprise; phrase wants to continue
8. **No cadence (elision)** — the phrase ends on a non-cadential chord; motion continues uninterrupted

This ordering is contextual — a deceptive cadence inside a piece can feel more conclusive than a half cadence at the very end. Always check what the cadence does *in context*.

## Quick decision matrix

| Need to... | Use |
|------------|-----|
| End a piece firmly | PAC |
| End a section | PAC or IAC |
| Set up another section (open question) | HC |
| End softly without strong resolution | Plagal, IAC inverted |
| Surprise the listener / delay closure | Deceptive |
| Sound modal / non-classical | Mixolydian, Aeolian, Phrygian, Dorian (depending on color) |
| End a minor piece on an unexpected major | Picardy third |
| End a jazz tune | ii–V–I (with extensions) or ii–V–I with tritone sub |
| Sound flamenco-/Phrygian-adjacent | Andalusian cadence (`i – ♭VII – ♭VI – V`) — technically a progression, but functions as a cadential pattern |
| Avoid landing — keep motion alive | Deceptive, or elision into next phrase, or end on inversion |

## Notes on combining cadences across a piece

A typical 8-bar period:

- Antecedent (bars 1–4): ends HC or IAC
- Consequent (bars 5–8): ends PAC

A 16-bar AABA bridge to chorus often uses a deceptive cadence at the end of the bridge to launch into the chorus, since landing on `I` would feel premature.

Pop songs often avoid PAC at section ends to keep motion forward. A song that PACs at the end of every chorus feels overly resolved; one that uses HC, deceptive, or no cadence at all between sections feels propulsive.

A film cue that resolves PAC at the climax can feel conclusive — sometimes too conclusive for a story still in motion. Many composers prefer plagal, modal, or unresolved endings to leave space for what comes next on screen.
