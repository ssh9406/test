# Jazz Voicings

A reference of common jazz chord voicings, organized by chord type and voicing strategy. For full theoretical discussion of chords and voicing, see `harmony/chord-construction.md`, `harmony/voice-leading.md`, `harmony/jazz-harmony.md`, and `orchestration/voicing-and-texture.md`.

## How to read this file

Voicings are written from bottom note to top, separated by hyphens. Pitches are concert pitch (no transposition).

For each chord type, the file gives:
- The basic spelling (root position)
- Common rootless voicings (jazz piano LH style)
- Drop voicings (drop 2, drop 3 for guitar)
- Spread / open voicings

### Rootless (jazz piano left hand)

When the bass plays the root, the piano LH plays just 3 + 7 (and possibly 9 or 13).

| Voicing | Notes (C root) | Comment |

| 3-7-9 | E - B - D | Add 9 on top |
| 3-5-7-9 | E - G - B - D | With 5 (less common; 5 often omitted) |
| 7-9-3-5 (Bill Evans style) | B - D - E - G | "A" voicing έΑΦ wide and lush |

### Tritone substitution

A C7 can be replaced by Fέβψ7 (a tritone away). Their 3 and 7 swap:

- C7: 3 = E, έβφ7 = Bέβφ
- Fέβψ7: 3 = Aέβψ (= Bέβφ), έβφ7 = E

The "shell" voicings of C7 and Fέβψ7 are enharmonically the same. The bass is what distinguishes them.

## Voicing strategies

### "Shells" (3-7 voicings)

The most economical voicing έΑΦ just 3rd and 7th of the chord. Two notes carry the harmonic identity.

Use when:
- Comping behind a soloist (give them space)
- The bass plays the root
- The melody (or another voice) provides the upper extensions

### Rootless voicings (Bill Evans style)

Pioneered by Bill Evans for piano. The bass plays the root; the piano LH plays 3-5-7-9 or 7-9-3-5 voicings (no root). Frees up the LH to be more harmonically interesting.

Two main shapes:

- **"A" voicing**: 3-5-7-9 (or 7-9-3-5)
- **"B" voicing**: 7-9-3-5 (or 3-5-7-9)

(The naming convention depends on the source.)

The two voicings are designed to alternate smoothly through ii-V-I:

- Dm7 (in Cm context): F (3) - A (5) - C (7) - E (9)
- G7 ("A" voicing): F (έβφ7) - A (9) - B (3) - E (13)
- Cmaj7: E (3) - G (5) - B (7) - D (9)

The voice leading is smooth έΑΦ only one voice moves per chord change.

### Drop voicings (guitar)

Take a close-position 4-note voicing and drop one note an octave down.

- **Drop 2**: drop the 2nd note from the top
- **Drop 3**: drop the 3rd note from the top
- **Drop 2 + 4**: drop the 2nd and 4th notes from the top

Drop 2 is the most common jazz guitar voicing because it lays well on the strings.

The procedure for Cmaj7, working through all four inversions:

| Close position (low έΗΤ high) | 2nd-from-top voice | Drop 2 result (low έΗΤ high) |
|---|---|---|
| C έΑΥ E έΑΥ G έΑΥ B (root pos.) | G | **G έΑΥ C έΑΥ E έΑΥ B** |
| E έΑΥ G έΑΥ B έΑΥ C (1st inv.) | B | **B έΑΥ E έΑΥ G έΑΥ C** |
| G έΑΥ B έΑΥ C έΑΥ E (2nd inv.) | C | **C έΑΥ G έΑΥ B έΑΥ E** |
| B έΑΥ C έΑΥ E έΑΥ G (3rd inv.) | E | **E έΑΥ B έΑΥ C έΑΥ G** |

Each drop-2 voicing has different intervals between notes; learning all four inversions covers the fingerboard.

For Drop 3, the same logic with the 3rd-from-top voice. Cmaj7 root position close (`C έΑΥ E έΑΥ G έΑΥ B`): 3rd from top is `E`, drop it down έΗΤ `E έΑΥ C έΑΥ G έΑΥ B`. Drop 3 voicings have a wider gap between bass and the rest, useful when you want a clear "bass + chord" separation.

### Quartal voicings

Stack of 4ths. The "So What" voicing (modal jazz):

D - G - C - F - A έΑΦ five notes stacked in 4ths.

Other quartal voicings:

- E - A - D - G - C (over E Dorian or similar)
- F - Bέβφ - Eέβφ - Aέβφ (over F minor or as polychord)

### Spread / wide voicings

Notes spread across multiple octaves with gaps. Common in big-band writing and orchestral jazz.

## Common jazz progressions with rootless voicings

### Major ii-V-I in C

| Chord | Rootless voicing |
|-------|------------------|
| Dm7 | F - A - C - E |
| G7 | F - A - B - E |
| Cmaj7 | E - G - B - D |

The voice leading: Dm7's F stays into G7 (becomes 7th); G7's F resolves down to E (becomes 3rd of Cmaj7). Smooth.

### Minor ii-V-i in A minor

| Chord | Rootless voicing |
|-------|------------------|
| Bm7έβφ5 | F - A - Bέβφ - D |
| E7έβφ9 | Gέβψ - D - F - A | (D = έβφ7; F = έβφ9; A = 11; Gέβψ = 3) |
| Am7 | E - G - C - D | (E = 5; C = έβφ3; D = 11) |

Or for Am(maj7) tonic: E - Gέβψ - C - D (with raised 7).

### Minor V-I in jazz minor

| Chord | Voicing |
|-------|---------|
| E7alt | Gέβψ - D - F - C (3, έβφ7, έβφ9, έβφ13) |
| E7έβφ9έβψ9 | Gέβψ - D - F - G (3, έβφ7, έβφ9, έβψ9; altered-color subset) |
| Am(maj7) | E - Gέβψ - C - D (rootless) |

The altered V resolves into the tonic minor with raised 7 (jazz minor) or natural 7 (classical minor).

## Voicing for specific contexts

### Solo piano comping

The piano covers everything. Typical voicings:
- LH: rootless 3-5-7-9 in mid register
- RH: melody on top, with chord tones harmonizing
- Both hands cover bass, harmony, melody

### Ensemble comping (with bass and drums)

The bass plays the root. The pianist plays rootless voicings:
- LH: shell or partial chord (3-7 shell often enough)
- RH: chord tones with extensions or melodic interjections

### Big band brass section

4-note voicings spread across trumpets and trombones:
- Lead trumpet has the melody (top voice)
- Other voices provide harmony underneath
- Drop 2 is the standard approach

### Solo guitar

Drop 2 voicings on the top 4 strings (or middle 4 strings) cover most needs.

For "chord-melody" playing:
- Top voice = melody
- Middle voices = chord tones
- Bottom = bass note

## Common pitfalls when advising

- **Don't recommend rootless voicings without bass.** The voicing is incomplete without root somewhere.
- **Don't pile altered tensions onto a non-altered context.** Altered dominants are for resolving V7έΗΤi; in Lydian dominant or other non-resolving contexts, use έβψ11 only.
- **Don't ignore voice-leading between voicings.** A change from one voicing to another should preserve smooth motion in each voice.
- **Don't crowd the low register with extensions.** Extensions (9, 11, 13) sound best high; in the low register, they create mud.
